These 61 fintechs are primed to take a big step in 2023, according to top investors

By Julie Bort , Carter Johnson , Bianca Chan , Asia Martin , and Emilia David
2023-01-10T15:41:13Z
Facebook Email X LinkedIn Copy link Impact Link
Save Saved Read in app

  • Insider asked more than 40 top fintech investors to nominate the most promising fintechs.
  • Investors highlighted startups that haven't raised more than a Series B. 
  • Here are the 61 most promising fintechs.

Even as funding in fintech fell to one of its lowest levels since the pandemic started, investors still see bright spots in the sector. 

Insider surveyed 43 investors — including those from Bain Capital Ventures, Lightspeed Venture Partners, and QED Investors — about the most promising fintechs to watch. Startups that were nominated included a mix of investors' portfolio companies and ones in which they have no financial interest. In total, 61 startups that haven't raised beyond a Series B round were identified. 

As was the case last year, the majority of those nominated work with other businesses, not individual consumers. Many investors noted B2B startups are one of the biggest bright spots in the sector as businesses and financial institutions continue to streamline their processes and cut costs where they can.  

Fintech has been hit hard by the economic downturn. Global fintech funding dropped to $20.4 billion in the second quarter, falling 46% from last year, according to data from CB Insights. This represents the lowest level of funding for fintech since the last quarter of 2020, as venture investors pulled back amid the looming recession and public-market volatility. 

Among the B2B fintechs in the list, infrastructure and payments were popular focus areas, with many companies also looking at making it easier to access digital assets. Investors also nominated financial-advisory apps, lending services, and digital banks on the more consumer-facing side of fintech.

Check out the 61 fintechs identified as most promising by top investors. 

Albert

Cited by: Portage (investor)

Total funding: $171.6 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Albert pairs financial advice with financial products so consumers can find the right service for them. 

Why it's on the list: "No one else in the market can actually connect you to a real human to answer your financial questions. This kind of service has traditionally been reserved for high-net-worth individuals," said Stephanie Choo, a partner at Portage.

Arch

Cited by: Jump Capital

Total funding: $5.5 million

What it does: Arch Labs is a digital administrator for private-market investments, providing services like tax automation and tech supporting capital-call requests and distributions. 

Why it's promising: "The last decade has ushered in a democratization of alternative investments and grown appetites for direct and fund investment. Managing that growing complexity is an immense challenge. Arch empowers financial advisors and investors to invest with confidence with information at their fingertips," said Yelena Shkolnik, a partner at Jump Capital. 

Ascend

Cited by: Redpoint and Nyca Partners

Total funding: $39 million

What it does: Ascend offers insurance companies a platform to automate customer payments. 

Why it's promising: "Insurance is a large and complex sector with large funds flowing to many parties. Ascend looks to streamline and improve the payments process from customer payment through allocating and accounting for commissions and other complexities," Medha Agarwal, a partner at Redpoint, said.

"After selling their last startup to Hippo, Andrew Wynn and Praveen Chekuri teamed up to found another business in the insuretech space.  They are tackling the problem of payments. Most insurance premiums are paid semi-annually or annually. Ascend makes it possible for customers, consumers, and businesses to pay for insurance on a monthly basis rather than in a lump sum. Nyca managed to squeeze onto the cap table of their last business, but we missed this one. I wish we hadn't," Tom Brown, a partner and general counsel at Nyca Partners, said.

Atomic

Cited by: Two Sigma Ventures

Total funding: $25 million

What it does: Atomic offers drop-in APIs that consumer-focused fintechs can use to offer investing and wealth-management products to their customers. 

Why it's promising: "In addition to providing all the functionality of trading APIs and investing-as-a-service infrastructure, Atomic has productized various wealth-management services (i.e., tax-loss harvesting, ESG, etc.) for consumers as well as treasury-management products for businesses," Frances Schwiep, a partner at Two Sigma Ventures, said.

Basis Theory

Cited by: Costanoa Ventures

Total funding: $17 million

What it does: Basis Theory provides a way for companies to tokenize and better protect customers' financial data. 

Why it's promising: "So many companies (in particular large B2C merchants) struggle to manage customer-payment information in a compliant manner and end up outsourcing the management of this data to their payment-service providers. Basis Theory lets their customers take back control of their data without compromising their security or taxing engineering resources," Amy Cheetham, a partner at Costanoa Ventures, said. 

Bilt Rewards

Cited by: CapitalG

Total funding: $63.3 million 

What it does: Bilt Rewards offers a loyalty-rewards program and credit card that allow renters to earn points that can be redeemed at airline companies, hotels, fitness classes, and other places. 

Why it's promising: "Renters win because they get point-based credit-card rewards for this huge area of spend. Property owners win because they receive timely and free payments via Bilt's integrations with property-management systems," said Jesse Wedler, a general partner at CapitalG.

"As the largest monthly expense, rental payments form a foundational piece of the financial lives for most consumers. Driving rewards for these payments, helping consumers build credit for timely rental payments, and then assisting in navigating a consumer's financial journey (buying a house, buying a car, and consolidating debt) is a huge opportunity, and Bilt has the smartest approach we've seen for addressing it," Wedler added.

Blue Onion

Cited by: Battery Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $8 million

What it does: Blue Onion provides accounting services such as reconciliation for e-commerce merchants. 

Why it's on the list: "With the rise of e-commerce, these merchants need a new set of tools to help run their business across multiple channels, particularly with automating their back-office systems and workflow," said Michael Brown, a partner at Battery Ventures.

Boom & Bucket

Cited by: Avid Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $5.5 million

What it does: Boom & Bucket is an online marketplace for used construction equipment, providing data like ownership history and maintenance records as well as financing options in one place. 

Why it's promising: "Boom & Bucket is turning the archaic, opaque process of buying and selling used construction equipment (a $300 billion market) into a reliable, efficient, and fully online transaction by leveraging data, APIs, and telematics. While moving largely offline transactions online alone creates a significantly better user experience, Boom & Bucket has been innovative in its ability to bundle additional services to the purchase," Addie Lerner, the founder and managing partner at Avid Ventures, said.  

"Boom & Bucket has realized strong attachment of initial financial and insurance products with a road map for further warranty, guarantee, and logistics ancillary products to continue to improve margins and core unit economics," Lerner said.

Brigit

Cited by: Bling Capital

Total funding: $125 million (equity and debt)

What it does: Brigit helps prevent bank-account overdrafts through its prediction engine and automated lender. Since launching its core instant-cash product and utilizing its cash-flow-underwriting platform, it has launched other services such as budgeting, job recommendations, credit monitoring, credit building, and identity-theft protection all under its monthly subscription rate of $9.99.

Why it's promising: Brigit is a market leader serving 100 million Americans living paycheck-to-paycheck and paying an aggregate $43 billion in overdraft and predatory lender fees, said Ben Ling, the founder and general partner at Bling Capital. The company issued over 10 million advances, totaling $1 billion and saving users $450 million in predatory fees, he added.

Butter

Cited by: Atomic (investor)

Total funding: $6.8 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Butter works with subscription-based companies to reduce payment churn and maintain legitimate, regular payments with customers. 

Why it's promising: "Butter aspires to become the AWS for payments. Butter's payment-intelligence platform starts by helping subscription (consumer and SaaS) companies solve involuntary churn, payment failures which account for $440 billion in lost revenue. On average, Butter boosts subscription revenue by 5%, generating millions for its customers, which is of course very welcome in this market environment," said Chester Ng, a general partner at Atomic.

CapIntel

Cited by: FinTech Collective (investor)

Total funding: $16.1 million CAD

What it does: CapIntel offers financial advisors a variety of tools, including seamless portfolio creation and comparison, fund discovery, performance analysis, and portfolio modeling.

Why it's promising: "Advisors continue to manage 25 times more assets than robo and self-directed platforms combined, but clients are demanding increasingly more value for lower cost. CapIntel is the first mover in the enterprise wealth-management space to create a solution that bolsters advisors' ability to sell while also broadening their value proposition," said Sarah Parsons Wolter, a principal at FinTech Collective.

Caplight

Cited by: DRW Venture Capital

Total funding: $7 million

What it does: Caplight is a marketplace where institutional investors can take positions in private companies through derivatives. 

Why it's promising: "This year, we have seen an increase in secondary volume, which is exciting for the space. As liquidity increases so does the need for transparency, and Caplight has introduced unique price-discovery tools and derivative products to innovate methods for managing risk," said Kimberly Trautmann, the head of DRW Venture Capital.

Cardless

Cited by: Activant Capital (investor)

Total funding: $60 million

What it does: This San Francisco-based startup offers digital credit cards tied to brands like the Boston Celtics and Manchester United that offer unique rewards. 

Why it's promising: "Modern brands can finally launch a digital credit-card program that aligns with their user experience in weeks, not years. Cardless have already launched a major airline (with 100 times fewer employees than the incumbent) and recently became the first fintech that can issue on the American Express network, further enhancing their premium offering," said Andrew Steele, a partner at Activant Capital.

Carputty

Cited by: Panoramic Ventures 

Total funding: $9.65 million, according to PitchBook

What it does: Carputty gives credit lines to car consumers that go to the user instead of the car.

Why it's on the list: "Carputty provides an auto credit line that stays with the consumer rather than the car. It has the potential to radically change the rates paid for used-car loans (due to the credit of the consumer rather than the quality of the car)," Dan Drechsel, a partner at Panoramic Ventures, said.

Chaos Labs

Cited by: Bessemer 

Total funding: Not available

What it does: Chaos Labs is developing a cloud-based platform that secures blockchains and smart contracts. 

Why it's promising: "Chaos Labs is the market-leading smart-contract security and simulations platform. Decentralized finance protocols are highly susceptible to hacks/exploits, given the immutable and composable nature of these protocols and the clear financial incentives for bad actors," said Charles Birnbaum, a partner at Bessemer Venture Partners. 

CNote

Cited by: Commerce Ventures

Total funding: $16.25 million

What it does: CNote offers a data- and treasury-solutions service that helps corporations meet their environmental, social, and corporate-governance goals. 

Why it's promising: "CNote's breakthrough is providing the rich ESG data and associated treasury solutions that enable Fortune 500s and other institutions to achieve their ESG goals by more intelligently placing their cash deposits with community-focused FIs, as well as earning fixed-income yields from mission-aligned borrowers. Everyone wins with their approach," said Dan Rosen, a founder and general partner at Commerce Ventures. 

Coefficient

Cited by: Sapphire Ventures 

Total funding: $6.7 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Coefficient connects spreadsheets to share and update data to different programs so datasets can be more comprehensive. 

Why it's promising: "Coefficient offers non-technical business users a simple, one-click-at-a-time interface for creating spreadsheets that can ingest and utilize live data across the company. The tool not only helps eliminate human error from data entry, it solves the painfully manual and repetitive copy/paste process of report building that takes weekends away from data-focused roles. Best of all, the data analysis dashboards are streamlined, giving teams across the business user-friendly insights to dig into the data they need.," said Rajeev Dham, a partner at Sapphire Ventures.

CoinLedger

Cited by: DRW Venture Capital (investor)

Total funding: $6 million

What it does: CoinLedger provides tax-reporting and portfolio-management tools for crypto investors. 

Why it's promising: "CoinLedger is constantly adapting their services to support crypto investors by creating new, efficient ways to manage and track their crypto transactions and deliver tax reporting across a broad selection of crypto assets. As the crypto ecosystem increases in complexity, the robust transaction-tracking and tax-reporting software that CoinLedger provides is necessary in this space," said Kimberly Trautmann, the head of DRW Venture Capital.

Coinme

Cited by: Pantera Capital (investor)

Total funding: $34.5 million

What it does: Coinme allows users to buy and sell cryptocurrencies with cash. The startup also has an API for legacy financial systems to provide a way to buy, sell, store, and manage digital currencies.

Why it's promising: "Coinme is the top company providing cash to crypto on-ramp and cross-border payments. Coinme has strong partnerships with Coinstar and Moneygram for go-to-market and distribution," said Paul Veradittakit, a partner at Pantera Capital.

Column Tax

Cited by: Bain Capital Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $27 million

What it does: Column Tax enables fintechs, financial institutions, and other developers to integrate tax calculation and filing directly into their product.

Why it's promising: "Tax is an essential component of any individual's finances, yet it's only starting to get integrated with the rest of our financial lives, leading to unclaimed refunds, inaccurate returns, and surprises in April. Column Tax has partnered with leading fintechs to make every American's experiences with tax more effortless, affordable, and continuous," said Kevin Zhang, a partner at Bain Capital Ventures.

Cortina

Cited by: Point72 Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $6 million

What it does: Cortina's commerce platform allows brands to sell third-party products on their own site, keeping their own brand experience while offering complementary products for their customers.

Why it's promising: "Merchants of all types are choosing to build out third-party marketplaces, which is exactly where Cortina comes in. The company helps e-commerce providers meet their goals by eliminating the technological and logistical complexity of selling multiple brand products on one storefront and handling all payments on the backend. By providing consumers with a wider set of options in their shopping experience, merchants are better able to attract, convert, and retain customers," said Tripp Shriner, a partner at Point72 Ventures.

Flock Homes

Cited by: Inspired Capital

Total funding: $26 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Flock Homes enables landlords with rental units to exchange their properties for shares in a broader portfolio of real-estate investments. 

Why it's promising: "They are enabling homeowners, specifically landlords who own a few properties, to access a diversified portfolio of residential real estate that's been historically limited to large institutions. I'm always interested in companies that are unlocking alternate-investment opportunities and providing liquidity in innovative ways," said Mark Batsiyan, a cofounder, partner, and the COO of Inspired Capital.

Forage

Cited by: Nyca Partners (investor)

Total funding: $25 million

What it does: Forage is a third-party payment processor for electronic-benefit-transfer transactions, allowing those who receive EBTs to shop for groceries online. 

Why it's promising: "This is a special team tackling an important and difficult problem: enabling firms that sell groceries to accept EBT for mobile and online purchases. And the person leading the company built this product for Instacart. If anyone can solve this problem, they can," said Tom Brown, a partner and general counsel at Nyca Partners. 

Found

Cited by: Lightspeed Venture Partners (investor)

Total funding: $75 million

What it does: Found provides digital-banking services like income and expense tracking, invoicing, and tax help for self-employed people. 

Why it's promising: "Found is building a modern financial platform for one of the largest, most underserved segments of the US economy — self-employed workers," said Alex Taussig, a partner at Lightspeed Venture Partners.

"Found will exclusively serve this segment with a product suite that merges and automates three critical finance functions: (1) banking, (2) bookkeeping, and (3) tax accounting," Taussig said. "Found was created by two early employees of Block/Square, who built some of the company's most important products from the ground up. They have intimate knowledge of the payments stack, a penchant for viral growth, and a passion for solving problems for SMBs."

Fund That Flip

Cited by: Edison Partners (investor)

Total funding: $33 million 

What it does: Fund That Flip connects residential real-estate developers to sources of funding on its online-investment platform. 

Why it's promising: "Fund That Flip is more than just democratizing real-estate investing and more than empowering individuals and SMBs to grow their businesses and communities through access to capital, even if both are important. This company's on its way to build a full real-estate-investment operating platform and a complete flywheel, while always putting their relationships, mission, and humans first. It also doesn't hurt at all that this company continues to grow at an extraordinary rate while being profitable," said Jennifer Lee, a partner at Edison Partners.

GloveBox

Cited by: Panoramic Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $11.5 million, according to PitchBook

What it does: GloveBox makes it easier for insurance providers to offer better customer experience. 

Why it's on the list: "GloveBox is an insurtech that puts an independent insurance agency's brand at the forefront of great customer experience," said Dan Drechsel, a partner at Panoramic Ventures.

Highline

Cited by: Costanoa Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $17.5 million

What it does: Highline is a payments service through which consumers can automate bill payments directly out of their direct-deposit paychecks. 

Why it's promising: "Their payments platform allows consumers to easily automate bill payments directly from their paycheck. This enables consumer lenders to improve their portfolio performance while allowing them to expand approvals and reach more potential customers," said Amy Cheetham, a partner at Costanoa Ventures.  

"Highline targets consumers who need access to credit and who right now get charged exorbitant rates and don't get credit for their hard work and consistent paychecks. The company has the opportunity to change the way consumer credit works for the better, specifically for the roughly 40 million Americans who don't have access to mainstream credit options," Cheetham added.

Hummingbird

Cited by: Accel

Total funding: $41.8 million

What it does: Hummingbird is a customer-relationship-management and workflow tool for compliance and risk-management teams.

Why it's promising: "They allow you to integrate with your existing KYC (Socure, etc.) data and activity monitoring systems to have customer data, case management, investigations, and regulatory reporting all in one place. We've seen our portfolio adopt them as customers as well as partner with them very closely — a really interesting company," said Ben Fletcher, a partner at Accel.

Inscribe

Cited by: Commerce Ventures

Total funding: $13.7 million

What it does: Inscribe is an automated fraud-detection and underwriting service that financial firms can use to review documents like proof of business ownership, income, and assets. 

Why it's promising: "Too often a financial institution or fintech must task a person with reviewing documents or a complex data pattern or be faced with risk of loss. The result is added processing expense, latency (which leads to bad experiences for good customers), and often costly fraud losses that result from inevitable human error or oversight. Inscribe eliminates the need for this human intervention in many of the most challenging of underwriting scenarios," said Dan Rosen, the founder and general partner at Commerce Ventures.

Interchecks

Cited by: Commerce Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $42.5 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Interchecks works with businesses so they can offer a variety of payment options (from check to ACH to prepaid card) to their customers.  

Why it's promising: "While enabling businesses to accept payments has progressively gotten easier, most businesses still struggle with the challenge of disbursing funds quickly, safely, and across a diverse set of payment channels. Interchecks makes it easy to solve this challenge with one integration to their comprehensive payouts platform, while enabling a user experience for the recipient that is as delightful as the disbursing company requires," said Dan Rosen, the founder and general partner at Commerce. 

Kalshi

Cited by: QED Investors

Total funding: $36 million

What it does: Kalshi is a federally regulated financial exchange and prediction market through which customers can place bets on the outcome of certain events, ranging from changes in the weather to winners of award shows.

Why it's promising: "A system that combines the invested wisdom of crowds and incentives to discover facts on which to ground forecasts can increase our ability to estimate future likelihoods accurately. Previous hurdles including ease of use, insufficient regulatory approval, and a lack of sufficient capital for a marketplace to grow have been obstacles for a liquid prediction market with high participation to be built," said Matt Risley, a partner at QED.  

"Kalshi may be putting the pieces together (including regulatory approval from the Commodity Futures Trading Commission) to fulfill the promise of prediction markets in the US that could produce spillover effects to broader leadership decision-making," Risley added.

Kanmon

Cited by: Bling Capital and Activant

Total funding: $4.2 million

What it does: Kanmon enables software companies for specific industries to build and offer financing products to their business customers and suppliers.

Why it's promising: "Credit is at the heart of every B2B transaction, and with improved data access and KYB solutions, the embedded lending space is set to take off. Kanmon, led by a team of industry veterans from the likes of LendUp, Afterpay, and Plastiq, is well-positioned to emerge as the leader in the space," said Andrew Steele, a partner at Activant Capital.

Lean Financial

Cited by: Inspired Capital (investor)

Total funding: $6 million

What it does: Lean offers benefits services for companies employing gig-economy workers, including products like cash advances and instant payouts. 

Why it's promising: "Most notable is Lean's B2B2C approach — the company offers an embedded experience that marketplaces can turn on in short order to offer to their workers. They are poised to serve the 1099 and W2 flex workforce at scale and are already working with major marketplaces like Flywheel, the taxi app," said Mark Batsiyan, a cofounder, partner, and the COO of Inspired Capital. 

Mantl

Cited by: CapitalG (investor)

Total funding:  $83 million

What it does: Mantl enables US banks and credit unions to offer customers the ability to open accounts digitally.

Why it's promising: "Community banks and credit unions comprise a critical part of the US economy and, on a local level, form the fabric that keeps communities healthy and vibrant. Mantl has become a critical partner to these important local financial institutions which, in order to compete with fintechs, need to provide best-in-class digital customer solutions. Opening an account is the first step in any banking journey," said Jesse Wedler, a general partner at CapitalG.

"Mantl removes the arduous, in-person experience of opening an account and seamlessly moves it completely online — much to the benefit and delight of consumer and business-banking customers across the country."

Masterworks

Cited by: Citi Ventures

Total funding: $110 million 

What it does: Masterworks allows investors to buy and trade shares in multimillion-dollar works of art by offering an easy-to-use secondary market. 

Why it's promising: "Fine art is an asset class that has traditionally only been accessible to a select few. Masterworks unlocks this asset class and enables more investors of all types to diversify their investment portfolio and appreciate the many benefits of investing in fine art," said Thompson Barro, the vice president for venture investing at Citi Ventures.

Mercury

Cited by: Sapphire Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $163 million

What it does: Mercury provides banking for startups to start and scale their businesses. 

Why it's on the list: "Immad Akhund, Mercury's CEO and founder, understands the unique business-banking challenges facing startups and e-commerce businesses alike. As a four-time founder and active fintech investor himself, Immad has personal exposure to the evolving financial needs of a company," said Rajeev Dham, a partner at Sapphire Ventures.

"Mercury was built as a digital-first banking platform that scales as companies' financial needs become more complex. While traditional banks do play a key role in enabling fintech services, these bigger rivals are cumbersome and less agile to support innovation at the customer layer. Mercury has the unique flexibility to support both TradFi/Web2 and crypto-native businesses," Dham said.

Merge

Cited by: Bain Capital Ventures

Total funding: $19.5 million

What it does: Merge offers developers a single API to add hundreds of data sources across accounting, ticketing, and many other software categories.

Why it's promising: "Business data has moved to the cloud over the past two decades, but it remains a pain for developers to integrate with different data sources in order to deliver better experiences and analytics. Merge dramatically reduces time and money spent on integrating to accounting systems, among other categories, which enables developers to focus on adding value, not syncing data," said Kevin Zhang, a partner at Bain Capital Ventures.

Metronome

Cited by: Index Ventures

Total funding: $35 million

What it does: Metronome offers billing infrastructure that helps other software companies launch usage-based business models.

Why it's promising: "Constructing a billing system that can handle flexible, usage-based billing is no easy feat. I experienced it firsthand during my time at Stripe and have seen it cause roadblocks in pricing and packaging discovery, delay product launches, and directly impact revenues for many of the companies I partner with. Some of our own Index portfolio, like Starburst and Cockroach Labs, that rely on consumption-based pricing models are happy customers, and I'm positive there are many more," said Mark Fiorentino, a partner at Index Ventures.

Middesk

Cited by: Sapphire Ventures 

Total funding: $77 million

What it does: Middesk provides analytics around business and customer identity to allow banks to speed up onboarding and provide better underwriting models. 

Why it's promising: "Business identity is 'table-stakes' for fintechs and financial institutions, a necessary input for provisioning a plethora of financial products such as bank accounts, credit cards, working capital and payroll solutions. Co-Founders Kyle Mack and Kurt Ruppel recognized the lack of a 'single source of truth' for accessing this data in a consumable format. To tackle the challenge head-on, they created Middesk, which has since emerged as the de facto Know Your Business (KYB) platform and is well-positioned to leverage its business identity dataset to expand into other verticals for data and analytics.," said Rajeev Dham, a partner at Sapphire Ventures. 

MonetizeNow

Cited by: Two Sigma Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $3 million

What it does: MonetizeNow is a quoting and billing service for enterprise, business-to-business software companies. 

Why it's promising: "B2B SaaS enterprises are struggling in their go-to-market as they have to duct-tape several disparate systems for billing, usage, and quoting. This results in self-induced stunted revenue growth. MonetizeNow unlocks jammed GTM processes and enterprise-revenue growth by providing a frictionless and single system of truth for GTM across all sales channels," said Frances Schwiep, a partner at Two Sigma Ventures. 

Moonpay

Cited by: Pantera Capital

Total funding: $555 million

What it does: MoonPay helps users convert fiat currencies into cryptocurrencies. 

Why it's promising: "MoonPay is a top fiat-to-crypto on-ramp company servicing both existing crypto and venturing into non-crypto companies. MoonPay has strong partnerships and traction with large crypto exchanges, developers, and even non-crypto companies. They are leading in terms of independent fiat-crypto on-ramps, and have top-tier investors," said Paul Veradittakit, a partner at Pantera Capital.

Mudflap

Cited by: Point72 Ventures

Total funding: $50 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Mudflap is an app that facilitates diesel-fuel payments for small and mid-sized commercial-truck fleets. The startup aggregates demand and negotiates best-in-the-industry discounts at roughly 1,000 fuel stops across the country.

Why it's promising: "The company launched in 2020 and became the third-largest fuel network in the US, behind just FleetCor and WEX, within 18 months. The company is expanding their discount suite to include convenience-store spend and repairs, as well as expanding their product suite to include insurance, banking, and lending," said Tripp Shriner, a partner at Point72 Ventures.

"The company is providing much-needed payments and spend-management innovation in an industry that historically lacks data transparency, intuitive user experiences, and seamless payment flows," he added.

Northstar

Cited by: GGV Capital

Total funding: $10.8 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Northstar works with companies to offer financial-wellness tools — like compensation visualizations and access to financial advisors — as an employee benefit. 

Why it's promising: "Northstar enables employers to better attract and retain top talent by providing an all-in-one platform to guide employees through every financial and life situation, and show them the full value of their compensation, equity, and benefits. We see potential for Northstar to become the definitive employee financial-management platform for mid-market and enterprise companies in the US and across the world," said Marcello Rossi, an investor at GGV.

Novo

Cited by: GGV Capital

Total funding: $135.5 million

What it does: Novo is a small-business-banking app.

Why it's promising: "Many small, mainstream American businesses operate in an analog manner. Novo has an opportunity to digitize their operations, drive greater business efficiency, and meet their financial needs. Furthermore, Michael spent the early years at Novo building out sophisticated, in-house fintech infrastructure as opposed to relying on banking-as-a-service providers to scale quickly," said Hans Tung, a managing partner at GGV Capital.

"As a result, today Novo is able to create a unique user experience and has achieved superior, profitable unit economics when servicing small businesses relative to other SMB neobanks," Tung added.

Onward

Cited by: Citi Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $13 million

What it does: Onward is a fintech platform designed to help coparents manage shared finances for their children more easily, from communicating about finances to sharing past or future expenses and seeing a clear balance of who owes whom. 

Why it's promising: "Modern coparenting comes with many challenges, with financial disagreements often at the top of the list. Onward's user-friendly platform bridges the divide by simplifying expense tracking and planning, enabling coparents to discuss money without conflict. Such a thoughtful and human-centered product brings people closer to fintech and highlights its ultimate goal, which is to make people's lives easier," said Luis Valdich, the managing director for venture investing at Citi Ventures.

Payabli

Cited by: Bling Capital (investor) and Fika Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $4.5 million

What it does: Payabli enables niche software companies to launch their own payments products.

Why it's promising: "Payabli is truly turning embedded payments from a cost center into a profit center for brands, merchants, and ISVs of all sizes," said Matt Hersh, an operating partner at Fika Ventures.

"Vertical software companies and marketplaces are embracing payments as a way to deepen customer relationships and generate additional revenue opportunities. Payabli makes it easy for these companies to launch and manage the full suite of payments products to their customers," said Kyle Lui, a general partner at Bling Capital.

Pocketnest

Cited by: Mercury Fund

Total funding: $2 million, according to Crunchbase

What it does: Pocketnest is a financial-wellness tool that uses behavioral science and psychology designed specifically for millennial and Gen X customers and can be licensed by credit unions and banks. 

Why it's promising: "The company is based on behavioral science and licenses its software to financial institutions and employers, helping them to more deeply connect with a younger audience and grow their business," said Adrian Fortino, a managing director at Mercury Fund.

Portabl

Cited by: 6th Man Ventures (investor)

Total funding: $2.5 million

What it does: Portabl uses decentralized identity technology that banks, fintechs, and crypto companies can use to offer a universal verification process for customers. 

Why it's promising: "There is a huge shift taking place around digital identity from federated to self-sovereign identity. Portabl is solving this issue by creating a universal digital identity that gives consumers control of who has access and what data they share with a vertical focus on financial institutions," said Michelle Dhansinghani, an investor at 6th Man Ventures.

"What differentiates Portabl's solution is that it spans both traditional financial institutions and Web3 native companies looking to securely onboard and maintain users and enabling an interoperable future," Dhansinghani added.

Protego Trust Bank

Cited by: Science (investor)

Total funding: $104.9 million, according to PitchBook

What it does: Protego is a trust bank offering custody, lending, and trading services. 

Why it's on the list: "Protego is one of only three digital custody banks to have conditional approval for its OCC Charter, and it was the first financial institution that handles trading, lending, issuance, and custody of digital assets to receive approval from the federal regulator. As the cryptocurrency market matures, institutions like Protego will be critical for holding and protecting those investments," Mike Jones, the CEO of Science, said.

Sardine

Cited by: Nyca Partners (investor) and XYZ Venture Capital (investor)

Total funding: $71 million

What it does: A fraud and payment infrastructure provider for fintechs and crypto companies.

Sardine announced a $51.5 million, Series B round this September led by existing investor a16z, with participation from Nyca Partners, XYZ, and others.  

Why it's promising: "Sardine's solution was developed by a team of fintech-industry veterans from Coinbase and Revolut who understand firsthand the pain associated with fraud as well as the friction traditional AML/KYC solutions create in the conversion funnel for new users," said Ross Fubini, the founder and managing partner of XYZ Venture Capital. 

"Sardine's suite of solutions bridges traditional financial services and crypto infrastructure, allowing for instant bank ACH transfer for crypto on-ramps. This is increasingly  important as we are still in the very early innings of onboarding new users to both online financial services and crypto," Fubini added. 

"Sardine is helping to bridge the gap between the archaic payment infrastructure in the US and the real-time expectations of the consumers and firms. The US payment infrastructure essentially requires funds to come to rest at one financial institution before they can be moved to another. This delay is a significant pain point for every bank, broker-dealer, crypto exchange, NFT marketplace, and a host of other firms. Sardine relieves this pain by fishing out the fraud," said Tom Brown, a partner and general counsel at Nyca Partners. 

Signal Advisors

Cited by: Mercury Fund (investor)

Total funding: $75 million

What it does: Signal works with independent financial advisors by offering software they can use to better distribute annuities and life-insurance products. 

Why it's promising: "Signal is revolutionizing the financial advisory space by providing a world-class, efficient, end-to-end software platform that simplifies every part of a financial advisor's practice. Based in Detroit, Signal is led by an impressive team of finance and technology experts and middle-American entrepreneurs with firsthand experience in financial-advisory firms," said Adrian Fortino, the managing director of Mercury Fund. 

Sila

Cited by: Madrona Venture Group (investor)

Total funding: $20.7 million

What it does: Sila builds developer tools and APIs for fintech companies to send, receive, and hold funds while being in regulatory compliance. 

Why it's promising: "Sila's platform allows fintech developers to easily integrate compliant payment infrastructure via their proprietary APIs. This reduces time to launch from nearly a year to less than a month and allows fintech companies to provide a modern end-user experience. The platform is built for scale and can support companies through significant growth with no change in architecture," said Hope Cochran, the managing director of Madrona Venture Group.

"They have several major customers using Sila to provide instant ACH capabilities and are particularly strong in enabling crypto on- and off-ramps and next-generation fintech use cases," Cochran added. 

Spectral Finance

Cited by: FinTech Collective

Total funding: $30 million

What it does: Spectral Finance helps lenders better understand borrowers' creditworthiness in the Web3 economy.

Why it's promising: "The company has a very talented team of engineers and machine-learning scientists solving one of the hardest problems left in DeFi, which is under-collateralized debt for institutions (such as DAOs) that are native to blockchains," said Sarah Parsons Wolter, a principal at FinTech Collective.

Stratyfy

Cited by: Madrona Venture Group

Total funding: $3.9 million

What it does: Stratyfy offers artificial-intelligence and machine-learning tools for credit-risk assessment for lenders such as fintechs, neobanks, and small-business lenders. The startup also has fraud-detection software that helps identify credit- and debit-card transaction fraud.

Why it's promising: "Stratyfy is offering a new kind of 'transparent' infrastructure that sits behind lenders' machine-learning algorithms for making credit choices. One of the problems with running AI on today's credit-related datasets is that the AI can (and often does) inadvertently reinforce bias and discrimination in credit decisions. Stratyfy solves this problem using their probabilistic-rules engine, where they help customers better 'weight' various rules as well as find new rules from their data," said Hope Cochran, the managing director at Madrona Venture Group.

Treasure Financial

Cited by: Jump Capital (investor)

Total funding: $10 million

What it does: Treasure offers a cash-management service companies can use to earn more money on their idle cash reserves. 

Why it's promising: "Jump first partnered with Treasure in 2021. Then, they were at the vanguard of CFOs adopting more sophisticated tools and technology to manage the finance organization. Now, as access to equity has tightened and yields have risen, Treasure's growth has been explosive," said Sach Chitnis, a cofounder and partner at Jump Capital.

Unit

Cited by: Insight Partners (investor)

Total funding: $170 million

What it does: Unit enables customers to easily embed financial products and services into their offering via APIs.

Why it's promising: "Financial products and services continue to be unbundled from traditional financial institutions," said Nikhil Sachdev, a managing director at Insight Partners. "Unit facilitates this by creating a win-win-win for customers, platforms, and banks alike by giving platforms the ability to quickly and easily embed financial products and monetize them, giving customers the experience they want in the forum they want it, while allowing banks to leverage the growth and customer base of those platforms without having to integrate each platform into their core."

Wildfire Systems

Cited by: QED Investors (investor)

Total funding: $24.5 million

What it does: Wildfire Systems is a drop-in loyalty, rewards, and cash-back program that online customers can use to tap into merchant benefits. Wildfire announced a strategic investment from Citi Ventures in October. 

Why it's promising: "Tech giants have been battling to become the starting point for years — for browsing, for search, and now for commerce. The company that controls the start of the buyer journey can influence what people buy, where they buy from, and how they pay," said Laura Bock, a partner at QED.  

"Wildfire makes it possible for any service to monetize and reward users' organic shopping across desktop and mobile. Financial services companies have a huge opportunity to improve their rewards offerings for e-commerce purchases," Bock added.

Willa

Cited by: FinTech Collective (investor)

Total funding: $30 million (debt and equity)

What it does: This US- and Sweden-based fintech helps facilitate payments for freelancers and creators.

Why it's promising: "Willa is on a mission to power the future of work and become the financial OS for creators — a market that represents some 60 million people who contributed $1.3 trillion to the US economy last year alone," said Sarah Parsons Wolter, a principal at FinTech Collective. "The team's first product allows agencies to pay on their own terms, while influencers can receive payment of their invoice in 30 seconds or less for a small fee." 

Wrapbook

Cited by: Cowboy Ventures

Total funding: $130.6 million

What it does: Wrapbook provides payroll, onboarding, compliance, and insurance products for the entertainment industry.

Why it's promising: "Wrapbook has found a real pain point in the production industry and by doing so is able to find a wedge into this industry and continue to upsell additional services that are useful to the business. This is especially useful for businesses that have traditionally been underserved by financial services. By using this go-to-market, Wrapbook is able to continue to expand vertically (as we've seen with companies like Toast) but also horizontally to other adjacent industries with similar pain points," said Jillian Williams, a principal at Cowboy Ventures.

Zip

Cited by: Sapphire Ventures

Total funding: $81 million 

What it does: Zip uses automation to manage spending requests for companies. 

Why it's promising: "As procurement-spend initiation becomes increasingly decentralized and more lateral reviews are needed within organizations, Zip becomes a mission-critical procurement-software solution for any fintech, software, and/or IT infrastructure business. Zip makes the overly complicated and tangled procurement process effortless by automating workflows, providing real-time visibility, and ultimately reducing risk across startups and large enterprises," said Rajeev Dham, a partner at Sapphire Ventures.

Zirtue

Cited by: Mercury Fund (investor)

Total funding: $6.5 million

What it does: Zirtue is a relationship-based, peer-to-peer lender. 

Why it's promising: "Studies show that people are more willing to lend money to family or friends if they could trust and have confidence in where the money is going or how it is being use," said Samantha Lewis, a principal at Mercury Fund. "Zirtue has built a platform that provides transparency and clarity in relationship-based lending and creates financial-inclusive solutions for consumers, so that people don't have to turn to predatory loans or worry about damaging close relationships."
