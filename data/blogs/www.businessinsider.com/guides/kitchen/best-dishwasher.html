    The best dishwashers don't just save you time and energy and help prevent chapped hands. They can keep your water bill down, too. In fact, according to Energy Star, a standard modern dishwasher requires less water than it takes to wash dishes by hand. What's more, because dishwashers use much hotter water, they can kill bacteria and sanitize dishes much more effectively. Therefore, a dishwasher is one of the better large appliance purchases you can make to improve your quality of life, especially if you regularly wash dishes for more than one or two place settings. 

    During my years as a residential carpenter, I helped to install, adjust, and remove a variety of dishwashers. I used this hands-on experience, as well as the professional insight of five experts, to assemble our top 10 dishwashers for a range of budgets and kitchen needs. If you're searching for the best dishwasher for your home, this list can help you narrow down your options.

    What to look for in a dishwasher

    When choosing the best dishwasher for your needs, it may help to consider the following features:

    Drying: All dishwashers have a drying feature, but the best dishwashers may dry more effectively. If you want to make sure you won't have to spend any extra time hand-drying dishes before putting them away, opt for models with dedicated drying features or technology. These tend to be more expensive, but the added convenience — especially if you have a lot of plastic dishware or utensils — could make them worth the investment.

    Condensation vs. electric drying: Condensation drying works by finishing the rinse cycle with extremely hot water. The much cooler surface of the stainless steel tub then draws this moisture out as condensation. Electric drying dishwashers use a heating element to turn the moisture on your dishes into steam, which is then vented out with a fan. The high temperatures reached during condensation drying makes it more hygienic than electric drying. Although electric drying tends to work faster, the heating element may melt nearby plastic items. Electric drying is also less energy efficient than condensation drying.

    Cycle speed: If you'd like the option to clean and dry loads quickly, you'll want to make sure your dishwasher has an "express," "speed," or "quick" cycle. Typically, these cycles work faster by using extra-hot water, although they may work less efficiently for heavily soiled loads. The best dishwashers will usually have a few different cycle speeds to choose from.

    Noise: Walker says most modern dishwashers now come with advanced sound insulation technology that reduces noise levels during operation to as low as 40 dBA or lower. This allows you to run the dishwasher without disrupting the household or conversations. "Keep in mind that quieter dishwashers tend to be more expensive," Walker says. In short, you may want to consider whether a few decibels is really worth the bigger price tag. Nick Yahoodain, CEO of Advanced Builders and Contractors, also recommends checking reviews online before settling on your choice to learn what other users who own the model have to say, since decibel ratings may be misleading at times.

    Cleaning: The best dishwashers prioritize cleaning performance to make sure your dishes always come out clean, even when they're greasy or heavily soiled. They can also save you water by helping minimize the need for pre-rinsing. Walker recommends features like multiple spray arms, rotating jets, and powerful water pressure to ensure a deep and thorough wash. Dishwashers that come with bottle washer jets can help properly clean tall or narrow items like baby bottles.

    Food disposal: Dishwashers use one of two methods to dispose of food particles during the cleaning process: a hard food disposer or a filtration system. Disposers work much like a sink garbage disposal by pulverizing scraps of food and flushing them out of the drain. Filtration systems, on the other hand, collect and trap this food, preventing it from recirculating in the clean wash water. If your dishwasher has a manual filter, you must empty it out periodically. Self-cleaning models force food through a fine mesh, turning it into tiny particles that are washed away. While hard food disposers are essentially maintenance-free, they are noticeably louder than filtration systems. Make sure to check a dishwasher's food disposal method, and any maintenance required, before purchasing.

    Efficiency: The more efficient your dishwasher, the less power it will require to operate — and the less it will cost annually. Walker says energy-efficient dishwasher models like Beko are designed to minimize water and energy consumption, helping you reduce your environmental footprint and lowering your energy bills. "Some models may also qualify for rebates or incentives from local utility companies," she says.

    Aesthetics: The best dishwashers come in a range of handle, finish, and control panel options, so take the time to consider what will look best in your kitchen. Many high-end dishwashers now come with panel-ready doors, Walker says, which allows you to customize the appearance of your dishwasher to seamlessly match your kitchen cabinetry for a sleek and integrated look.

    WiFi Connectivity: If you prefer smart appliances, the best dishwasher for you will likely feature WiFi connectivity so you can control it via smartphone app. Smart dishwasher features let you do things like remotely start and stop cycles, monitor cycle progress, and receive notifications when the cycle is finished or when your unit needs maintenance.

    Specialty cycles: In addition to the standard cycle options found on many dishwashers, like quick wash and heavy wash, the best dishwashers often feature specialty cycles for specific purposes. These include sanitizing cycles that use extra hot water to eliminate bacteria, which is specifically recommended by Kaylyn Brunskole, the technical manager of Food Equipment at NSF International. She says the certified sanitizing cycle on your dishwasher means it has been tested to reduce 99.999% of bacteria. "Cleaning removes visible soil from a surface, while sanitizing reduces the bacteria on a surface," Brunskole says, adding that it's not always necessary to sanitize dishes, though you'll want to use this cycle when you have raw meat juice on a cutting board, for instance. Other useful options are "delicate" cycles that use low-pressure water to protect fragile stemware and high-temperature cycles to tackle baked-on grease and food residue.

    Rack options: Walker says many dishwashers now come with flexible racks you can adjust to accommodate various sizes and shapes of dishes, including large pots, pans, and tall glasses. "Collapsible dividers provide additional customization options, maximizing the dishwasher's capacity," she says. What's more, you can easily find dishwashers with a third rack at the top for smaller items like utensils, cutlery, long cooking tools, and small bowls — items that might otherwise clutter up the bottom racks or fall to the bottom. A third rack can help keep the unit more organized.

    Dishwasher FAQs

    What types of dishwashers are there?

    Built-in dishwashers are the most common type. These install directly into a dedicated area under your kitchen counter and connect to permanent plumbing hookups. Portable dishwashers are freestanding units that hook up to your faucet and sit on wheels so you can move them around as you like. If you're very tight on space, you might opt for a countertop dishwasher for added convenience. These connect right to your kitchen faucet and use less water than full-size dishwashers, though they also have a much smaller size.

    What sizes of dishwashers are there?

    Safaradi says standard dishwashers are 23.75 inches wide by 33.625 inches high by 24.625 inches deep. ADA compliant units are shorter, measuring 24 inches wide by 32 inches high by 24 inches deep. Oversized dishwashers can be as wide as 42 inches, while compact dishwashers tend to have a width of about 18 inches.

    What is the average life of a dishwasher?

    On average, you can expect a dishwasher to last you about 10 years before you need to replace it, Yahoodain says. He adds that your dishwasher may require maintenance during this period. This timeline can also depend on how you use the machine, so make sure to follow the manufacturer's guidelines for optimal operation. Keep in mind, too, that stainless steel tubs will last longer than plastic ones.

    Is a front or top-mounted control panel better?

    This is largely a matter of personal preference. Some people like the minimal look of the top-mounted control panel design, which also prevents curious kids from pressing buttons at random. But top-mounted units don't let you monitor the progress of the dishwasher, though they do tend to have a light that comes on when the cycle finishes. You may prefer a front-mounted control panel if you want to press buttons without having to open the unit.

    What certifications should I look for?

    The best dishwashers will often have a NSF/ANSI 184: Residential Dishwashers certification. This certification confirms the sanitizing cycle will kill 99.999% of bacteria. Energy Star certifications indicate that the dishwasher meets strict energy efficiency standards. Depending on where you live, purchasing one of these dishwashers might qualify you for certain rebates.

    Which dishwasher gets the dishes the most clean?

    Walker says Bosch dishwashers are her top choice for models that clean dishes most effectively. The brand holds the reputation for best in class for high-end, efficient dishwashers, she says. As a close second, she recommends Miele dishwashers, emphasizing the overall reliability, durability, performance, and innovative features of both brands.

    Which dishwasher brand is the most reliable?

    "Bosch is incredibly reliable and easy to integrate into US-made cabinetry," Walker says, adding that it runs quietly and doesn't have many repair issues. Walker says Miele is another reliable brand, adding that her company trusts Miele and Bosch dishwashers for their quality and performance and uses them almost exclusively. Our appliance retail expert Safaradi says, "High quality brands we recommend and carry include Bosch, Samsung, LG, and GE."

    What months do dishwashers go on sale?

    Most dishwashers tend to go on sale between September and October, when retailers need to make room for new models coming in. That said, Bosch and Miele dishwashers rarely, if ever, go on sale, Walker says.

    How much should I pay for a dishwasher?

    You can expect to pay between $1,300 and $1800 for mid-range dishwashers and $2,200 to $3,000 for high-end models, Walker says. Budget dishwashers typically cost around $1,000 or a little less.
