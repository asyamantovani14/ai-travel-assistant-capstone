10 causes of pancreatitis and what to do if you have signs of this serious medical condition

Written by Adam Barnes
Medically Reviewed

  • Pancreatitis is a serious condition that can cause severe abdominal pain and digestive problems.
  • Common causes include gallstones, heavy alcohol use, high levels of fat in your blood, and smoking.
  • If you have pancreatitis, a doctor can suggest treatments and help lower your risk of complications.

Your pancreas plays a vital role in healthy digestion. It makes enzymes to break down food and produces insulin and glucagon, which regulate your blood sugar. 

But inflammation in your pancreas — often caused by things like your diet, cigarette smoking, alcohol use, and infections — can lead to a serious health condition called pancreatitis. 

There are two types of pancreatitis:

  • Acute pancreatitis is the main cause of gastrointestinal-related hospital admissions in the US. Symptoms start suddenly and usually improve within a week, though some cases can involve serious complications. You may have multiple episodes of acute pancreatitis, especially without treatment. 
  • Chronic pancreatitis causes progressive damage to your pancreas. Your symptoms may come and go, but they can last for long periods. They also tend to get worse over time. You'll need ongoing medical care to manage your symptoms.  

Both types of pancreatitis can cause severe abdominal pain and make it difficult for you to digest food properly.

While pancreatitis may develop as a sign of pancreatic cancer, it can have a number of other causes, too.

Read on to learn the symptoms of pancreatitis, along with the most common causes of this condition and how to treat them.

Symptoms of pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis usually starts with sudden upper abdominal pain. You may also feel pain in the middle of your back, says Dr. Petros Benias, director of gastroenterology at Lenox Hill Hospital.

Other symptoms include:

  • Nausea and vomiting
  • A tender abdomen
  • Fever

Most people fully recover from a case of mild pancreatitis. But severe cases can cause serious complications, including:

  • Kidney failure
  • Breathing issues
  • Infection of your pancreas
  • Internal bleeding

Chronic pancreatitis, on the other hand, usually progresses slowly over time, so it can be difficult to diagnose. 

"It's the result of many, often low-grade, episodes of pancreatitis that, when compounded, create scar tissue in the pancreas," Benias says.

Symptoms of chronic pancreatitis can come and go. When you do have symptoms, they may include:

  • Pain in your upper abdomen
  • Pain in your abdomen that feels worse after eating
  • Weight loss
  • Oily, smelly stools
  • Nausea and vomiting

Here are 10 of the most common causes of acute and chronic pancreatitis.

1. Gallstones

Gallstones are the most common reason for acute pancreatitis, causing up to 40% of cases. 

Gallstones develop when your gallbladder doesn't empty completely. Your gallbladder stores a fluid, called bile, that helps digest fat. When you eat fat, your gallbladder releases the bile through a tube called your common bile duct. 

However, when the gallbladder doesn't empty completely or often enough, bile becomes concentrated into crystallized stones, called gallstones. Those gallstones can then get stuck in the common bile duct or the pancreatic duct, causing a blockage that leads to pancreatitis.

When gallstones cause pancreatitis, you may notice symptoms like:

  • Severe abdominal pain on your upper left side
  • Nausea and vomiting
  • Fever and chills
  • Jaundice, or yellowing of your skin and the whites of your eyes
  • Increased heart rate and shortness of breath
  • A swollen abdomen

What to do about it: Sometimes, gallstones will pass on their own. But if not, they may cause serious complications, so your doctor will likely recommend removing them via:

  • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): In this procedure, a gastroenterologist or surgeon runs a tube with a camera at its end — called an endoscope — from your throat to your bile duct. They can remove gallstones with tools placed through the endoscope and clear blockages by placing stents, or small hollow tubes that keep the bile duct open. 
  • Gallbladder removal: Your doctor may also recommend removing your gallbladder to prevent gallstones. This is usually done via laparoscopic surgery, which means your surgeon uses a tiny camera and small tools to remove your gallbladder.

2. Alcohol

Heavy alcohol consumption can greatly increase your chances of developing pancreatitis: If you have a history of alcohol use disorder, you have about four times the risk.

Alcohol use causes up to 40% of chronic pancreatitis cases, and up to a third of acute pancreatitis cases.

Note: Pancreatitis from alcohol consumption usually happens when you drink heavily over several years and have other risk factors, such as smoking. Your risk starts to increase when you have four to five drinks per day. 

What to do about it: If you have an episode of pancreatitis, your doctor may recommend you stop drinking alcohol. Quitting drinking will lower your risk of further episodes and decrease your chances of developing chronic pancreatitis. 

Your doctor may also recommend taking steps to address alcohol use, such as: 

  • Medical detox, which can reduce and treat your withdrawal symptoms.
  • Counseling, support groups, and recovery programs like Alcoholics Anonymous (AA).
  • Medications, such as naltrexone and acamprosate that help reduce cravings, or disulfiram, which helps prevent alcohol use by making you feel very ill if you drink.
  • Treatment for co-occurring mental health symptoms, like anxiety or depression.

3. Hypertriglyceridemia

Hypertriglyceridemia, or high levels of fat (aka triglycerides) in the blood, causes up to 10% of acute pancreatitis cases. 

When you have very high levels of triglycerides, they get broken down to free fatty acids that can cause inflammation in your pancreas, says Dr. Shaheer Siddiqui, a gastroenterologist at Memorial Hermann.

Potential causes of elevated triglycerides include:

  • Lifestyle factors: Drinking alcohol, a sedentary lifestyle, and eating saturated fats and refined carbohydrates can all raise your triglyceride levels.
  • Underlying medical conditions: Diabetes, hypothyroidism, and rheumatoid arthritis can affect your triglyceride levels.
  • Medications: Certain medications can also affect your triglyceride levels. Examples include beta blockers used to treat high blood pressure, corticosteroids that treat inflammation, and oral estrogen used in hormone replacement therapy.
  • Genetics: Some lipid disorders that raise triglyceride levels run in families. Examples include familial combined hyperlipidemia and familial hypertriglyceridemia.  

Typically, your triglycerides won't cause pancreatitis unless they're in the "very high" range — 500 milligrams per deciliter (mg/dl) — and you also have another risk factor, such as uncontrolled diabetes or heavy alcohol use. 

What to do about it: If you're at risk for pancreatitis, you can lower your triglycerides by:

  • Reducing your consumption of sugar and refined carbohydrates.
  • Reducing or stopping your alcohol consumption.
  • Developing a regular exercise routine that includes 50 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise or 75 minutes of intense aerobic exercise, plus two sessions of strength training per week.
  • Eating a balanced diet that includes plenty of fresh fruit and vegetables, whole grains, and lean proteins.

Your doctor may also prescribe medications to lower your triglycerides, such as:

  • Statins
  • Prescription-strength omega-3 fatty acids
  • Fibrates, such as fenofibrate

4. Cigarette smoking

Smoking cigarettes can increase your risk of developing both acute and chronic pancreatitis. 

When your body breaks down tobacco, toxic byproducts, like nicotine and nicotine-derived nitrosamine ketone (NNK) can damage your pancreatic cells and lead to inflammation, Siddiqui says.  

Evidence suggests current smokers have a much higher chance of developing acute pancreatitis compared to non-smokers — and the more cigarettes you smoke, the greater your risk. 

What to do about it: The good news: Quitting smoking can help lower your chances of developing pancreatitis.

No single method for quitting smoking is foolproof, but some expert recommendations to help you quit include:

  • Knowing why you want to quit to help you stick with your plan.
  • Gradually cut down smoking before you quit to help minimize cravings.
  • Getting plenty of exercise, which may help reduce cravings.
  • Working with a therapist if mental health symptoms contribute to your smoking or make it harder to quit.
  • Considering nicotine replacement products. 

5. Certain medications 

Medications only rarely cause pancreatitis — about 0.1%-2% of acute pancreatitis cases, in total. 

Pancreatitis due to medication typically causes mild to moderate symptoms, though some cases may become severe.

Your symptoms will usually begin within one week of starting the medication, though in some cases you may not experience symptoms for weeks, or even months.

A few examples of the many medications associated with pancreatitis include:

  • Angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors (ACE inhibitors), 
  • Statins
  • Oral contraceptives and hormone replacement therapy
  • Diuretics
  • Highly active antiretroviral therapy
  • Valproic acid
  • Hypoglycemic medications

What to do about it: If your medication causes pancreatitis, your doctor will likely tell you to stop taking it immediately and recommend an alternative medication. 

6. Infections

Viral, bacterial, and parasitic infections cause about 10% of acute pancreatitis cases.

Viruses cause most of the infections that lead to pancreatitis. Examples include:

  • Hepatitis B, an infection of your liver
  • HIV (human immunodeficiency virus), a virus that weakens your immune system
  • Herpes simplex, an STI that causes outbreaks of painful, itchy sores in your genital region
  • Varicella-zoster, the virus responsible for chickenpox and shingles

What to do about it: Treatments for these viruses vary, but antiviral medications can help you manage symptoms. Vaccines can also help prevent infections of hepatitis B and varicella-zoster.

Other microorganisms that can sometimes cause pancreatitis include: 

  • Salmonella bacteria 
  • Parasites such as Ascaris lumbricoides and the Chinese liver fluke.

Antibiotics can treat salmonella, and anti-parasitic medications help kill parasites.

7. Hypercalcemia

Hypercalcemia — high calcium levels in your blood — can cause pancreatitis. 

You're most likely to have excessive calcium in your blood if you have a condition called 

hyperparathyroidism, which means your body makes too much parathyroid hormone. This hormone helps regulate calcium levels. 

Between 1%-8% of cases of hyperparathyroidism cause pancreatitis, but hyperparathyroidism itself isn't that common: It only affects about an estimated 100,000 people in the United States each year. 

Often, this condition doesn't cause any symptoms and only shows up in routine blood work. 

That said, you might experience a few symptoms, such as: 

  • Weak bones
  • Kidney stones
  • Excessive urination

What to do about it: Treatment for hyperparathyroidism generally includes either medications to regulate calcium levels in your blood or surgery to remove portions of your parathyroid gland.

8. Trauma, injury, or surgery to the abdomen or pancreas 

Rarely, you might develop pancreatitis after abdominal trauma caused by things like: 

  • Car and bicycle accidents
  • A fall or other high-impact injury
  • Stabbing or gunshot wounds

But this is pretty uncommon. To put things in perspective, only about 0.2%-1.1% of traumatic abdominal injuries lead to pancreatitis. 

It's also possible to develop pancreatitis after:

  • Surgery involving your abdomen: Between 0.8%-4% of abdominal surgeries will cause pancreatitis. While this condition usually needs medical monitoring, it rarely becomes a dangerous complication.
  • Non-abdominal surgeries: Spinal surgery or surgery of your throat can cause pancreatitis in some rare cases. 
  • Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP): Along with removing gallstones, ERCP helps diagnose other problems of your gallbladder, pancreas, and bile ducts by allowing a doctor to see the area. However, 2%-10% of people develop pancreatitis after this procedure.

9. Genetic factors 

Hereditary pancreatitis can cause chronic pancreatitis and increase your risk of pancreatic cancer. This rare condition develops because of genetic mutations that affect how your pancreas works.

Symptoms of hereditary pancreatitis usually begin in childhood. You'll have episodes of acute pancreatitis that eventually turn into chronic pancreatitis.

What to do about it: Genetic testing can confirm whether you have hereditary pancreatitis. Treatment focuses on managing symptoms and reducing lifestyle factors that can damage your pancreas, like smoking or drinking alcohol.

10. Underlying health conditions 

Health conditions that may lead to pancreatitis include:

  • Cystic fibrosis: About 1.25% of people with the genetic disorder cystic fibrosis will eventually develop pancreatitis. Cystic fibrosis damages your lungs and digestive system by thickening fluids in your body.  
  • Pancreatic cancer: In some cases, you might develop acute pancreatitis as an early sign of pancreatic cancer.
  • Obesity: Your risk of acute pancreatitis increases about 35% if you have obesity, or a BMI over 30, compared to a BMI that falls within the "normal" range. 
  • Diabetes:  If you have type 2 diabetes, your risk of acute pancreatitis increases between 1.5 and 3 times compared to people who don't have diabetes.

Treatment

Both types of pancreatitis require medical treatment. 

How to treat acute pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis often requires hospitalization, since this type can cause serious, life-threatening complications. For instance, some severe cases may lead to necrotizing pancreatitis, where part of your pancreas dies and becomes a risk for infection. 

"Necrotizing pancreatitis can be quite serious, and historically has a high mortality rate," Benias says.

About 80% of people admitted to the hospital with acute pancreatitis have mild cases and leave in a few days, but the condition is fatal in about 0.7% of cases. 

The treatment for acute pancreatitis usually involves resting your bowels and gut. So, your doctor may prescribe a liquid-only diet and large amounts of intravenous fluids. 

Once acute pancreatitis develops, the only thing doctors can really do is offer supportive care, or take steps to reduce how severe it gets, according to Benias.

Further treatment depends on the underlying cause of your acute pancreatitis. For example, you may need surgery to remove gallstones, or professional support to stop drinking alcohol, Benias says.

Important: About 3%-13% of cases of acute pancreatitis cause enough damage to your pancreas to develop into chronic pancreatitis — but getting timely medical treatment can lower your risk of chronic pancreatitis and help prevent long-term health effects. 

How to treat chronic pancreatitis

Treatment for chronic pancreatitis focuses on eliminating underlying triggers, like alcohol, tobacco, or medications you're taking. 

Benias says treatment may also include: 

  • Enzyme replacement therapy before meals. 
  • Endoscopic or surgical procedures to relieve pressure inside your pancreas due to scar tissue.

You can also develop diabetes from chronic pancreatitis if parts of your pancreas that produce insulin become scarred and damaged. Treatment, in this case, may involve taking metformin or insulin to manage your blood sugar. 

What's more, the constant inflammation in your pancreas may also increase your risk of pancreatic cancer. Treatment for this typically includes:

  • Surgery to remove tumors in or near your pancreas. In some cases, your entire pancreas may be removed.
  • Chemotherapy
  • Radiation therapy

Insider's takeaway

Gallstones and excessive alcohol consumption cause most cases of pancreatitis, though other factors like genetics and injuries can also increase your risk. 

You can take steps to lower your risk of pancreatitis by quitting smoking, reducing alcohol consumption, and eating a balanced diet.

The most common symptom of pancreatitis is pain in your abdomen, often in the middle and radiating to the back. So, you'll want to check in with your doctor right away if you have this type of pain. 

Pancreatitis can lead to serious complications, but prompt treatment can help address your symptoms before they get worse.
