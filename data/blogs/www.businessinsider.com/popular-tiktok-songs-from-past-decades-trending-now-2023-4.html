TikTok's power of resurrection

How the app creates new hits from old songs

TikTok's power of resurrection

How the app creates new hits from old songs

Callie Ahlgrim and Taylor Tyson
Apr. 11, 2023

Once widely dismissed as a silly app where teens share dance videos, TikTok has emerged in recent years as a preeminent tastemaker and cultural compass, especially for the music industry. It has the power to push a 2-year-old song to the top of the charts, build hype for a record-breaking album, and even catapult a previously obscure artist to the Grammys.

But this effect is not reserved for new artists and 21st-century superstars. Songs released more than 20 years ago — before many TikTok users were born — have been effectively resurrected when they grab the app's attention.

Whether it's a hip-shaker primed for choreography, a pleasant soundtrack for montages, or a niche hit that highlights an algorithmic quirk, creators employ a variety of methods to breathe new life into decades-old tracks.

To dive deeper into what makes older songs TikTok-certified to stand the test of time, Insider consulted Chartmetric, which compiles a weekly list of the most popular sounds on TikTok. We looked at the top trending 50 sounds used on TikTok for 80 weeks — from June 6, 2021, to October 15, 2022.

Our criteria for inclusion in our database were that each song had to be originally released before 2003 and had to have trended on TikTok's weekly top-50 chart over the 80-week period that we analyzed. We did not include any remixes or covers of songs that were released after 2003. We also did not include any holiday songs, as seasonal trends could skew the data.

While it's not always clear how or why a song resurfaces, we found that anything from a 1932 children's song to an 1984 pop hit have the ability to go viral on the platform.

We then analyzed the number of Spotify streams for each song — also sourced from Chartmetric — ranging from 60 days before its peak ranking on TikTok to 60 days after. This gave us a rough measure of TikTok's influence on songs that saw a resurgence outside the platform.
Of the 33 songs we analyzed, a third had up to a 12% increase in Spotify streams from 60 days before to 60 days after their peaks on TikTok's weekly top-50 chart. In this lower range, we more consistently saw songs that topped the charts when they were originally released.
For songs like "Frontin' (feat. Jay-Z) - Club Mix" by Pharrell , which has remained relatively popular since its release, a 10.9% streaming increase translates to about 8.9M streams.
When we adjust the scale to up to a 45% increase, another third of the songs showed significantly higher streaming growth than their earlier counterparts.
"Let's Groove" by Earth, Wind & Fire , which was first released in 1981, saw its Spotify streams rise by 24.7% 60 days after its peak on TikTok charts, following a viral dance trend.
Zooming out even further, seven songs saw streams increase by as much as 140% in the four-month time period.
For instance, Henry Hall's "Teddy Bear's Picnic" gained popularity after TikTok users played the 1932 children's song in combination with a popular autumn filter. The song went from 780K to 1.4M Spotify streams after its TikTok peak.
Two songs skyrocketed in streaming growth by nearly 700%. "Love Grows (Where My Rosemary Goes)" by Edison Lighthouse , which was originally released in 1970, became a staple sound for instructional content. Its Spotify streams increased from 8.7M to over 67.7M during this time.
Two songs in our dataset broke the scale, with one song's streams up by 2,228% and the other up a whopping 12,783%.
"Camel by Camel – Mix Vocal" by Sandy Marton took off thanks to a viral dance trend. Though it hit the top-50 sounds on TikTok for only one week, the song saw its Spotify streams skyrocket from 45K to over 5.8M between 60 days before and 60 days after its TikTok peak.
Insider reached out to some of the artists and producers whose songs appeared in our dataset to learn more about how they view the streaming surges and whether their TikTok success has felt like a career revitalization.

Insider's research suggests an online populace that's enamored with nostalgia, untroubled by a song's apparent outdatedness — perhaps surprising for an app heavily associated with Gen Z, a generation that seems to prize modernity.

"The young kids are pulling it out because they're realizing that the music today, it has no substance," the superproducer Teddy Riley said. "So people are always going back."

"They want the substance. They want good music. That's all they want," he added.

Riley produced several songs that have inspired dance trends on TikTok, including "No Diggity" by Blackstreet and "I Like" by Guy, the popular '80s hip-hop group he founded with Aaron Hall and Timmy Gatling.

To Riley, if a bygone track catches fire on TikTok, that indicates the song is "considered a classic."

He also said Gen Z creators seem hyperaware and skeptical of insincerity in music — which is to say songs that were engineered for virality with TikTok in mind.

For example, Michael Jackson's 2001 album cut "Heaven Can Wait," which Riley cowrote and produced, doesn't have a sticky hook that’s primed for a viral dance trend like the Renegade. In fact, it's known as one of Jackson's slowest songs.

But it nevertheless found success on TikTok and, in turn, entered Jackson's top 10 on Spotify — an outcome Riley attributed to the song's emotion, which he described as both genuine and palpable.

"That song was meant for someone else," he said. "That song was how Michael felt at that moment and he said, 'Man, this is going to be mine.' And I couldn't resist giving it to him because when he started singing it, it brought tears. People were like, 'Wow, he really meant it.'"

"Heaven Can Wait" was a moderate hit for Jackson when it was released, peaking at No. 72 on Billboard's Hot R&B/Hip-Hop Songs chart in 2002. But just a few weeks after the song picked up steam on TikTok, fans noticed that its streaming numbers and YouTube views had shot up by millions.

This is perhaps the most impressive sign of TikTok virality: It doesn't appear to stay contained within the app but quickly spreads to other platforms.

According to the current five members of Edison Lighthouse, which scored one of TikTok's biggest hits last year with the 1970 single "Love Grows (Where My Rosemary Goes)," the song's ubiquity has generated more appreciation for the band's entire discography.

In a statement provided to Insider via email, Brian Huggins, Peter Butt, Stephane Booroff, Mark Forton, and Rick Piggott said "the band name itself has skyrocketed" thanks to the virality of “Love Grows,” citing an average of 1 million Spotify streams a week across their catalog in 2022.

"We're seeing searches for 'where are they now,'" they added, saying they have no doubt the increased interest stems directly from TikTok.

The bandmates said the "Love Grows" lyrics are "vintage" and abstract, opening up the song to a variety of interpretations. The titular character, Rosemary, is "a figure of the imagination for each listener," they said, "much like the protagonist of a book."

"We've received renditions where creators have sung the gender-neutral pronoun 'they' instead of 'she,'" their statement read, concluding: "With so much negativity in the world, to have a song called 'Love Grows' be viral, is a refreshing reminder of what this world can be."

That may be true, but positivity is not a prerequisite for scoring a viral TikTok hit.

While the dark music of Insane Clown Posse exists on the opposite end of the spectrum from "Love Grows," TikTok has had a remarkably similar impact for the artists involved.

The hardcore-rap duo was founded by the Detroit natives Joseph Bruce, known as Violent J, and Joseph Utsler, known as Shaggy 2 Dope, in the early '90s.

Mike E. Clark coproduced Insane Clown Posse's first six albums, including 1997's "The Great Milenko," which includes the breakout TikTok favorite "Boogie Woogie Wu." The song is written from the perspective of a fictional, murderous boogeyman.

"I loved to work with those guys because I can really do anything I want and I can just do some crazy shit that maybe your typical rap group wouldn't really want to get into," Clark told Insider.

Though Insane Clown Posse has been critiqued for grotesque lyrics and described by the FBI as a nontraditional gang, its early music gave a voice to countercultural ideas and created a tight-knit fandom known as "Juggalos."

"It was kind of remarkable," Clark said. "It seemed a lot of the music that was coming out at the time, everyone was bragging about their rims and all the fancy rich stuff. Joe and Joey had no money so they started representing the scrubs, the kids that didn't get the good-looking girlfriends and couldn't afford the Dom Pérignon."

"It really connected with the lower-income suburban kids and it spread like wildfire," he said.

Clark said Insane Clown Posse's music took off "in a cult way” that resembles modern virality, which is fueled by TikTok's personalized algorithm.

Today a song can be used in tens of thousands of videos, more than enough to be deemed "viral," but remain contained within a niche community on the app — like MomTok or Corporate Girlies — while never appearing on the mainstream radar. The most-liked videos using "Boogie Woogie Wu" often use hashtags like "AltTikTok," "KinkTok," and "Horrorcore."

Still, a limited version of success on the app doesn't necessarily translate to disappointing numbers outside it, Clark said.

"This stuff is still paying the bills," he said. "It's just crazy. My statements, they hit a low once the download stuff started happening 10, 15 years ago or whatever. Everything kind of crashed down."

"But now they're getting the digital stuff figured out, I'm noticing increases dramatically within my publishing statements," he continued.

Clark gave credit to record companies for improving monetization strategies in the streaming era, but he also said TikTok played a role. Within the past two years, he said he's noticed "an upswing" across his catalog: "Maybe they'll find a song on TikTok, get turned onto it, and then go listen to the album."

TikTok is clearly a dynamic tool for music discovery, motivating users to dig through the digital crates and take advantage of the internet's vast sonic archives.

But at the end of the day, to the artists and producers behind the songs that take off decades after their release, the virality is proof they made music worth returning to.

"When I do go back and hear something we made, I go, 'Wow, that still sounds as fresh as it did when I did it,'" Clark said. "That was 25 years ago, and it still holds the test of time."

Methodology

Insider analyzed Chartmetric data for the weekly top trending 50 sounds used on TikTok for 80 weeks – from June 6, 2021, to October 15, 2022.

We removed any trending sounds that were not categorized by TikTok as copyrighted songs. Of the specified songs, our criteria for inclusion were that each song had to be originally released before 2003 and had to have trended on TikTok's weekly top-50 chart over the 80-week period. We did not include remixes or covers of songs that were released after 2003. We also removed holiday songs because of likely increased seasonal Spotify streaming outside of TikTok's influence.

We refer to a song's peak on TikTok based on when it reached its highest ranking within the top-50 sounds on the platform over the 80-week period. We analyzed Spotify streaming data every five days for day -60 (60 days before its peak on TikTok) to day 60 (60 days after its peak). We marked the peak on TikTok as zero. This time frame helped illustrate TikTok's influence on songs that saw a resurgence outside the platform.

Chartmetric's Spotify streaming data is limited to an artist's top-five most-streamed songs on their artist page. If a song was not in an artist's top-five songs on day -60, it was not included in our database.
