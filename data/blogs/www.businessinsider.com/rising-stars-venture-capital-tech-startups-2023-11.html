Meet the 66 rising stars of venture capital, who shook up the industry in 2023

By Leena Rao , Melia Russell , Samantha Stokes , Vishal Persaud , Ben Bergman , Madeline Renbarger , and Darius Rafieyan
2023-11-30T10:01:01Z

Save Saved Read in app

  • We asked our readers and top VCs from around the US to name the best up-and-coming investors of 2023.
  • These VCs come from big and small firms and invest in startups across all sectors and stages.
  • These 66 venture capitalists are the names to keep on your radar in 2024 and beyond. 

2023 was a tumultuous year for the venture capital industry. The first quarter of the year saw the collapse of its well-known partner, Silicon Valley Bank, and high interest rates helped to slow the pace of investing significantly compared to its 2021 highs. Tech and startup layoffs also reached a peak this year, with more than 180,000 jobs being cut across the tech industry, according to Crunchbase.

However, despite the economic downturn, there were several bright spots, including huge deals in generative AI and AI infrastructure startups.

And many young enterprising venture capitalists were able to source hot startups for their funds. Some even braved the tougher economic climate and successfully raised funds of their own.

This year, Business Insider asked the most important VCs across the US to name the most promising VCs in their cities.

Business Insider also asked the general public and last year's rising stars who they thought should make the cut.

The investors selected come from a wide array of backgrounds and range from associates to founding partners at their funds, and we also threw in a few picks of our own based on the investor's performance throughout this year.

And just like last year, more of this year's rising stars are women than men, indicating that more women are being brought into the pipeline of the traditionally male-dominated venture capital industry.

Scroll to see 2023's rising stars of venture capital, organized alphabetically by the investor's name.

Medha Agarwal, Defy.vc

Defy.vc has recently tapped Agarwal as its newest general partner. Based in San Francisco, she brings seven years of experience from Redpoint, where she led investments in fintech and vertical software that's mission critical for customers. Her portfolio includes dental practice Tend, livestream shopping app Whatnot, and infrastructure developer Livekit.

In her new role, Agarwal is expanding her scope of investments to include healthcare, climate tech, and marketplaces — a category that hits close to home. Before she got into investing, Agarwal started a social website for finding roommates and put in a brief stint at Thumbtack.

Outside of her day job, Agarwal writes a newsletter unpacking the biggest fintech news and trends of the day, titled Make Cents, that garners tens of thousands of views per post.

Amber Atherton, Patron

Atherton is a shining example of a successful startup founder-turned-venture investor. She founded Zyper, a startup that helped brands create private communities of their top fans, in 2017, and shepherded the company through the startup accelerator Y Combinator in 2018 and a successful acquisition by Discord in 2021.

In addition to backing seed-stage startups at the intersection of gaming and consumer tech at Patron, she launched and runs the Atherton Award at 30 girls schools to encourage more young women aged 11 to 18 to become entrepreneurs. She also wrote and published a best-selling book, "The Rise of Virtual Communities," earlier this year.

Atherton is looking for founders that "go ten layers deep to understand their users and players," she told Business Insider.

While Patron doesn't do deal attribution, said Atherton, some of the portfolio companies include the social music learning platform Tonic Music and the credit card recommendation platform Kudos.

Rob Biederman, Asymmetric

Biederman is the founder and Managing Partner of Asymmetric, a $105 million fund he founded in 2021. In the past year, he's made 30 new investments and 7 follow on investments across the fund's focus, early stage B2B software companies.

Biederman led Asymmetric's investments in OneScreen.ai, an AI-enabled marketplace for out-of-home (OOH) advertising; Torc, AI-powered platform for engaging remote software developers; and UpSmith, an upskilling and workforce platform.

Biederman co-founded and served as co-CEO for eight years of Catalant Technologies, a talent management company.

Charlotte Black, Oak HC/FT

When healthcare investor Black evaluates founders, she says she looks for humility and the ability to be a good recruiter.

"The best CEOs are the ones that set ambitious goals for their companies and trust their teams to execute, but are maniacal about the details," she told Business Insider.

Black, who has a specific interest in healthcare financial services, has been at Oak HC/FT since 2021, and her investments include virtual pediatric healthcare startup Imagine Pediatrics and healthcare payment platform Rialtic. She previously worked at Lightyear Capital and in investment banking.

Laura Bock, QED Investors

Fintech investor Bock has been at QED Investors since 2018 and has led the firm's investments in startups including freelancer financial management platform Collective and B2B payment processor Oatfi. When evaluating startups, she appreciates founders who balance their vision with realism.

"Founders must be inspiring and be able to share an exciting vision to potential employees, customers, partners, and investors," she told Business Insider. "They must equally be tethered to reality so they can appropriately steer the ship."

Before moving to venture capital, Bock cut her teeth at management consulting firm Oliver Wyman.

Natalie Borowski, Coefficient Capital

Borowski is an investor at Coefficient Capital, a five-year-old venture firm, where she focuses on digitally powered consumer companies. She helped execute investments in Nom Nom, a pet food company that Mars reportedly purchased for $1 billion; Oatly, which went public in 2021; and Lemme, a vitamin and supplement line from Kourtney Kardashian Barker.

Based in New York, Borowski keeps über-busy with her side hustles. Three years ago, she launched the Consumer Trends report franchise with veteran business reporter Dan Frommer. In 2022, she launched an online community, called OGC, for young people working in the consumer ecosystem. It includes peer mentoring, events, book clubs, and a Slack channel.

Taylor Brandt, Headline

Brandt built her operating career around growth and analytics for direct to consumer brands, including at kids clothing startup Rockets of Awesome. She then was an investor at seed stage firm Red Sea Ventures, and in 2021, joined Headline in New York where she focuses on backing B2B software companies with focus on vertical SaaS, marketplaces, commerce infrastructure and supply chain.

She has sourced the firm's investments in Avoma, Swell and Air. She's also a leader at the Emerging Venture Capitalists Association, a community for the pre-partner and junior partner VC investors. Her advice for an aspiring investor is reflective of her own experience trying to get into venture.

"Be shameless, but thoughtful. When I was getting started, I asked anyone I could for time and feedback as an opportunity to learn," said Brandt. "Many said no and I was always respectful of that. I remember everyone who said yes and am so appreciative that they gave me some of their time."

Patrick Chase, Redpoint Ventures

Chase got his start as a software engineer at LinkedIn before eventually becoming a partner at Redpoint Ventures, an early backer of Netflix, Sonos, and Justworks among many others.

His investments at Redpoint include SpotAI, Timescale, and Hex.

He says that he strives to differentiate himself from other investors by embracing empathy and by always being available to help his portfolio companies with any challenge that arises. "If it's 3:30 a.m. and there's something urgent that comes up, I want to be their first call," he says.

Vivian Cheng, CRV

Cheng, the daughter of immigrant small business owners, became enamored with technology in 2012 while living in San Francisco after college when she took her first Uber. She lived in the seedy Tenderloin district where she says Taxi's literally refused to stop, so she could see right away how game changing the new ride hailing app could be. "I became intrigued with tech as an equalizer," she said, "how it can impact consumers at a wide scale and help the underserved."

She became one of the first 200 employees at Uber, helping to scale the company into the household name it is today. She would go to serve as a venture investor at Cota Capital and Javelin Venture Partners where she backed companies such as Truebill, Current and Homeward.

Cheng is currently a principal at CRV, where she leverages her knack for storytelling and her belief in the transformative power of technology to help grow unicorn startups like DoorDash, Airtable, and Vercel.

Hui Cheng, Town Hall Ventures

Digital health investor Cheng has been with Town Hall Ventures since 2021 and was an early investor in Thyme Care, Connections Health, Zing Health, and Brave Health and a number of other healthcare startups. Before venture capital, she was a consultant at Bain and Company and worked in investment banking at Morgan Stanley.

For Cheng, it's imperative for aspiring VCs to have their own voice and opinions.

"Nine out of 10 times, that will help you discover what kind of investor you want to be, hone your sense of what funds and team you'd want to be part of (venture is as much about the people you work with as the companies you invest in!), and show how you can be valuable from day one," she told Business Insider.

Nicole Chung, Upfront Ventures

Chung joined the investment team at Upfront Ventures last year, after leading business operations and finance at Extend, a product protection solution. Previously she worked in operations at Salesforce and held management consulting roles in South Korea and Silicon Valley.

Chung says she looks for founders who are curious but also focused.

"Most entrepreneurs tend to be curious humans and have the ability to question the status quo and build their own solutions," Chung explains. "But being curious alone isn't enough. With so many distractions and responsibilities competing for our attention, the ability to harness deep focus and relentlessly prioritize can make or break the success of a new business."

Alysaa Co, Bain Capital

Co recommends that people aspiring to land a role in venture capital start to help founders they know while working in their current industries.

"Venture firms want to hire investors that can show they are already doing the job," she told Business Insider. "Whether it's entrepreneurial friends, colleagues that leave to start their own thing, or founders you've interviewed in the past, stay close to them and find ways that you can contribute to growing the business."

Originally from Toronto and now based in San Francisco, Co joined BCV in 2022 and focuses on early-stage application software and fintech startups. Her investments range from data-syncing platform Hightouch to private credit loan management company Finley.

Prior to BCV, Co was an analyst and associate at Inovia Capital and graduated from Western University in London, Ontario.

Tobi Coker, Felicis

A deal partner at Felicis, Coker focuses on backing startups in cloud infrastructure, developer tools, enterprise software, and biotech. At Felicis, he's helped back a number of startups including investments in Cellino, Meilisearch and Maze.design. This isn't Coker's first stint in venture. Previously, Coker was an investor at The Production Board, a Bay Area-based early-stage venture firm, where he helped found and seed six companies in life sciences and human health.

He's also an active member of BLCK VC, a community for Black venture investors

His advice for any aspiring investors is to "find a way to ingratiate yourself into the startup or technical community or moonlight or volunteer for a startup."

"Presuming you have the capital and risk appetite, find a startup that interests you and invest, Coker said. "Hopefully, your diligence as a prospective investor would be a microcosm of the work you would be doing at a venture capital firm.

Elias Davis, MS&AD Ventures

Davis is a third-generation entrepreneur, who says that starting companies is in his blood. Davis told Business Insider that his father was a physician-turned-entrepreneur, and his grandparents all started businesses and built them from the ground up. While in college, he started an online community for medical professionals called WeMedUp.com and realized that a path in technology made more sense for him than a career in medicine.

He joined Google after graduating as a product manager, and got exposure to the venture capital world there through founder and investor events.

After that experience, he knew he wanted to break into venture and ultimately got introduced to the founder of the New York-based investment firm BNSG Capital, which would later become Kli Capital. He spent over five years there before joining MS&AD Ventures earlier this year.

Davis truly lives and breathes venture, and that's his advice for any aspiring investor looking to break into the industry. "As Jason Shuman, close friend, and GP at Primary Venture Partners, has publicly said: Do the work consistently for a long time, and the right opportunity will present itself. That's how both of us broke into venture," Davis told Business Insider.

Danel Dayan, Battery Ventures

Dayan began his career advising on tech transactions at PwC and Deutsche Bank before joining Google as part of their corporate development team. He led and closed over 20 acquisitions and strategic investments for the tech giant with a total value of more than $2 billion.

Currently he's a principal at Battery Ventures where he invests in cloud infrastructure, developer tools, and cybersecurity. His current investments include Postman, Harness, and Matilion. He's also had successful exits from his investments in Confluent and JFog which both went public on Nasdaq, and Bionic Security which was acquired by Crowdstrike.

Elana Dickman, Red Beard Ventures

Dickman started her career as a consultant at KPMG and Deloitte before joining Red Beard last year to invest in Web3 and crypto startups. This year, she was promoted to partner and also co-founded Denarii Labs, a tokenomics accelerator.

"The most important quality in a founder is grit," Dickman said. "There is always going to be uncertainty, challenges, and frequent setbacks as an entrepreneur, but an individual's ability to persevere through difficult times will set them apart from others."

When she's not sourcing web3 and crypto deals, Dickman frequently hosts events in Los Angeles for founders and investors; Her pickleball tournaments are especially popular. Dickman also founded The Girls Table, a social media platform that amplifies entrepreneurs, creators, and investor's voices through a podcast, newsletter, events, and a private chat.

Anna Doherty, G9 Ventures

Former Morgan Stanley analyst Doherty joined G9 Ventures, the VC firm founded by Amy Griffin, in 2019 as the firm's first hire, rising to partner in 2021. A consumer focused firm, G9 has backed some of tech's hottest consumer brands including Bumble, On Running, Oura, Kindbody, Athletic Greens, Julie, and Spanx. Doherty says that one of the most important qualities she looks for in a founder is self-awareness.

Doherty, who was the captain of Princeton's varsity lacrosse team, admits that she's competitive, more recently delving into the pickleball craze.

Doherty advises anyone who wants to get into investing to start "tracking companies that have the potential to be great outcomes."

"Use your network, online channels, and IRL communities to begin to get in the flow of meeting founders and finding deals. You will really differentiate yourself in an interview or when interacting with other investors and founders if you are already starting to see opportunities," she added.

Faraz Fatemi, Lightspeed Venture Partners

Fatemi joined Lightspeed in early 2022 as the firm's first LA-based investing partner after operating roles at Clubhouse and Wave.tv. Focusing on consumer platforms, he has helped lead the firm's investments in beehiv, a newsletter platform, and Gardens, a remote games studio.

He says he looks for founders with "extreme intensity" and a "bias to action."

"That usually manifests in terms of rapid product iteration and willingness to do whatever it takes," he said.

Fatemi prides himself on being a super connector in the LA tech scene; He started Lightspeed's LA celebrity + founder salon dinner series that has featured glitzy guests like Jessica Alba, John Legend, and Gwyneth Paltrow.

Aaron Fleishman, Tola Capital

Fleishman is a partner at the Seattle-based venture fund Tola Capital, where he looks for investments in enterprise software, AI, and blockchain technology.

In 2023 alone, he led the fund's investments in a $10 million Series A round for London-based AI Ethics and Governance platform Holistic AI, a $13.5 million Series A round for the cyber security startup Zilla Security, and a $5.25 million seed round into ESGFlo, an ESG data infrastructure startup.

Before joining Tola Capital, he worked in M&A at Microsoft, and was an e-commerce startup founder himself.

Fleishman likes founders who are persistent and have the ability to adapt during difficult periods, he told Business Insider. "Markets change. Competitive dynamics change. The best founders can navigate all of that and find a path to success," he said.

Luci Fonseca, Base10 Partners

As a first generation college student at Yale that hailed from the small African island nation Cabo Verde, Luci Fonseca had never heard of venture capital. She ended up following the advice of the college's career center and went into investment banking at Goldman Sachs, before taking roles in venture philanthropy and government. She was economic advisor for the office of the prime minister of Cabo Verde where she led work on the country's strategic plan to transform its economy to be more digital and embrace more technology.

Fonseca told Business Insider that these experiences helped her realize that she wanted to "move massive amounts of capital in ways that created transformative impact."

In 2015, she moved to the Bay Area to pursue an MBA at Stanford University to ultimately get into venture capital and impact investing. She later started the impact fund at Salesforce Ventures, and led McKinsey's work on JP Morgan's $30 billion racial equity commitment. She now leads investing for the growth-stage fund at Base10 Partners, backing startups like Notion, Stripe and Oyster.

Javier Grevely, Wellington Management

Grevely is a venture capital investor at Wellington Management, an investment firm that manages $1 trillion for pensions, endowments and foundations, insurers, and family offices. In that role, he seeks to partner with gritty founders building across software and consumer tech.

Grevely believes that storytelling is one of the most important skills a founder can have, especially when funding is harder to come by. "At the earliest stages, investors are making bets on team, mission, and vision," he said. "The ability to craft an authentic story that depicts what you are building and why, along with how that's expected to evolve, is a solid skill set to have."

Outside of his day job, Grevely is a cofounder and co-chair of the Boston chapter of BLCK VC, a nonprofit dedicated to amplifying the success of Black investors.

Amanda Herson, Founder Collective

Herson invests in the "weird and wonderful" as a general partner at Founder Collective, a venture firm that invests across sectors at the earliest stages of a company.

She joined the firm as a principal in the beginning of 2020, leaving behind a senior leadership role at one of Africa's largest outdoor equipment and apparel retailers, where she grew the online business.

The pandemic accelerated Herson's crash course in venture. Since then, she's helped lead investments in Odyssey, a climate-tech startup focused on emerging markets; Trek Health, which makes payments and insurance-workflow software for mental health provider groups; and Draftboard, a twist on the online jobs site. In 2022, Founder Collective promoted her to partner.

Vivien Ho, Pear VC

Ho has been an investor at Pear Ventures since 2019 and was promoted to partner earlier this year. Based in San Francisco, she focuses broadly on "health" investing, an area that, for her, encompasses human, planet and financial health. Ho also founded Pear's Female Founders Circle, a 10-week program for female technical founders that she leads twice a year.

Ho's investments include electronic health record provider Osmind and eco-friendly production company Hertha Metals.

Her best advice for aspiring investors? Give before you take.

"Offer your superpower - whether it's community building, content, GTM prowess, or your unique network to founders or other investors," she told Business insider.

Brandon Hoffman, Sunset Ventures

Hoffman began his career as a bank teller in 2006 at a local bank in downtown Anaheim. He used his wages to pay for college, and eventually built a music production studio and eventually worked in equity research at Morgan Stanley. Hoffman got the startup investing bug and joined Samsung's innovation and VC firm, Samsung Next, where helped lead investments in Ready Player Me, Yuga Labs, and Wonder Dynamics.

Last year, Hoffman broke out on his own with Sunset Ventures, an early stage fund that got the backing of Bank of America and Alexis Ohanian's Seven Seven Six.

Hoffman says that the best way to break into investing is by getting "hands-on experience doing the work of a VC."

"Start by joining VC communities and programs that offer intern, fellow, or scout opportunities," said Hoffman. He said he started angel investing with small amounts on AngelList and then built an angel community. He also volunteered as an intern at Harlem Capital in 2018 when their first fund was being launched.

Fiona Huang, DCM

Huang is the newest partner — and the only female checkwriter — at DCM, an early-stage venture firm with more than $4 billion under management. She's working to give the firm a foothold in Asia, sourcing investments in China and Southeast Asia, with a sector focus on consumer applications, online marketplaces, artificial intelligence, and software.

Huang knows the real work begins after an investment's been made. This year, she advised the Chinese online-learning company Quantasing on the road to becoming a publicly traded foreign entity on an American stock exchange, and worked with Chinese logistics startup YunQuNa, last valued at $1 billion in 2021, to make key hires and expand around the world.

Huang, who lives in Beijing, began her career at the strategic consultancy Roland Berger, and delved deeper into venture capital at Sequoia Capital and Tencent Investment.

Nmachi Jidenma, General Catalyst

Jidenma is a vice president on the investment team at General Catalyst, where she backs founders that are working to build AI, fintech, healthtech, and enterprise startups.

Since joining the firm at the end of 2022, she has spearheaded the development of the firm's $800 million Creation fund, where investors on the team take a hands-on approach to building startups alongside founders from the earliest days of a company's founding.

Before joining General Catalyst, Jidenma cut her teeth as a senior director at Sequoia Capital, and as a global strategy and business development lead for WhatsApp.

Jidenma shared with Business Insider that her best advice for aspiring venture capital investors is to "make an effort to understand what great looks like" in a founding team.

"There are lots of good people and ideas out there but only very few are truly exceptional," she said.

Prateek Joshi, Moxxie Ventures

Joshi's advice for any would-be investors is to become "spectacularly good at a specific thing." For Joshi, an investor at seed stage VC firm Moxxie Ventures, that thing is machine learning. A former machine learning software engineer at Nvidia, Joshi has published 13 books on Machine Learning, hosts a podcast, Infinite Machine Learning, and writes a newsletter on the intersection of AI and startups.

He was previously the founder/CEO of Plutoshift, a machine learning platform for physical infrastructure.

Joshi joined Moxxie, which has backed Carta, Certn, Rewind AI, Veho, Heirloom Carbon, August Health, earlier this year after founding a machine learning startup, Plutoshift. He says he looks for founders "who spike in a specific dimension and succeed in changing the landscape of my mind."

Ashish Kakran, Thomvest Ventures

Before turning his eye to investing, Kakran cut his teeth in the early days of cloud computing revolution when, in 2008, he was tasked with building scalable apps on AWS. This allowed him to hone his technical skills on some of the industry's most challenging problems. Today he uses that technical know-how to advise early-stage data, machine learning, and cybersecurity startups.

Kakran was an early investor in Cohere (one of the few rivals to OpenAI in the area of foundational AI models) as well as Harness, a maker of software development tools. In addition to being one of the few VCs with a hardcore technical background, Kakran is also a natural community builder who enjoys bringing builders together for dinners, talks, and events.

Neil Kapur, TTV Capital

Kapur has spent over a decade in the finance industry, both as an operator and investor. He started out in investment banking and consulting, which sparked an interest in the payments ecosystem. That led to him working at two small startups developing payments infrastructure and lending, before landing at Google, where he said he launched and scaled Google Pay in the US and abroad.

Kapur got his start in venture at B Capital as a principal and then went on to join TTV Capital in 2021, where he now invests in early-stage fintech startups.

"We back founders who are evangelists of their business," Kapur said, and he typically looks for founders that can speak about high-level strategic plans, but can also get into the weeds of their company, when they need to.

Boyoung Kim, Griffin Gaming Partners

Kim grew up in South Korea and then worked as an equity-research analyst for Bloomberg and Citi in New York. She is an avid gamer, and joined Griffin Gaming Partners last year, which is the world's largest gaming-focused firm, with more than a billion dollars under management.

"As uncertainty increases across the space, we have maintained our conviction and have continued to be one of the most active investors in games," Kim said. "We've partnered with founders known for creating billion-dollar franchises, and that's a journey I'm proud to be a part of."

Kim helped lead Griffin's first investment in South Korea in a gaming platform called Black Storm that offers MMOARPG, or massively multiplayer online role-playing game. She also helped lead the firm's investment in Nexus.gg, a creator infrastructure allowing developers and publishers to launch their own support-a-creator programs.

Christine Kim, Greylock

Fresh out of Dartmouth College with a computer science degree, Kim landed at Uber as an engineer. She spent over five years there in engineering and worked on products across the company's Rides and Eats units. Kim started angel investing and said she became more curious about the investing world.

She initially connected with Greylock to consult on a few operating roles she was considering at the time, but was "compelled to join the firm after spending time with the incredibly smart people here." Now as a principal there, she focuses on early-stage vertical software, marketplaces and consumer startups.

At the early-stage, she looks for founders with "velocity and hustle." "I think the combination of these two elements can beat being right, smart, or first," Kim told Business Insider. "People often forget that Uber was not the first product in market, but we moved the fastest."

Hyun Koo, March Capital

Hyun Koo grew up in Seoul, Singapore, and New York and now lives in Santa Monica, where he is one of the youngest partners at March Capital. Koo primarily invests in AI applications and vertical SaaS.

"My unique upbringing helped me become a well-rounded person with a healthy mix of empathy, analytical skills, and creativity," Koo said. "VC is a people's business, and I find my 'superpower' to be connecting with founders of all backgrounds, personalities, and skills."

Koo recently helped lead the firm's $175 million Series C funding round for Nile, a networking-as-a-service startup.

Prior to March Capital, Koo spent five years at Allen & Company, a boutique investment bank focusing on media and tech.

Tony Liu, Costanoa Ventures

Before becoming a VC in 2019, Tony Liu held product management and data science roles at Databricks, Koalas, Turo, and Amazon. He was promoted to partner at Costanoa Ventures last year and has helped lead the firm's investments in Rerun, a data visualization infrastructures startup, and Earthmover, a data platform for geospatial and climate data.

Liu says his favorite part about his job is helping first time founders navigate through murky waters.

"To do so, I have also had to learn a lot and constantly revisit my perspectives and assumptions," Liu said. "There are so many evolving macro dynamics at play and so much noise from news and social media. It's a confusing time and takes a lot of work to stay focused."

Max Luo, Acme Capital

After a stint advising on M&A and product strategy at McKinsey, Luo worked at X, Google's "moonshot factory" which has built everything from self-driving cars to internet balloons.

For the past 3 years he's served as investment associate and chief of staff at Acme Capital, an early stage venture capital fund that was an early backer of Uber, SpaceX, and Airbnb among other household names. Luo investments at Acme so far have included Pier Finance, Partiful, and Forma.ai.

Ihar Mahaniok, Geek Ventures

Growing up in Belarus and then moving to the United States, Mahaniok saw how difficult it is for immigrant entrepreneurs to make connections within the U.S. venture capital ecosystem. He founded Geek Ventures in 2021 to drive equal opportunity for immigrant entrepreneurs looking to enter the U.S. market after working for more than 20 years as a software engineer for companies like Google, Facebook, and WeWork.

As an angel investor, he had a proven track record with successful seed investments in future unicorns like People.ai, Airbyte, and Instacart, which went public this September.

In June, he closed his inaugural oversubscribed seed fund for Geek Ventures, which totaled $23 million, an impressive feat in this current venture market. Some of his more recent successful deals skew towards AI, such as Spice.AI and ShapesXR, which have already raised follow-on rounds.

When looking for pre-seed and seed stage startups to back, Mahaniok prioritizes investing in "founders who, while being exceptional themselves, actively seek and possess the ability to hire individuals smarter and more skilled than themselves," he said.

Nurzhas Makishev, NKM Capital

A former software engineer and MIT and Harvard graduate, Makishev started making angel investments while working at tech giants like Google and Oracle. Nurzhas' notable early-stage investments include Truebill (acquired by Rocket Money for $1.3 billion), Relativity Space, Robinhood, and Ripple.

At NKM Capital, a San Francisco-based venture capital firm he founded, Makishev has doubled down on backing Ai startups. The firm has invested in seed rounds of speech to text AI company Deepgram LLM-powered search startup and AI software development company Mutable.ai, among others.

Makishev looks for founders and startups that are "mission-driven with deep domain expertise and non-consensus elaborate vision, versus opportunistic mercenary-driven" founders.

​​As for advice for anyone looking to break into venture, Makishev said, "Do extensive homework on markets and technologies you have experience in or passionate about; talk to founders, investors, and industry/academia leaders at the frontier; develop deep insights into value creation and technology inflections; and build track record by risking your time and capital helping smartest founders whose high conviction views you understand."

Emily Man, Primary

By the time Man joined Primary as a partner this past summer, she had already amassed a stable of startup unicorns with investments in fintechs like Ramp, MX, and Floqast.

Previously, she was an investor at Redpoint, where she spent almost three years investing across early and growth stage companies. In her first partner role, Man will double down on fintech investing and also expand her scope of investments to include applications and industry-specific vertical software.

The fintech industry has been rocked by hiked interest rates, bank failures, and a dearth of venture capital funding over the past 18 months. Undeterred, Man says she's long on fintech investing, writing in a LinkedIn post, "There has never been a time in my investing career more exciting for early stage fintech than right now and the problems are as unsolved as ever."

Mike Marg, Craft Ventures

Marg cut his teeth developing go-to-market strategies as an operator at Dropbox and Slack. Today he brings his firsthand experience to his role as an investor at Craft Ventures where he invests in and advises enterprise SaaS startups like Sentilink, FingerprintJS, and People Data Labs.

In addition to his role on multiple company boards, Marg writes a newsletter, EarlyGTM, where he shares his insights on everything from negotiating SaaS contracts to crafting sales emails.

Kleida Martiro, Glasswing Ventures

Martiro has been investing in artificial intelligence long before it became the only sector investors were still funding in a downturn. She's a partner at Glasswing Ventures, an early-stage venture firm dedicated to data and security startups, where she specializes in data science and product management. And in four years, she's climbed the ranks from associate to partner.

Before joining Glasswing in 2019, she led the machine learning efforts as head of data science at SocialFlow, a company that helped media publishers optimize where and when they post.

Outside of her day job, Martiro sits on the steering committee of the Boston chapter of All Raise, a nonprofit focused on amplifying the success of women in venture capital.

Ahmed Mirza, Techstars

Mirza says there's two tracks in his career that have led to where he is now. First, he did a "tour of duty" in the investment banking world, starting at Merrill Lynch; then DC Advisory, formerly known as Signal Hill Capital; and then in corporate development at Tungsten. The second track was trying his hand as a startup founder. He launched his first startup in the fitness space called Elevate, but that company didn't work out.

While getting his MBA at the UCLA Anderson School of Management, he started another company called Blueprint, which developed a presentation software for remote teams. After that, Mirza said he had a "soft landing" and became an entrepreneur-in-residence at several venture studios in Los Angeles, taking seven companies from initial concept to its first fundraise in just three years.

Now at Techstars, he coaches and mentors entrepreneurs and invests in pre-seed startups. During a 13-week accelerator program, Mirza helps founders accelerate their companies as much as possible. "That means that the founders I invest in need to have a combination of IQ, EQ, and GSDQ (Get Sh!t Done quotient), " he said. "The combination of these quotients equates to coachability in my mind. Founders need to be coachable."

Devon Morris, Group 11

Morris is a partner at Group 11, an US/Israeli-focused fintech-focused venture firm, where he is responsible for overseeing deal structuring, due diligence, and portfolio management. He is also a board observer for Lili and helped lead the firm's investment in Sunbit, a buy now, pay later technology startup.

"We love the founders and the team," Morris said. "It's an incredibly needed product in the market, and customers love them."

As a VC, Morris prides himself on rolling up his sleeves.

"There is no shortage of people with good ideas," Morris said. "There is an extreme shortage of qualified people who are willing to put in the work, lead others, and see things to completion. I've always seen myself in this light and VC, startups, and any business can use more of these types of people."

Demi Obayomi, Sapphire Ventures

In three years at Sapphire Ventures, Obayomi has played a key role in investments worth a combined $300 million. His sweet spot: cloud software and vertical software, which refers to applications that target specific industries. He's also a board observer supporting investments in chatbot developer yellow.ai, sales pipeline startup Qualified, and intranet builder Simpplr.

Obayomi, who joined Sapphire in 2021, describes himself as a "thematic investor," having written weighty tomes on categories for investment such as human resources, the supply chain, and video intelligence, as well as on the stages and secrets of company-building.

Outside of his day job, Obayomi keeps ties to his alma mater, Johns Hopkins University, as an advisor to A-Level Capital, the student-run venture fund he cofounded as a student.

Claire Pan, Inspired Capital

Pan is an early-stage investor at Inspired Capital, where she focuses on backing developer tools, AI and machine learning, and B2B SaaS startups.

Prior to Inspired Capital, Claire was an associate at Insight Partners, and worked at The World Bank.

Claire has focused on building out NYC's AI community, through intimate dinners and events. Most recently, Claire led a 100-plus person AI Demo Day that included presentations from MosaicML, Arthur AI, Runhouse, Arize AI, and Baseten. She also helped to build 20/20 Vision Capital, a fund of funds vehicle backing early-stage venture funds led by diverse GPs.

Ashley Paston, Meritech Capital

Paston was the first woman hired as partner at Meritech Capital, a growth-stage venture firm with $5 billion under management, when she joined in 2022.

Based in San Francisco, she invests in her wheelhouse of fintech, enterprise software, and infrastructure, and most recently led an investment in Pigment, a company that's taking on Salesforce and Oracle in creating products for large companies that need to forecast, budget, and build comprehensive reports.

Before joining Meritech, Paston spent four years at Bain Capital Ventures, where she was responsible for sourcing and executing early-stage and growth-equity investments in companies such as SmartRent and ACV Auctions, which both went public in the good times of 2021.

Outside of her day job, Paston sits on the Penn Museum's Young Alumni Council.

Mariano Payano, Iconiq Growth

This is Payano's second stint at Iconiq-he rejoined the firm in 2022 after getting an MBA from Stanford's graduate school of business. Payano focuses primarily on investments in enterprise infrastructure, AI and machine learning and security software, and has led investments in Pinecone, Netskope, Twistlock, Highspot, Dialpad, BambooHR and Guild Education. Prior to ICONIQ Growth, Mariano was part of the technology investment banking group at Morgan Stanley in New York.

Payano advises those interested in venture to "take the time to be a perpetual student of the craft and the industries you wish to invest in, ensuring you have a good pulse and perspective on the latest trends and developments."

"Don't be afraid to expand your appetite for risk, frequently ask yourself not just what can go wrong but, more importantly, what can go right. Lastly, network relentlessly – talk to everyone who participates in the ecosystem: investors, advisors, engineers, product managers, customers, industry experts, and more. You'd be surprised by what you can learn and at the doors that can open just by creating meaningful connections," Payano said.

Chas Pulido, Alix Ventures

Before founding the life science-focused venture firm Alix Ventures, Pulido started his own company, Pareto Life Sciences, a stem cell marketplace startup that was part of Y Combinator's summer 2018 batch. He then moved on to work at the healthtech startup Clara Health before jumping into venture as an entrepreneur-in-residence at Liquid 2 Ventures, the firm founded by NFL Hall of Fame quarterback Joe Montana.

After that experience, he decided to start Alix Ventures, a life sciences firm that focuses on backing early-stage startups "engineering biology to drive radical advances in human health," according to his LinkedIn profile. The main quality Pulido looks for in potential founders to invest in is resilience and the "continued ability to weather constantly changing circumstances" and get back up, he told Business Insider.

Ali Rohde, Outset Capital

As the managing partner of Outset Capital, Ali Rohde successfully raised her $10 million debut fund from prominent tech players and leaders in the AI space, including Quora's Adam D'Angelo, Dropbox's Drew Houston and Arash Ferdowsi, and Google DeepMind's Dorothy Chou.

Rohde has also garnered a following for her "Thursday night in AI'' fireside chats, which she hosts live in San Francisco and live streams online. Past guests of hers include NVIDIA's Jim Fan, Notion's Linus Lee, and Replit's Amjad Masad.. The event series has been dubbed as "one of the best signals'' in the AI community and receives over 400 applications per event, said VC Jay Rughani.

Some of Rohde's most recent highlights include a seed investment in the home-swapping startup Kindred, a check into the dev tools startup Reflex, and the buzzy AI for biotech startup Meliora.

Rohde's best advice for aspiring startup investors? "Just start doing the work," she says.

"Go find exciting companies. Then tell the funds you're interested in working for what companies you've found, and why they should pay attention."

Luna Schmid, GV

Luna Schmid has loved technology since she was a little kid growing up in Canada, after her parents moved there to pursue their master's degrees in electrical engineering and computer science.

Now, living in London, Schmid has turned that passion for cutting-edge tech into a promising career as an investor. She worked in investment banking before jumping into venture capital at Summit Partners and Softbank's Vision Fund. These days, as a partner at GV, she's searching for the hottest deals in automation, AI tools, and AI infrastructure.

This spring, Schmid led carbon removal startup Cur8's $6.5 million pre-seed round for GV.

Jessie Sheff, Insight Partners

Sheff has only been at Insight Partners for two years, but so far during her time there she has helped to land 13 deals for the firm, including deploying funds into Abnormal Security's $210 Series C round and Builder.ai's $100 million Series C and $250 million Series D rounds.

She invests primarily in vertical software and infrastructure, including data engineering, AI, and cybersecurity startups.

Before joining Insight Partners, Sheff was an investor at Summit Partners from 2017 to 2021.

KJ Sidberry, GV

KJ Sidberry jumpstarted his investing career in management consulting and firms like Accenture and Boston Consulting Group, which he says nurtured his "obsession in understanding how businesses worked, and identifying solutions to meet their needs."

But Sidberry soon realized that he wanted to work with younger companies driving innovation, and set out to find a venture fund that fit his wants: a focus on early-stage, comprised of a team that "felt like the future of the industry vs. the past," and one that "deeply prioritized the human condition and sought to empower businesses that worked to address all the different dimensions of life, from education to healthcare and beyond."

He ultimately found a fit at the consumer-focused, San Francisco-based firm, Forerunner Ventures, where he spent the last six years. And just this year, he joined the investing team at GV.

Parul Singh, Initialized Capital

Singh is a partner at Initialized Capital, an early-stage venture firm with $3.2 billion under management, where she spearheads investments in software, data, and healthtech startups.

Singh believes we're on the cusp of a digital transformation in healthcare, in which the world will see technologies that have been in development for decades, ranging from AI to IVF to wearables, incorporated into patient care in new and meaningful ways. She's working to ensure Initialized has a place in the revolution — and so far, she's invested in egg-freezing startup Cofertility, diagnostics company Glass Health, and healthcare planning system Radial Analytics.

Before joining Initialized, she was an investor at Founder Collective, where she covered Boston and New York, evaluated more than 3,000 companies, and wrote some prolific blogs.

Mahati Sridhar, Revolution

Sridhar has been at Revolution since 2021, where she focuses on climate tech and the care economy. Before joining the Washington DC-based fund, she was an associate at Bull City Venture Fund in Durham, North Carolina, which specializes in early-stage software and internet companies based primarily in the mid-Atlantic and Southeast US.

Given her background, it's no surprise that Sridhar looks for founders who are using their unique geography to their advantage.

"At Rise of the Rest, we take founder-market fit one step further by factoring geography into the equation," she told Business Insider. "In my experience investing beyond Silicon Valley, New York, and Boston, founders leveraging regional strengths — from rich talent pools to a legacy corporate presence to industry-specific infrastructure — are always compelling."

Tara Stokes, Point72 Ventures

Stokes joined Point72 Ventures in 2020 as a vice president, and was promoted to principal in 2022. She focuses on deep tech and artificial intelligence investing, and she's led deals in hot startups like Dashworks and Tomato AI. Before Point72, Stokes was an associate in the Investment Banking Division at Morgan Stanley, and was a summer associate at Bain and Company.

This fall, she led a $10.6 million seed round in the buzzy AI cybersecurity startup NexusFlow.

Based in New York, she is an active blogger for Point72 Ventures, and has written multiple posts mapping out market trends in AI.

Stokes' best advice for those wanting to break into venture is to be "gritty, but also strategic," she said. "Ventures is a narratives business. Reflect on your experiences and be ready to pitch why you're uniquely positioned to add value."

Jake Storm, Felicis

Storm began his venture career at IVP before moving to Felicis in 2021 where his investments have included Oso, Tines, and Resourcely. This year, Storm was promoted to partner. Storm says he tries to get teams out of the office as much as possible.

"Over the past 12 months I've taken groups of startups and buyers to skydive, race Ferraris, and attend boat outings," Storm said. "Barriers are demolished when you're in nature, doing fun things with good people."

"Every single day my sole focus is to bring 110% in unconventional and scrappy hustle, creativity, and true partnership to these relationships," Storm added.

Adina Tecklu, Khosla Ventures

Tecklu was promoted to partner at Khosla Ventures in 2023 and has been at the firm since 2020. She currently leads the firms' investments in SaaS and fintech businesses and has invested in startups including trucking and logistics operator CloudTrucks, physical therapy provider Limbic, and Web3 corporate card Rain.

She also sourced and led the firm's investments in Aven, Luzia, and Tortus. She works closely at the board level with the firm's investments in Homebase, Nomba, Ori, and Overture, among others.

Prior to joining Khosla, Tecklu built Caanan Partners' consumer seed practice, Beta, and also held roles at Zenefits and Oracle. She graduated from Stanford University.

Janelle Teng, Bessemer Venture Partners

Since joining Bessemer Venture Partners in 2020, Teng focuses primarily on growth-stage investments in cloud and enterprise software, including AI startups in these sectors.

She also specializes in product strategy for companies looking to scale much larger. She was even featured in a Harvard Business School case study, as an alum herself, written on Bessemer's growth investing practice called Century Capital.

Most recently, Teng led the due diligence process for two hot deals for Bessemer's portfolio earlier this year: Coactive AI's $14 million combined seed and series AI round and the cloud platform startup Render's $50 million Series B round.

As a former product manager for SalesforceIQ, Teng likes working with founders who are "bold product visionaries," she told Business Insider, "from technical founders who have pioneered completely new categories of products based on their ground-breaking PhD research, to others with acute customer empathy and a keen eye for design," she said.

Evaline Tsai, Gigascale Capital

Tsai has been a principal at Gigascale Capital since the climate investment firm launched earlier this year. She focuses on energy generation, green chemicals, and enteric emissions reduction, and in 2019 earned her Ph.D. in chemical engineering and biotechnology from the University of Cambridge.

Tsai is currently based out of Palo Alto, and recommends that aspiring investors get operator or founder experience before moving over to VC.

"When you have that operating experience as an investor, you can connect with and empathize with the founders you work with in a different way," she told Insider. "Operating experience also gives you the lens through which to ask better diligence questions and to partner with founders to help them get stuff done because you better understand how startups operate themselves."

Ary Vaidya, Kyber Knight Capital

Vaida has had a standout year as a founding member of the investment team at Kyber Knight Capital, a new early stage fund which launched a sizable $120 million backed by executives at Google, Airbnb, and Zynga, among others.

Vaidya leads the AI and commerce investing for the fund, and looks to back pre-seed and seed stage startups in those fields. Just this year, he's participated in over 10 of the fund's investments, including the legal startup Paxton AI's $6 million seed round earlier this fall.

Vaidya is on the hunt for founders that can grow and develop their startup's core product quickly once their product is in the hands of their customers, he said, noting that they will likely have "strong competitive advantages in the years to come."

Justin Wexler, WndrCo

A graduate of MIT's Sloan School of Management, Wexler previously worked at powerhouse growth equity firm TCV where he advised companies like Zillow and Datto. He later served as director of corporate development for unicorn cybersecurity firm Pango, backed by Accel and General Catalyst among others.

Today he's a vice president at WndrCo, the venture firm co-founded by Hollywood legend Jeffrey Katzenberg. While consumer facing AI applications like ChatGPT may be getting the lion's share of the attention, Wexler oversees go-to-market strategy for the enterprise focused startups that are helping transform the Fortune 500.

He serves on the board of generative AI startups Writer, Netomi, and Sparrow and also works closely with productivity software makers like Deel, Airtable, and Orby, which enjoys the backing of NEA and Pear VC.

Sean Whitney, Craft Ventures

Whitney differs from pretty much every venture capitalist in that before his time as an investor he was a professional hockey player. The skills he learned as an operator as well as on the ice have informed his approach to investing at Craft Ventures.

He also leverages the experience he earned as one of the first finance hires at decacorn design startup Figma. He spent nearly four years at the company helping to lead strategy around pricing, financial forecasting, and capital raising. As a principal at Craft, Whitney focuses on enterprise SaaS, developer tools, and APIs and has backed startups such Replit, Dock, and Modyfi.

Justin Williams, Seae Ventures

Before breaking into the venture-capital industry, Williams spent two years as an analyst at the investment bank, Houlihan Lokey, and then a stint at the company, Alexion Pharmaceuticals. Williams spent two years as an associate biotech investor at Pathway Bioventures, where he focused on biotech startups, particularly early-stage therapeutic companies with a focus on genetic medicine. Earlier this year, he joined Seae Ventures where he focuses on healthcare technology. One of the key things he looks for in a startup founder is perseverance and the ability to pivot and adapt based on customer needs and market demands.

Williams told Business Insider that he lives by this quote: "Be yourself so the people who are looking for you can find you." And he firmly believes that in the world of venture, "authenticity is your greatest asset," and shared that advice for aspiring investors trying to break into the industry.

Daisy Wolf, a16z

New York-based Daisy Wolf has been a partner at a16z since 2021 and focuses on the healthcare sector. Her investments include Fortuna Health, a TurboTax for Medicaid, and Pomelo Care, which offers virtual maternal health.

Prior to joining a16z, Wolf cut her teeth at January Ventures and co-founded allergy startup Crux Health. When assessing founders, Wolf says she looks for people who value speed as well as storytelling ability.

"Building a company is full of roadblocks and obstacles that can slow you down; I'm always impressed by founders who seem to defy laws of physics when it comes to velocity," she told Insider. "The ability to communicate in a clear and compelling manner is important in selling customers, employees, and investors."

Sarah Wu, Emerson Collective

Wu is an AI and healthcare investor with a deep understanding of the technology behind the companies she backs: she holds dual degrees in computer science and a baseline understanding of medical concepts from her parents, who were cancer research scientists at the National Institute of Health and the National Cancer Institute.

Throughout her investing career, she's used her computer science background to connect with the engineers behind some of the hottest developments in AI technology.

As a partner at Emerson Collective, she's led deals in startups like Guild Education, Brightwheel, and Lyra Health, and several stealth AI deals, she said. Before that, she led over 10 investments at Sapphire Ventures.

Her best advice to those looking to break into venture capital is to "Find a niche that is authentic to who you are," she told Business Insider. "The more specific you can be, the better."

Amber Yang, Bloomberg Beta

Yang was hired at Bloomberg Beta while she was still getting her master's degree at Stanford in computer science and has helped lead the firm's investments in Chroma, Embra, Poly, and Syft.

"When I was in school, I started a company using AI to predict space debris collisions with satellites," Yang said. "My technical expertise gives me a unique angle to doing diligence on AI startups, and it also gives me an edge to win deals, especially technical ones."

Yang also has a knack for branding, as she is credited with popularizing the term "Cerebral Valley," to describe an area in San Francisco considered an up and coming hotbed of generative AI.

Yang says she looks for founders who have black and white thinking when it comes to their vision of the future with the ability to adjust over time as new data presents itself.

"Great founders need to be uncompromising in what they believe when most people can be easily swayed," Yang said.

Russell Yue, Tru Arrow Partners

Before joining the team Tru Arrow Partners as vice president, Yue saw an impressive trajectory for his investments. His deals during his days at H.I.G. Capital includes the $9.5 billion unicorn ServiceTitan and the $2.6 billion cloud computing juggernaut JumpCloud.

Startups that he's backed since joining the team at Tru Arrow are equally as impressive, including notable deals in AI. He made a smart bet on MosaicML, which was acquired by DataBricks earlier this year for $1.6 billion.

Yue's best advice for aspiring startup investors? "Develop deep expertise & networks in a few sectors and find investors & firms that share a similar thesis on those areas," he shared with Business Insider.

Angela Zhu, IVP

Growing up Zhu split her time between the US and China. She says that this collision of different contexts allowed her to see firsthand the seismic impact technology can have on people's lives. Her investments at IVP have included Volt, Papaya Global, and Deputy.

As an investor, she looks for businesses that tackle big problems and address major pain points in ways that have the potential to fundamentally upend whole industries. "I'm always on the lookout for founders who are building companies with the potential to become generational businesses," she said. She says she always encourages the entrepreneurs she works with to think deeper about the opportunities before them.
