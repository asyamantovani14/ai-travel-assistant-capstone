Linux creator Linus Torvalds cancelled himself to become a nicer person — now he needs to bring talent back to the world's most important software

 Rosalie Chan
2021-06-29T11:59:44Z

The list of scathing messages that Linus Torvalds has written over the years is so rich and colorful that there's an entire subreddit devoted to the topic. 

"Mauro, SHUT THE F--- UP!" Torvalds, the famed creator of the Linux operating system, wrote publicly to a software developer in 2012, calling the hapless recipient's opinion "garbage and idiocy."

"The above code is s---, and it generates s--- code," Torvalds rebuked a different colleague working on Linux, while blasting the work of yet another because "I expect *some* quality control, and this piece-of-s--- driver has clearly seen none at all."

He's attacked tech giants like Intel, wondering whether the company is "committed to selling you s--- forever and ever, and never fixing anything?" When the graphics chipmaker Nvidia ran afoul of Torvalds one time, he said simply: "f--- you."

As Torvalds labored over the wording of an important announcement in 2018 though, he was gripped with doubt and self-consciousness. Nearly three decades after creating the software that made him famous, revered and feared, Torvalds — on the advice of a few close friends — was temporarily stepping away to "get help on how to behave differently."

"I am not an emotionally empathetic kind of person," Torvalds would acknowledge in the 989-word public apology.

The news that Torvalds was taking a timeout to work on being a nicer person caused quite a stir in the programming world, where the Finnish coder is an iconic figure who oversees a realm of technology so wide-reaching it's difficult to overstate.

In tech, Linux is like the air we breathe: It's free and mostly invisible to the average person, but major technologies and corporations can't run without it. 

Linux software underpins Android phones and the displays in Tesla cars. It powers the vast majority of the world's fastest supercomputers, stock-trading software, big data projects, AI apps, nuclear submarines, the US Postal Service, autonomous driving, banks, airlines, and cloud computing. It's even being used on Mars. 

Not only that, but Linux has also spawned a community of more than 10,000 developers who have devoted significant chunks of their lives, often as unpaid volunteers, to enhance, expand and maintain the software's capabilities. The latest release of Linux included changes in 12,000 files and over a million lines of code. Almost 2,000 developers were involved.

Torvalds' 2018 hiatus turned out to have a minimal impact on this hive of activity. After one month he was back at his keyboard ("Boy, was I bored," he wrote. "Linux is what I do."). But the episode exposed a flaw in Linux that runs deeper than Torvalds' day-to-day demeanor: Linux is ubiquitous, but the community of people who support Linux is aging. A steep learning curve and well-documented incidents of verbal harassment are not exactly a great pitch for recruiting young programmers.

At 51 years old, Torvalds is one of the last of a generation of "brilliant jerks," whose volatile mix of brains, vision, ego, and tantrums shaped a tech industry that remains predominantly white and male. Critics are skeptical that someone like Torvalds could experience a profound personal transformation at this stage in their life, and he doesn't disagree. Whether he can reprogram the sprawling and chaotic Linux community he created is another matter. 

When I contacted Torvalds over email, he had just gotten his power and internet back after an ice storm in Portland, Oregon, where he lives. I asked him whether he was available for a video interview, and he replied that, "I absolutely abhor video chats in all forms, and these days obviously face-to-face isn't an option either." 

I proposed a phone interview instead. "Heh, no, my abhorrence covers phone calls too. Nasty things, phones. Except as quick email devices."

Email it would be.

Like most people in the tech industry during the pandemic, Torvalds works from home. But he's been doing it for decades, often in a bathrobe and at a standing desk equipped with a treadmill that runs at a constant 1 mph pace (he calls it the "zombie-shuffling desk").

With his trademark round, wire-frame glasses and side-parted salt-and-pepper hair, Torvalds' look hasn't changed much over the years. His preference for casual, comfy attire extends even to public events. When Boas Betzler, the global architecture guild leader at IBM, met Torvalds at the Santa Clara, California, Marriott, he was eager to meet the "hero of my youth." He and two other IBM colleagues wore suits and prepared a conference room with some drinks. When Torvalds walked in, he wore flip-flops, shorts, and a T-shirt.

Did it make me a different and better person? No, I don't think so...But I hope and think that it perhaps made me slightly more aware and careful about my public emails.

Torvalds doesn't do much coding for Linux these days. He describes his role now as a "top-level technical lead." He never wanted to start his own company, he said — only to create the "best operating system out there." 

For nearly his entire career, Torvalds has devoted himself to Linux and open-source software. He turned down a job offer from Steve Jobs because the Apple CEO insisted he stop working on Linux. In addition to Linux, Torvalds created Git, the source-control system that's the basis for companies like GitHub and GitLab and which makes most of today's open-source projects possible. He has three daughters, and his oldest is actually a software engineer, though she doesn't work on the Linux kernel ("I'm not creating some Linux-kernel dynasty," he said).  

Despite being an introvert who doesn't "particularly like crowds of people," Torvalds said he loves "communicating and doing development in the open." To Torvalds' mind, building software through open-source collaboration is the most practical way to do the job.

"It just adds a huge dimension to the whole problem solving part of being an engineer, because you can work together with people from different backgrounds, and with people who have completely different goals from you," Torvalds said.  

The contradiction — of an irascible coder who champions collaboration and owes his legacy to it — is at the heart of Linux's success, and its current predicament. 

"Linux is changing with time. Linus is changing as well," said Igor Seletskiy, CEO and founder of CloudLinux. "He's trying to be more and more accepting, and we get less and less outbursts from him."

Around the time of his leave in 2018, Torvalds began going to therapy for the first time in his life and continued with it for a few months after his return. "I come from a generation where talking to a therapist is not very natural, and I wasn't sure what to expect," Torvalds said. "But I turned out to enjoy it, explaining the background and where I came from and what people complained about, and I think it was useful and good."

Jens Axboe, a Facebook software engineer and Linux developer, noted a "marked improvement," an assessment he attributes the fact that no email that Torvalds sends these days is "directly offensive in itself."

"Did it make me a different and better person?" Torvalds asked. "No, I don't think so. A few months of talking things over only does so much. But I hope and think that it perhaps made me slightly more aware and careful about my public emails."

If it weren't for Torvalds' impatience and frustration, there might be no Linux.

As a 21-year-old computer-science student at the University of Helsinki in 1991, Torvalds had to wait in line to use the school's computer system, which was limited to 16 users at a time. The Unix operating system that powered the school computers, not to mention the hardware itself, was priced for business customers — way too expensive for a student to buy on their own — and serious programmers like Torvalds thought the PCs at the time weren't up to snuff for their needs.

So Torvalds bought a PC and started fiddling around, writing his own Unix-like code that enabled the machine to perform certain tasks. Soon he realized his homegrown tinkering had essentially produced the kernel, or core, of an operating system. He decided to keep going and to spread the word.

"I'm doing a (free) operating system (just a hobby, won't be big and professional like gnu) for 386(486) AT clones... I'd like to know what features most people would want," Torvalds wrote on an online bulletin board that August. "Any suggestions are welcome, but I won't promise I'll implement them :-)"

The project attracted other programmers who submitted code to fix bugs they discovered or to enable new functions. The Linux open-source community was born.

As Linux gained popularity within tech circles, it went from an "odd toy operating system" to something used on web servers and in other real-world applications. By 1993, more than 100 people were independently contributing code to Linux. Torvalds, who was still a student, was overseeing the entire project. 

The formidable challenges of the situation resulted in many of the practices and norms that still shape how Linux is managed today.

The best developers were allotted certain parts of the kernel, essentially their own little fiefdoms, to oversee as "maintainers," reviewing all relevant code that developers submitted, providing quality control and feedback, and interacting with Torvalds.

Developers say that they admire Torvalds for "technical vision" for code quality, for acting as a "catalyst" who helps the thousands of Linux engineers work together, and for being "helpful" in teaching other engineers. The Linux developer Laura Abbott, the only woman on a white-male-dominated Linux Technical Advisory Board, said Torvalds is "not the warmest and fuzziest person out there," but he can "give good feedback when needed."

Torvalds' "frank style of communication" and penchant for dropping F-bombs and arguing also fostered a free-for-all culture in which technical discussion often devolved into insult-laden spats. 

Neil Brown, a programmer who spent 15 years as a maintainer in the Linux community, said that "having a leader who demonstrates rare but real abusive outbursts" doesn't cause everyone in the group to behave similarly, but it emboldens those already prone to such conduct. 

"This is, I think, what led to a variety of poor behaviors" within the Linux developer community, Brown said. 

Brown himself would pay close attention to what Torvalds wrote on the email lists so he could work with Torvalds more effectively and learn how he worked. Occasionally he and Torvalds had strong disagreements. 

"We all make mistakes. That is understood, and it probably upsets the person who made the mistake more than it upsets Linus," Brown told Insider. "I think that what Linus expects of those he trusts is humility, though that needs some refinement."

Inside many tech companies is a designated employee who serves as the primary liaison with the developers in the Linux community.

It is not a fun role. 

The liaison submits "patches," or updates, to the Linux code that ensure the company's latest features will work with the Linux operating system. If the patch has a bug or some other problem, the person will bear the brunt of the barrage of criticism and possibly verbal harassment that often ensues. 

"When you bust your patch, they humiliate and mock you," a former Linux developer said, referring to when a developer makes a mistake on a code fix. "So they're enjoying that."

In one incident in 2013, Torvalds and other Linux developers joked about the frequent yelling on the Linux mailing list, encouraging one of their peers to be more confrontational in order to be effective. "You may need to learn to shout at people," Torvalds wrote, after admonishing the person because "you make it easy to act as a door-mat."

The casual acceptance of shouting and verbal attacks within the Linux community has contributed to what some say could become an unwelcoming environment. "We want the best ideas from everyone, not just people willing to withstand withering criticism," IBM president and former Red Hat CEO Jim Whitehurst told Insider.

While Torvalds apologized for his behavior when he announced that he would step away in 2018, some developers feel an apology doesn't negate the harm he might have caused over the past few decades, nor does it bring back the people he might have pushed out.

"A whole bunch of nice people put up with it. Either that or they get tired of it and leave," the former Linux developer said. The developer, who asked to remain anonymous, said they were personally aware of several women and members of underrepresented groups who have left the Linux community because of the culture.

As it is, getting involved in Linux has significant barriers besides having a thick skin. One would have to work on it in their spare time, submit patches, and get noticed to become a trusted developer. That requires having free time, a computer, and a reliable internet connection — something not everyone has access to. It also helps to have a good education and to be fluent in English. As a result, the community leans toward people with those privileges, largely white men. 

We want the best ideas from everyone, not just people willing to withstand withering criticism

The lack of diversity in the Linux community, as well as the open-source and tech community at large, can also make it difficult for newcomers from marginalized groups to join. Over the years, the number of female contributors to Linux has risen overall, though it dipped some years. Linux now has more than 300 female contributors, who make up about 8% of all contributors. 

The Linux Foundation has a mentorship initiative, which works to welcome and retain new developers, including women. It's also working on making its leadership more diverse, the Linux Foundation fellow Shuah Khan said. Still, only 16% of participants in the mentorship program are women.

Until recently, Torvalds appeared to be dismissive of diversity in tech, preaching the gospel of meritocracy. "All that stuff is just details and not really important," he said in 2015. 

Now he says that while the low number of women in Linux reflects the tech industry as a whole, "we should try to do better" because Linux is one of the bigger projects in the industry.

Torvalds doesn't know what the solution to bringing on more women would be though.

"Do I believe there is a silver bullet? No," Torvalds said. "Do we have women and people of color in the kernel? Yes we do - but I'd be lying through my teeth if I claimed it wasn't a small small minority. I just don't know how to change it."

For all his assertiveness, Torvalds says his power is modest.

"You can't ever really force anybody to do something" in open source," he said. "The real power I have is that people trust me." 

How much Torvalds has changed since his therapy and timeout depends on who you ask. 

Torvalds himself described a newfound enlightenment even as he takes pains to temper expectations. 

"When people I trust and like say 'Linus, you look kind of like an a--hole, you need to take a step back and look at your behavior,' I think you have two options: either you think they are wrong and just continue to work like you always did, or you take their advice seriously," Torvalds said.

"Hey, I'm also not going to claim that I'm a whole different person just because I talked to a professional for a couple of months. That's not how things work. But hopefully I'm at least a bit more careful and aware of my language," he said. 

If Torvalds is ambivalent about his own transformation, there's little doubt about how much the world he lives in has changed in the decades since he started Linux. There's less tolerance now for the "brilliant a--hole," the Facebook engineer and Linux developer Axboe said.

"People are called out for that kind of behavior," Axboe said. "It was unthinkable 10 years ago. Back in the early days, it was kind of a Wild West thing."

The recent controversy over a free-software pioneer, the Free Software Foundation's Richard Stallman, is a sign to some insiders that the industry is grappling with issues of accountability and power dynamics in a way that didn't happen before. In the wake of allegations that Stallman harassed women or made them feel uncomfortable and criticism over remarks he made about the convicted sex trafficker Jeffrey Epstein, 3,000 developers signed an open letter calling for Stallman's removal from FSF's board of directors. The board ultimately kept Stallman, but IBM-owned Red Hat and Mozilla withdrew support for the FSF. 

When you bust your patch, they humiliate and mock you

Around the time Torvalds stepped away in September 2018, Linux adopted a code of conduct that explicitly holds project managers, or "maintainers," accountable. Maintainers who don't follow or enforce the code can face "temporary or permanent repercussions," the rules state.

People can send in complaints, which are reviewed and taken "very seriously," Khan said.

But because Linux uses what the open source community calls a "benevolent dictator for life" system, in which Torvalds has the final word on any and all changes or updates to the project, some people are skeptical about the extent to which Torvalds can be held accountable (Torvalds, incidentally, says he doesn't care for the word "dictator.") And some say not enough has changed with Torvalds, and developers tolerate his behavior only because Linux underpins much of the world's technology today. "He's in a position of temptation," the former Linux developer said. "He may have toned it down, but he hasn't fundamentally changed." 

At a Linux conference a couple of years ago, David Miller, a Red Hat distinguished engineer, immediately considered booking an arcade for an event he was organizing.

But it occurred to him that a video-game arcade would have been much more appropriate 15 years earlier, when the average age of attendees at Linux conferences was a lot younger. He booked a restaurant instead. 

Russell Coker recalled that when he first joined Linux, it was mostly people in their 20s. Now, people are in their 40s or older. He's concerned with the lack of recruitment of younger developers. When he mentioned it in a public forum, others agreed that it should be addressed, but not much has been done. 

The Linux project is still growing. The number of contributors to the Linux kernel each year has risen to more than 4,000 now, though growth has slowed somewhat in the past three years.

Many of the top maintainers, who know Linux's subsystems inside and out, have been around for decades, and it's rare to see major subsystems change hands. New Linux contributors tend to be professionals working on Linux for their company, rather than younger developers. 

"I think a lot of people feel comfortable with things going the way they are, but while they're working at the moment, I'm concerned about what will happen in 10 years' time when people my age are retiring, and we need to have younger people more involved," Coker said.

Joining the Linux community can have a major learning curve. The Linux kernel used to be a much smaller project, but now it's harder to break into. Today there are more contributors, more code, and more noise on the Linux mailing lists. Students learning different sets of tooling are now used to finding projects on platforms like GitHub. The way people interact over the internet has also changed, as Linux communicates mostly via email.

And there are more choices in the open-source world. In the years since Linux was created, a plethora of other open-source projects have emerged. For some up-and-coming techies, a newer open-source project like Google's TensorFlow or Facebook's React has more allure than an older, established project like Linux. 

"We are aging, and we need to bring people into the community as quickly as possible to replace these old hats," Miller said.

When I asked Torvalds about how several developers said the Linux community was getting older, he said that he's "actually not very concerned about that." While kernel maintainers used to skew young — Torvalds himself was 21 — it's been three decades, and many of these people have stuck around. 

"It's absolutely true that the kernel maintainers are getting older - it seems to happen to all of us, with each of us being a year older every year," Torvalds said. "Some of us get fatter too, while others have thinning hair."

Linux has "plateaued" in growth though, Torvalds acknowledges. While the project has a "healthy development pace," it's not "accelerating," he said. Still, Linux does releases every two to three months, with 1,500 to 2,000 developers involved in each, and he's comfortable with where the project is in terms of size and growth. 

Linux's reputation for not being inclusive might also deter newcomers. They might be intimidated, or even "mortified," about developing for the kernel, Miller said. And they're not going to stick around if they don't feel like they're being treated nicely, said Axboe, who has been actively recruiting new graduates at Facebook to work on the kernel.

"As you go into decade one or two or three, to make the project survive and thrive over time, you need to bring fresh, new blood," Axboe said. "A caustic environment or reputation for being caustic is not conducive to bringing younger people into the fold."

Perhaps the biggest question is what will happen to Linux when Torvalds steps down. 

Torvalds expects that Linux would go in a similar direction as the Python programming language. After the Python creator Guido van Rossum stepped away from day-to-day involvement, a steering committee was created to oversee things.

"Yes, we have several high profile maintainers, but if I suddenly went away, there's a wide and deep set of people who could maintain Linux," Torvalds said.

"There would be politics, there would be some infighting, but the chaos would very much be temporary."  

At least, that's what Torvald hopes.
