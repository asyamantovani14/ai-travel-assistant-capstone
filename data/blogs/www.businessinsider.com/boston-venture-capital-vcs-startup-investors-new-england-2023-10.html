The 52 most important VCs in Boston, according to other VCs

By Melia Russell
2023-10-09T09:00:04Z
Facebook Email X LinkedIn Copy link Impact Link
Save Saved Read in app
This story is available exclusively to Business Insider subscribers. Become an Insider and start reading now. Have an account? .
  • Boston-born company Klaviyo's Wall Street debut put a spotlight on the vibrant tech scene of Boston.
  • The city is home to many savvy, if under-the-radar, venture capitalists and emerging managers.
  • To toast their success, Insider asked investors to nominate the most important VCs in Boston.

In September, one of Boston's own homegrown companies, Klaviyo, was among the first tech startups to pry open the IPO market, which had been closed for a two-year stretch.  Shares of Klaviyo, which makes email-marketing software for online merchants, soared in its Wall Street debut and are trading a hair above its opening price. The exit will create a cash windfall for local investors who bet early on Klaviyo.

The moment also shone a light on the thriving ecosystem of startup founders and funders in and around the 617.

"I think there should be more entrepreneurs in the world. I don't think there's one magical place where great companies can get built," Klaviyo chief executive Andrew Bialecki told Insider on the morning of its stock launch.

Data shows that Boston is a place to make it big. According to research by Carta, Boston has a healthy lead on other cities for total capital raised across biotech, health-tech, medical devices, and energy companies so far this year. And the city's enterprise software and hardware startups secured more funding than companies in those verticals in tech hot spots like Austin, Seattle, and Los Angeles.

It's also worth mentioning that funding to Boston startups has cooled from the peak in 2021, as has funding everywhere.

In celebration of this vibrant scene, Insider is recognizing the most important venture capitalists in the greater Boston area. We asked VCs from across the country about the Boston investors to know, and we also referred to our past reporting on the top seed investors to find more picks.

Ajay Agarwal, Bain Capital Ventures

Why he's on the list: With 20 years at Bain Capital Ventures under his belt, Agarwal has spent more than a third of his life at the fund. In that time, he pioneered investing in the modern sales-and-marketing stack with early bets on Clari and Gainsight, and became one of the earliest and most active investors in logistics and supply chain, with stakes in Kiva Systems, FourKites, and Vention.

Prior to Bain, Agarwal founded his first company as a sophomore at Stanford and led product and go-to-market for Trilogy Software, one of the fastest-growing software companies of the dot-com era.

Select investments: Bloomreach, FourKites, Kiva Systems, SendGrid, ShipBob, Vention

How to get his attention: "I love founders solving hard problems in the physical world or for old-line industries.  Having backed companies like Kiva (logistics), Vention (manufacturing), FourKites (supply chain), ShipBob (logistics), Iodine (health care), I have come to appreciate the massive value startups can unlock for legacy industries.  The reduction in hardware costs, the advancements of GenAI, and the progress of computer vision are enabling a new generation of founders to solve huge problems in the physical world."

Neeraj Agrawal, Battery Ventures

Why he's on the list: Since joining Battery Ventures in 2000, Agrawal, who invests in software-as-a-service and internet companies across stages, has become one of the most sought-after enterprise investors anywhere. In 2015, he introduced the "T2D3" growth metric for methodically growing software-as-a-service enterprises — a framework that proved successful at companies like Salesforce, Workday, and Zendesk. Additionally, Agrawal's streak of successful exits with AppDynamics, Braze, Marketo, and Nutanix has landed him on the Midas List 11 times.

Select investments: AppDynamics, Braze, Dataiku, Kustomer, Level AI, Sprinklr

How to get his attention: "Truly great markets, like truly great founders, are still somewhat rare. But amazing product-market fit — successfully solving an acute need for a large group of potential buyers — is the most rare. When I see an amazing founder achieving true product-market fit in a very significant market, I know I've got to invest."

Payal Agrawal Divakaran, .406 Ventures

Why she's on the list: Agrawal Divakaran is a Boston native and partner at .406 Ventures, where she co-leads the digital health-investing practice. She cut her teeth sourcing and leading deals at Spectrum Equity, a growth-equity firm in Boston, before entering Harvard Business School. While there, she worked at the events company Eventbrite and started her own company, SpotRocket, a directory of job opportunities at promising startups.

At .406, a firm that invests in early-stage, business-focused startups, Agrawal Divakaran backs founders who are transforming healthcare technology and care delivery.

Select investments: Diana Health, Equip, Heartbeat Health, Nema Health, Nomad Health, Redox

How to get her attention: "Changing the current healthcare reality in the US is not for the faint of heart. The founders that resonate most with me are those who have a deep and lived experience with the problem that they are trying to solve, mission centricity at the core of their company and culture, and humility. I respond even to cold emails, particularly when these elements come across!"

Blake Bartlett, OpenView Venture Partners

Why he's on the list: Bartlett's claim to fame is coining the phrase "product-led growth," which refers to companies offering a free version of their product to capture more paying customers. Buzzwords aside, Barlett has shepherded some of the fastest-growing enterprise startups, from Expensify to Calendly, as a growth-stage investor. After four years sourcing and evaluating deals at Battery Ventures, he joined OpenView as a partner in 2013.

Select investments: Calendly, Expensify, Glassdoor, Highspot, Optimizely, Wayfair

How to get his attention: "I am focused on product above all else. Product delivers customer value. Product drives your SaaS metrics. Product creates your competitive advantage. And product is your resume as a founder. What's even better is if you've built a PLG product that I can try immediately with a few clicks or taps."

David Beisel, NextView Ventures

Why he's on the list: Beisel is a dot-com entrepreneur-turned-investor, focused on internet companies tackling multibillion-dollar opportunities. At NextView Ventures, an early-stage firm he helped start in 2010, he leads only two or three new deals a year, ensuring he has the bandwidth to work closely with founders he's backed. Previously, Beisel was a vice president at Venrock, a firm that started as the venture arm of the Rockefeller family.

Select investments: Attentive, The Nudge, Parsec, TripleLift, ThredUp

How to get his attention: "I strive to work with founders who are authentic. Demonstrating that your past experience provides a unique insight as to why you're starting the company and an unfair advantage about how you'll succeed really resonates."

Kent Bennett, Bessemer Venture Partners

Why he's on the list: Back in 2015, Bennett led the first institutional investment in restaurant management software company Toast, one of Boston's most successful startups. He's become synonymous with the Boston tech scene, investing in homegrown startups such as Bevi and RevenueBase and beyond the 617. As a partner at Bessemer, Bennett focuses on consumer products and services as well as software built for consumer-facing businesses. In 2023, he was appointed the board chair of the New England Venture Capital Association.

Select investments: Bevi, Blue Apron, GlossGenius, Toast, Truebill

How to get his attention: "email me @ kent@bvp.com and keep it short and sweet or ask any founder I work with for an intro."

Graham Brooks, .406 Ventures

Why he's on the list: Brooks leads the data and AI practice for .406 Ventures, focusing on highly disruptive platform and deep tech companies. Getting a jump on the sector before many other investors, he now sits on the boards of more than 20 companies, including Bobsled, a startup changing how teams access data created outside of their organization, which raised $17 million in Series A funding in April. He also runs the firm's college and university outreach program, .406 Ventures Student Fellows.

Select investments: Abacus, Bobsled, Corvus, Indico, Reltio

How to get his attention: "I always love warm intros from our portfolio, EIRs, executive network etc., but I also answer every email, so just reach out: graham@406ventures.com. Founders should know that we are very focused and thesis driven. We want to look at all data infrastructure or AI companies, but I won't invest outside of data and AI, and as a firm we invest in three areas: data and AI, digital health, and cybersecurity. Because we are very thesis driven, we can get to a response very quickly as to whether a particular entrepreneur is a fit for one of our open theses."

Jeffrey Bussgang, Flybridge Capital Partners

Why he's on the list: Bussgang knows startups from the inside. Before becoming an investor, he founded Upromise, a company that helps families save money for college. Sallie Mae bought the startup for $300 million in 2006. Since then, Bussgang has shared his earned wisdom with other entrepreneurs as a cofounder and general partner at Flybridge Capital Partners, a seed-stage firm focused on companies that tap communities to drive growth.

On the side, Bussgang lectures on entrepreneurship at his alma mater, Harvard Business School, and serves as board chair of Hack.Diversity, a nonprofit that breaks down barriers for Black and Latinx professionals in tech.

Select investments: AllSpice, Bowery Farming, FalconX, Finkargo, Habi, H2OK, Syrup.AI

How to get his attention: "The founders that I find most compelling have an 'earned secret' — a unique insight based on past work or recent research that no one else understands or appreciates. I love talking to founders who have figured out (or, at least, hypothesized that they've figured out) how to design compelling, valuable business models based on these earned secrets."

Liz Christo, Stage 2 Capital

Why she's on the list: Christo is a partner at Stage 2 Capital, where she invests in business-focused software companies at the early stage. The firm has a unique model of pairing investors into two-person teams it calls pods, and so along with partner Dan Heck, Christo has secured stakes in startups including Slang, a customer-service tech startup that launched out of stealth with $20 million in Series A funding in June. They also led the investment for AeroCloud, which makes software for airports, this year.

Before Stage 2, Christo spent six years at OpenView Venture Partners and worked on improving processes to help investors find and win the best software deals.

Select investments: AeroCloud, Cable, Gigpro, QuotaPath, Slang

How to get her attention: "While there is a long list of things we are looking for before making an investment, I anchor on the CEO and founding team. First, the founder-market fit. I need to be able to answer 'what makes YOU uniquely suited to solve this problem NOW?' And second, how you 'show up' in our interactions. I'm looking to work with competitive, driven founders who are building a world-class team, are customer-focused and, most importantly, do what they say they are going to do."

Ellen Chisa, Boldstart Ventures

Why she's on the list: As a partner at Boldstart Ventures, Chisa backs technical founders who may have little more than an idea. She invests in developer tools and business software, drawing on her product-management background and engineering skills from Microsoft. Before Boldstart, Chisa cofounded Dark, a programming language for seamlessly deploying code into production.

Select investments: Flow Club, Liveblocks, Skydio, Snaplet, Stellate, Zuplo 

How to get her attention: "I mainly invest in developer tooling, so if you're looking at building something that makes it easier to write software, you should definitely reach out. I particularly love when a founder can crisply share the problem they're looking to solve, and who has that problem. I answer cold emails — even if you aren't fundraising yet and just want someone to start to talk through the business with."

Kevin Colleran, Slow Ventures

Why he's on the list: Colleran's sales prowess and social network have made him one of the most influential investors in Boston, though his companies are scattered coast to coast. His firm, Slow Ventures, has made investments in more than 400 companies in the past 13 years. Of those, over 100 startups saw an exit through a merger or an initial public offering, Crunchbase reports.

Hired as Facebook's first ad salesperson at age 24, Colleran's specialty is helping founders at the seed stage prepare to raise and close their next funding rounds.

Select investments: Allbirds, Good Dog, Livongo Health, PillPack, Ro

How to get his attention: "The best way to get our attention is through a mutual introduction from somebody we consider high signal. Almost all of our investments originate from an introduction and recommendation from another founder, angel investor, and VC. Founders are typically very willing to introduce other high-quality founders to their investors as a way to help the startup ecosystem, and we always prioritize those introductions as we sort through our many inbound leads."

Mackey Craven, OpenView Venture Partners

Why he's on the list: They say those who can't do, teach. But Craven has done both. He started a company, BioVolt, focused on designing low-cost microbial fuel cells, while at MIT. In graduate school, he taught at the MIT business school and in the physics and biology departments. His background makes him an empathetic investor, highly attuned to the challenges of company building.

In 2013, Craven left Bessemer Venture Partners and joined as a partner at OpenView, where he focuses on enterprise infrastructure and data-driven application software.

Select investments: Axonius, Datadog, Jumpcloud, Twitch, UserTesting, Zapier

How to get his attention: "Building a large and enduring business from scratch is really, really hard — and so the founders that resonate with me have a clear and persuasive vision for the way in which their business will change the market, consistently persevere to achieve their objectives, and genuinely value the team they build along the way. Combining these characteristics with a product that users and customers can't help but use more of over time will always get my attention."

Richard Dulude, Underscore VC

Why he's on the list: Dulude has been with Underscore since its inception in 2015, starting as a principal and climbing the ranks to general partner. There, he focuses on investing at the seed stage in business-focused software startups, as well as writing the playbook that guides the firm's investment thesis and approach to deploying capital. His portfolio includes CloudZero, a Boston-based startup that aims to help other businesses manage their cloud costs, which raised $32 million Series B funding in June.

Select investments: Chainlink, CloudZero, Hue, Messari, Tetrascience

How to get his attention: "If you are building early-stage enterprise software, just DM me on LinkedIn or Twitter, simple as that — as a team, we work hard to be accessible and responsive, especially for Boston-based founders."

Semyon Dukach, One Way Ventures

Why he's on the list: Dukach became an overnight millionaire when he sold a software startup he founded, Fast Engines, for $35 million in 2000. He used his new riches to make more than a hundred angel investments, and went on to lead TechStars Boston, growing it into the most prolific startup accelerator in Beantown.

In 2017, Dukach, a Ukrainian-American, struck out on his own to raise a venture fund dedicated to backing other immigrant entrepreneurs. His firm, One Way Ventures, has $100 million in assets under management and invests in fintech, proptech, logistics, software, and deep tech.

Select investments: Brex, Chipper Cash, Helm.ai, Nuvocargo

How to get his attention: "We look for extraordinary immigrant founders building large, disruptive businesses."

Jeff Fagnan, Accomplice

Why he's on the list: Accomplice has stopped raising new funds, but Fagnan isn't putting away his checkbook. He's turned his attention to Spearhead, a program he founded with AngelList founder Naval Ravikant in 2018 to provide entrepreneurs with money and mentorship to start angel investing. Spearhead closed $100 million for its fourth fund in 2020 and now counts over 80 founders in the program. 

Select investments: AngelList, Hopper, Patreon, PillPack, Recorded Future, Whoop, Zoe

How to get his attention: "My time is now dedicated to Spearhead and supporting the movement of founders-backing-founders. The best way to get my attention is to apply to Spearhead!"

Andy Feinberg, Argon Ventures

Why he's on the list: Argon Ventures is the brainchild of two longtime executives at Brightcove, a Boston-born company that provides cloud services for video. In 2019, Feinberg, who held the roles of chief operating officer and acting chief executive over 13 years at Brightcove, locked arms with its cofounder, Bob Mason, to raise a debut fund. Together they lead or co-lead pre-seed investments in companies enabled by AI and data scale effects.

Select investments: Cognitive Space, PeakMetrics, Retrocausal, RevenueBase, Trilobilo

How to get his attention: "The two things that I look for from the outset are, 'Is this a great founder?' and 'How important is the problem they are trying to solve?' 'Great' can mean many different things in a founder: incredible domain experience and industry insight, interpersonal skills that create unfair advantages in selling and building teams, humble confidence and conviction that ensures they will listen, learn and iterate, superior energy and execution skills. These are just a few of the ways in which founders can stand out. 'How important is the problem' is also fundamental for me. I want to hear right away why we need the solution that the founder is proposing to build. What is the pain point that is being addressed? How much of a pain point is it?"

Julian Fialkow, 186 Ventures

Why he's on the list: Fialkow is an emerging manager with a record of exits beyond his years of experience. He began his career at DraftKings in Boston, where he worked on strategic initiatives and set up the company's venture arm, Drive by DraftKings. While there, he started pooling his personal money with a friend and coworker, Giuseppe Stuto, to write angel checks into startups including UiPath, which went public at a $35 billion valuation in 2021, and blockchain data startup Chainalysis, which was last valued at $8.6 billion. In 2021, the duo made quick work of raising a $37 million debut fund, closing in just four months.

Select investments: Givingli, Integrated Projects, Mentor Collective, Onyx, UiPath 

How to get his attention: "When considering founders for potential investment, my focus lies squarely on those who are navigating the uncharted waters of the zero-to-one phase in company development. Paramount to us is the founders' profound grasp of the problem they aim to tackle and the compelling 'why' behind their venture. Equally important is their comprehensive knowledge of the competitive landscape, a keen awareness of how to stand out in an often saturated market, and the relentless drive that underpins their commitment, passion, and hunger. Additionally, what truly distinguishes exceptional founders in my eyes is their mastery of sector-specific metrics and KPIs, coupled with an obsession for devising strategies to attain them. Lastly, the ability to weave an engaging narrative and inspire both their team and potential collaborators/customers with their mission is key. At the end of the day, we feel very lucky to partner and support founders and hopefully play a small role in their journey."

Alex Finkelstein, Spark Capital

Why he's on the list: Finkelstein joined Spark Capital, a multistage firm founded in Boston, as a young, hungry investor in 2005. Since then, he's climbed the ranks from principal to general partner, helping the firm nab stakes in Boston-based startups like Flywire and Wayfair.

Outside of investing, Finkelstein serves on the board of directors of the Brooke Charter Schools, a network of high-performing public charter schools in Boston.

Select investments: 5min Media, Flywire, Reggora, Talkspace, Wayfair

How to get his attention: "I'm looking for the product- and customer-obsessed founder who also knows how to sell."

Aaron Fleishman, Tola Capital

Why he's on the list: After four years on Microsoft's corporate strategy team, Fleishman left the company to join forces with two alumni who had founded a venture firm. At Tola Capital, he invests in his wheelhouse of enterprise software, AI, and blockchain tech, and gives the Seattle firm a set of eyes on Boston's startup ecosystem.

Select investments: Clipchamp, Robin, Zilla Security

How to get his attention: "I'm more focused on how to get a founder's attention. World-class founders in great markets can always raise capital. We at Tola believe in a deep hypothesis-based approach to investing whereby we have already validated a market opportunity and a company's potential before our first conversation with a founder. As a result, we want that first conversation to be a thoughtful, collaborative discussion instead of a pitch."

David Frankel, Founder Collective

Why he's on the list: Before he was 27, Frankel built and sold an African internet-service provider for more than $3 billion. He used his sudden wealth to write angel checks while studying at Harvard Business School in the early 2000s. On campus, he met a classmate, Eric Paley, and the two bonded over a shared interest in venture capital. In 2009, they started Founder Collective with the goal of being the investors they wished they had as entrepreneurs.

The past decade has seen the firm grow to $285 million in assets under management, with a track record of successful exits including PillPack and Stack Overflow. In 2022, Frankel ranked No. 11 on the Midas List for a second year.

Select investments: Coupang, Olo, PillPack, SeatGeek, Shield AI, Sift, Stack Overflow

How to get his attention: "I've been fortunate to lead a successful startup, invest in hundreds of entrepreneurs as an investor, and sit on the board of a public, multi-billion dollar tech company. I've learned I derive immense satisfaction working with founders, whether building software for event-driven calls or reinventing cat food, crafting SaaS tools for the flooring industry, or fast food point-of-sale systems. I invite founders with the passion required to win a market, no matter how weird and wonderful, to reach out via introduction, email, or Twitter (@dafrankel)."

Rob Go, NextView Ventures

Why he's on the list: A decade ago, starting a venture firm anywhere outside of Silicon Valley was unthinkable. That didn't deter Go, a cofounder and partner at NextView, from setting up shop in Boston and growing the firm into a bicoastal mainstay of the seed funding market, with $425 million in assets under management. As an investor, he focuses on companies that use software to deliver "transformative end-user experiences." This has led him to make investments in a wide range of industries including e-commerce, labor marketplaces, and digital health.

Select investments: Bobbie, Devoted Health, Form Health, Setpoint, Tive

How to get his attention: "I get immediately captivated by teams with exceptional founder-market fit. I think of this as the combination of unique domain insight with a clear overlap between the founders' super-powers and the specific challenges of the business they are building. When these things align, magical things can happen."

Jamie Goldstein, Pillar VC

Why he's on the list: A former entrepreneur, Goldstein knows that scaling a startup takes a village. That's why his firm, Pillar VC, has assembled an all-star team of local founders and chief executives to provide coaching and connections to the founders it's backed. During his career, Goldstein has backed dozens of Boston startups, including many spun out from his alma maters, MIT and Harvard. 

Select investments: Algorand, Asimov, Circle, Desktop Metal, Jellyfish, PathAI, PillPack

How to get his attention: "The best way to connect is by reaching out through one of our portfolio companies. We have great respect for their judgment. Connect through a founder, leader, advisor, or co-investor. I also really enjoy working with scientific founders. Forward me a paper you've published based on an amazing new discovery or insight. You can also try cold emailing me with a provocative subject line."

Samara Gordon, Hyperplane

Why she's on the list: Gordon was promoted this past year to general partner at Hyperplane, where she invests in her wheelhouse of enterprise software and deep tech. During college, she started a predictive-analytics company, which gave her a ticket to a fintech startup in Boston after graduation. She joined as Finomial's first product manager.

After a consulting stint in New York, Gordon returned to Boston to try her hand at startup investing. At Hyperplane, she puts her skills and checkbook to work for overlooked founders — half of her portfolio is female-founded.

Select investments: Flexpa, Integrate, MedEssist, Mudstack, Relevize

How to get her attention: "Because early stage investing is a long-term game, for me, it is first and foremost about entrepreneurs with whom I will want to work closely with for a long time, from first company to unforeseen ventures in the future. I value high integrity, curiosity, hustle, and strong character. If the people and company culture is there, success follows. People first. The rest are details. On a more tactical front, I love referrals from founders and LPs and my Twitter DMs are open."

Rick Grinnell, Glasswing Ventures

Why he's on the list: Long before AI became such a buzzword in startup land, Glasswing has invested in the next generation of intelligent enterprise and frontier technology startups, and it will continue to do so long after the hype fades. Grinnell, one of the firm's founders and a managing partner, focuses on investments in AI-enabled security and enterprise infrastructure. He brings deep ties to local universities as an alum and now a mentor through his involvement with Harvard's Rock Center for Entrepreneurship and the Sandbox program at MIT.

Select investments: Allure Security, Black Kite, Flux Cyber, Nametag, Trustle

How to get his attention: "The best way to initially get my attention is to find an introduction to me or one of my Glasswing partners through someone that we know and trust. It's not that difficult to find one of Glasswing's advisors, portfolio executives, or other relationships that you also know — LinkedIn can certainly help with this effort. Once you've found that warm intro, the factors that will elevate your company to the top of the pipeline start with the quality of your team — the collective experience, market insight, drive and commitment to succeed. Given we are we are an AI and frontier tech focused firm, the product, including the core IP, are of great importance to us. As a seed-stage investor, product-market fit may be still not be 100% clear, but early signals from the market — early references, including those from design partners or pilot customers, and feedback from our advisor network — are critical.  Essentially, we are looking for market demand for what you are building, and evidence that you are the team to succeed."

Sofia Guerra, Bessemer Venture Partners

Why she's on the list: Guerra brings a scientific rigor to her role as a vice president at Bessemer in Cambridge, where she invests in healthcare and life sciences technology. She studied chemistry at Harvard University and began her career as a consultant at Bain & Company, where she researched markets and potential investments in the healthcare and tech sectors. On the side, Guerra is a board director of Nucleate, a nonprofit dedicated to empowering the next generation of biotech leaders.

Select investments: House Rx, Oshi Health, Rupa Health, Turquoise Health

How to get her attention: "I'm focused on healthcare and biotech early- and growth-stage investments and look for three key things in founders. First, authentic founder-market fit with a passion for what they're building and the impact they plan to create. Second, strong articulation of the 'why now' for the adoption of their technology or tech-enabled service. And finally, an understanding of the nuances in a path to scalability for their business model. I'm always open to cold outreach, and accessible through Twitter and LinkedIn DMs."

Chip Hazard, Flybridge Capital Partners

Why he's on the list: Hazard's Boston roots run deep. He joined Greylock Partners, one of the oldest and most respected firms founded in the Boston area, in 1994, and built a track record of landing high-potential investments in enterprise software. Eight years later, that reputation gave him the leverage to raise his own fund at Flybridge Capital Partners, a seed-stage venture firm.

He also cuts checks out of XFactor Ventures, a Flybridge fund focused on investing in female founders and mixed-gender teams that are tackling billion-dollar markets.

Select investments: Firebase, Metaplane, MongoDB, Nasuni, Scitara 

How to get his attention: "Having been part of the start-up journey from pre-revenue to over $1 billion in revenue, passionate and ambitious founders who paint a picture of how their unique insights will transform markets and lead to significant scale always stand out.  Underneath the pursuit of massive market opportunities, I particularly look for community-driven companies that leverage highly engaged, connected, and passionate ecosystems to drive growth and success."

Sarah Hodges, Pillar VC

Why she's on the list: Hodges, a partner at Pillar VC, is one of her firm's superpowers. She runs the platform that offers founders services such as executive search, customer acquisition, and leadership development. She also sources and leads deals in enterprise and consumer companies.

Prior to Pillar, Hodges was head of people at Pluralsight, a company that offers online classes for tech professionals.

Select investments: Allma, Hometap, JobGet, Meru, OnDeck

How to get her attention: "Seed-stage investing is all about people. The companies we invest in often change dramatically — but their founders remain the same. Lead with your story. We invest in people who have demonstrated blow-through-walls resilience in their lives, who are lifelong learners, and who are compelling mission-driven leaders."

Lee Hower, NextView Ventures

Why he's on the list: Hower is one of the most well-connected investors in Boston's startup hub. He got hired straight out of Wharton as an early employee at PayPal, where he led product and business development through the company's public offering and sale to eBay. In 2002, he joined a group of PayPal alumni to build the world's most powerful professional social network, LinkedIn.

Hower's entrepreneurial streak continued when he cofounded NextView Ventures, a firm helping seed-stage businesses sprout. He invests in software and internet companies across consumer, enterprise, and fintech.

Select investments: GrabCAD, Grove Collaborative, Hologram, Skillz, Whoop

How to get his attention: "By having a great answer to 'Why did you start this company?' as opposed to starting or joining some other type of business."

David Jegen, F-Prime Capital

Why he's on the list: Jegen is a single degree of separation from pretty much any fintech founder in New England. He serves as a managing partner at F-Prime Capital, a spinout of Fidelity Investments, and FinTech Sandbox, a nonprofit he helped launch in 2015 to support fintech founders by providing them with access to critical data and resources.

A Harvard grad, Jegen focuses on investments at the intersection of fintech and enterprise software, and made successful bets on three of the largest Boston fintech exits ever: payments provider Flywire, restaurant-management software company Toast, and data analytics firm Kensho.

Select investments: Flywire, Kensho, Quovo, Snapdocs, Toast, Vendr

How to get his attention: "My partners and I are very thesis-driven and we go deep in a few sectors. We're especially eager to hear from founders building businesses in fintech, vertical SaaS, robotics, and healthcare. We believe in the elegant insight — seemingly small ideas that can become mainstream ideas — and we're wired to believe in a founder and their idea when others do not. We rank order impact, fortune, and fame; and look for founders who feel the same way. I love helping founders work through the challenges of building great businesses, and welcome hearing from them long before they need to fundraise."

Steve Kraus, Bessemer Venture Partners

Why he's on the list: With a background in journalism and political science, Kraus had no business backing healthcare startups when he took his first investing job at Ironwood Equity Fund. But he took Harvard extension classes, and soon enough, made a name for himself in healthcare investing. In 2004, he joined Bessemer in the Cambridge office, where he writes for the firm's blog and co-hosts the podcast A Healthy Dose on top of investing.

Kraus has a pair of degrees from Yale and Harvard Business School, where he serves as an advisor.

Select investments: Abridge, Groups Recover Together, Headspace Health, Hinge Health, House Rx

How to get his attention: "Innovating in healthcare is both really hard and really rewarding. I look for founders who have both a clear passion for as well as demonstrated expertise in the industry. I believe the best financial returns in healthcare come from the companies that also truly move the needle in terms of the cost and quality of care, so I focus a lot of the founder's vision in these areas."

Victor Lanio, Flare Capital Partners

Why he's on the list: Lanio is a partner at Flare Capital Partners, an early-stage venture firm dedicated to backing bold entrepreneurs in healthcare technology. He began his career as a healthcare consultant at PwC and McKinsey & Company, and later rolled up his sleeves as an operator, working in corporate development at Curaspan, a startup that provides workflow software for healthcare providers. His background makes him a sage advisor to companies like Greater Good Health, a startup dedicated to expanding primary care access that's raised $10 million in funding.

Select investments: Elektra Health, Greater Good Health, Ounce of Care

How to get his attention: "For a founder to get on our radar, there must be a strong mission to drive the company forward. Fortunately, this is often the case in healthcare software and services. What's less common is finding founders with true founder-market fit. The entrepreneurs with a unique understanding of the problem, from lived experience. Those individuals are rare. Make it clear that you know more about the problem than anyone else, and you have an unfair advantage."

Juan Luis Leungli, Tectonic Ventures

Why he's on the list: Leungli likes to back underdogs like himself. A first-generation immigrant and son of farmhands-turned-restauranteurs, he started a live-music company while attending Harvard Business School. After graduation, he leapt into venture at Primary Venture Partners and General Catalyst. In 2017, Leungli cofounded Tectonic Ventures, a boutique firm based in Cambridge, to fund the next generation of bold founders.

Select investments: 55ip, Auterion, Butlr, Catalant, Forta Health, Merama

How to get his attention: "Entrepreneurs catch my attention through their mission orientation towards solving an important problem, clarity of ambition (i.e., they have a big goal and a conceivable plan towards achieving it), and humility. This last one is subtly important. While the best entrepreneurs have high conviction, they also have the humility to ask questions, have a high learning modality, and can dynamically adjust course as conditions change."

Millie Liu, First Star Ventures

Why she's on the list: Liu is keen on founders solving real-world problems with data, algorithms, and computing. She's walked their shoes before: While a student at MIT, she founded a machine learning startup whose technology became the basis for InferVision, a Chinese company working to improve medical imaging analysis that's raised from marquee investors like Sequoia Capital China.

In 2014, she passed off the reins to her startup and began investing out of her own fund, First Star Ventures. Based in Cambridge, the firm seeks to invest in artificial intelligence, computational biotech, connected sensors, augmented reality, virtual reality, and blockchains sectors.

Select investments: Fyto, Jellyfish, Plus One Robotics, Robigo, Salient Predictions, Zanskar

How to get her attention: "We invest with conviction in visionary, technical founders inventing a better future with hard data and computing technology. With our pre-seed and seed stage early focus, if a founder is working on something within our thesis that's cutting edge that the rest of the world doesn't quite understand, even if there's no prototype to demo or revenue made yet, we are happy to read the papers or codes. The more an entrepreneur can demonstrate deep insights, thoughtfulness, and fast learning capability, the more likely we will get excited about the team on top of the technology itself."

Lily Lyman, Underscore VC

Why she's on the list: Lyman has quickly become a mainstay of the Boston tech scene as a general partner at Underscore, the firm she joined in 2018. She came from Meta, where she drove product decisions that added more than 100 million users in four years. At Underscore, she backs early-stage founders building in the areas of future of work, insurance tech, digital health, and commerce.

Outside of dealmaking, she spends time building community in the venture industry as a leader of All Raise's Boston chapter and a board director of the New England Venture Capital Association.

Select investments: Coda, Goldcast, Herald, Hi Marley, Macro, Project44, Wonderment

How to get her attention: "Like most seed-stage investors, I place emphasis on uniquely qualified teams, markets with massive potential, and products solving painful problems. To narrow the scope, I'm particularly interested in founders that are innovating in laggard industries (like insurance and healthcare), and have a clear 'why now.' I am drawn to founders who are rapid learners, can attract strong talent, and have demonstrated a 'heat-seeking' ability to figure things out."

TJ Mahony, Vinyl Capital

Why he's on the list: In 2021, Mahony spun up his own venture firm, Vinyl, after more than six years at another Boston firm, Accomplice. Its investment focus: the future of "how people buy stuff." Mahony tapped dozens of entrepreneurs from local tech giants like Wayfair, Klaviyo, DraftKings, and Drift as the fund's limited partners.

Before Accomplice, Mahony founded FlipKey, a startup building a vacation-rental marketplace. He sold the company to TripAdvisor for a reported $100 million in 2012.

Select investments: Drizly, Klaviyo, Postscript

How to get his attention: "Technical founders + the ability to demonstrate market mastery."

Rohan Malhotra, Brewer Lane Ventures

Why he's on the list: Malhotra is a general partner at Brewer Lane Ventures, a Boston venture firm focused on early-stage fintech and insurtech, with $375 million under management. He invests in enterprise software and tech-enabled service companies which are modernizing financial services. Prior to Brewer Lane, he held roles across investing, product, and business operations, including leading growth initiatives at insurtech brokerage Newfront, building the venture arm of an incumbent insurer, Argo Group, and designing new products for Fortune 500 financial institutions at a consultancy.

Select investments: Ease Capital, Empathy, EvolutionIQ, January, Savvy

How to get his attention: "I'm drawn towards founders who demonstrate unique insight into a desperate problem underpinning dusty sectors like insurance, asset management, healthcare, and the like. These problems are often not in clear sight and are uncovered by living in the industry for years or through a bit of luck followed by deep research. Clearly articulating an ambitious vision, the product's impact to a P&L, and the scalability of the business model not only grabs my attention, but more importantly, the attention of your future customers and employees."

Kleida Martiro, Glasswing Ventures

Why she's on the list: Martiro went from associate to partner at Glasswing in four years by going all in on AI. She started investing in the sector long before the hype set in, backing founders building solutions for the enterprise and security markets. She already observes six company boards in the Glasswing portfolio and serves as a director at Retrocausal, a computer-vision company that provides tools for the manufacturing industry.

Select investments: Allure Security, Armored Things, Black Kite, ChaosSearch, Retrocausal

How to get her attention: "I focus on AI enterprise software and investing at the seed and pre-seed stages. Founders who get my attention embody grit and determination in the problem they're pursuing, especially if they have first-hand experience. In addition, I look for founders who leverage their toolkit of strong technical skills coupled with business acumen to build products and teams. I'm happy to meet with founders who reflect these qualities, even before they're looking to raise VC capital. The more communication and validation there is along the way, the stronger the relationship can become."

Bob Mason, Argon Ventures

Why he's on the list: Mason is a rare technical founder who took a company from founding to exit when his startup, Brightcove, went public in 2012. Five months later, he stepped down as chief technical officer and went to the "dark side," as he calls venture. He started as a mentor to Boston startups at TechStars Boston and became part of the investment team, sourcing and selecting dozens of companies across three cohorts. In 2020, Mason teamed up with Andy Feinberg, a longtime executive at Brightcove, to raise an inaugural $30 million fund for Argon Ventures.

Select investments: Akooda, AppMap, Basetwo, Gigasheet, Reality Defender

How to get his attention: "I first want to understand the founders' personal story. Why did they come together as a team? I will also geek out on product to understand their point of view on the market opportunity. I look for detailed, creative thinkers that are open minded with a zeal to learn from their customers."

Ryan Moore, Accomplice

Why he's on the list: Moore is a stalwart supporter of the Boston tech ecosystem, writing among the first checks into homegrown companies like DraftKings and Drizly. He's currently investing out of a fourth and final $405 million fund at Accomplice, the firm he founded as a spinoff of Atlas Ventures. In 2023, he ranked No. 25 on the Midas Seed List, a ranking of the top seed investors globally.

Select investments: Caribou, DraftKings, Drizly, HqO, Skillz

How to get his attention: "Go after an industry you have no business trying to break into, or find me through another founder I've backed!"

Baiyin Murphy, Indicator Ventures

Why she's on the list: The daughter of Chinese immigrants, Murphy's grit makes her a patient and dogged investor. She's been backing tech companies for over a decade at venture firms such as OpenView and Indicator Ventures, a seed and pre-seed firm, respectively, both based in Boston. Her speciality is helping enterprise startups build the teams and processes they need to reach product-market fit.

Murphy also serves as a co-chair of the New England Venture Network, an organization dedicated to fostering community in the Boston venture ecosystem.

Select investments: Cylance, Lessonly, Makini, PolymerHQ

How to get her attention: "Convince me of the: Why You, Why Now? At the seed stage, I'm primarily investing in extraordinary founders going after a market that they are uniquely positioned to disrupt. Founders who can effectively show me why they are the person to innovate (whether it's a skill, experience, superpower, insight) and why this is the time to do it (due to a technology, market condition, level of customer pain) will instantly grab my attention. Then the fun begins!"

Vivjan Myrto, Hyperplane

Why he's on the list: Myrto beat the scramble of venture capitalists now trying to back the hottest companies in AI. In 2014, he launched Hyperplane to invest in early-stage companies that are leveraging machine learning and data to solve enterprise and industrial problems. Recent bets include ShipIn, a company building hardware and software that detects events onboard ships in real-time and which raised $24 million in Series A funding in October of 2022.

Select investments: Aryeo, Butlr, Elucidata, GreenEye, Modulate, ShipIn, Tive

How to get his attention: "To capture my attention, a founder should have a track record of hustle, a failure or two in the past, and a profound personal commitment to solving the problem their company aims to tackle. On a personal note, I dropped out of pre-med because a few lines of code could impact tens of millions of lives compared to a handful of patients directly. Meeting a founder who aspires to make a truly large-scale impact/transform an industry or society is both incredibly energizing and inspiring for me."

Jennifer Neundorfer, January Ventures

Why she's on the list: Neundorfer has devoted her career to rewriting the rules of venture capital. In 2018, she cofounded January Ventures to help spread dollars and opportunity to female founders, along with her former Stanford business school classmate Maren Bannon. Of the 50 startups backed by the firm, 90% have a female founder and about a third have a Black or Latino founder.

Before January, Neundorfer started an accelerator and micro-fund focused on software startups in the Midwest.

Select investments: Ethena, Gable, Pano AI, Planet FWD, Oula Health

How to get her attention: "To get my attention, founders don't need a warm intro or specific pedigree. Instead, what really stands out is a founder who combines deep market expertise and conviction around what they are building, with the peripheral vision to figure out where the largest opportunity lies. When I hear an early-stage pitch, the one thing I know for sure is that, in success, the idea will evolve. I get excited when I meet founders who have the commitment and skillset to figure out that evolution."

Eric Paley, Founder Collective

Why he's on the list: Paley is one of the most well-respected venture capitalists in Boston, thanks to a savvy bet on a little-known startup called UberCab in 2010. But his winning streak began long before the ride-hailing giant, when he built and sold two startups during his tenure as an entrepreneur in the early 2000s. Since then, he's used his business chops to invest in other entrepreneurs at his firm, Founder Collective, which invests across sectors.

Paley's pursuit of the "weird and wonderful" has led him to stakes in roughly a dozen startups valued at over $1 billion.

Select investments: Airtable, Cruise, Embark, The Trade Desk, ThredUp, Uber, Whoop

How to get his attention: "Founder Collective is a stage-focused, sector-agnostic firm that invests exclusively in seed rounds with the mission to be the most aligned fund for founders at the seed stage. We've backed everything from automated aquaculture to zoological DNA tests. We're excited to meet founders using software to disrupt almost any industry, no matter how weird and wonderful. If that describes you, please reach out via introduction, email, or even Twitter — I'm @epaley."

Ben Robbins, GV

Why he's on the list: A trained psychiatrist, Robbins brings empathy to his research and investments in healthcare delivery and neuropsychiatric therapeutics. He joined GV in 2014 after earning his medical degree and business degree from Harvard and completing his psychiatric training at Massachusetts General Hospital and McLean Hospital. Robbins, now a general partner at the firm, focuses on startups upgrading healthcare for more people than the current systems can support.

Select investments: Aspire, CareBridge, Cerevance, Headway, Rodin, Waltz Health

How to get his attention: "I gravitate towards investments across healthcare delivery and neuropsychiatric therapeutics. As a psychiatrist, I'm particularly interested in companies building systems that could treat a larger number of people at a consistently higher quality and greater scale than possible in the current system. In addition, some gaps persist in mental health, particularly geriatrics, and I'd like to help close those gaps through focused investments."

David Schenkein, GV

Why he's on the list: Schenkein has spent three decades trying to improve the lives of healthcare patients and their families — as a physician, an entrepreneur, and an investor in the next generation of health startups. In 2019, he joined Alphabet's venture arm, GV, where he oversees the life-sciences-investment team. He leads many of the firm's investments in therapeutics, diagnostics, medical devices, novel-care-delivery systems, and payer innovation.

Before investing, Schenkein spent ten years as chief executive of Agios Pharmaceuticals, a drug-development company focused on anti-cancer therapeutics.

Select investments: Leyden Labs, Prime Medicine, Treeline Biosciences

How to get his attention: "When I transitioned from being a hematologist and medical oncologist into developing new medicines as the former CEO of Agios, I saw the effects of helping tens of thousands of patients around the globe. So today, I look to work with transformative life sciences companies that maximize impact across all aspects of healthcare. For me, there is nothing more exciting than investing in therapeutics that positively impact patient outcomes."

Rudina Seseri, Glasswing Ventures

Why she's on the list: Before Seseri invested in startups, she bought them on Microsoft's dime. She worked as a senior manager in corporate development at the technology giant, where she closed a number of acquisitions in under two years. In 2007, she left Microsoft to take a full-time role in venture, joining a boutique firm called Fairhaven Capital in Cambridge. After a decade with the firm, Seseri struck out on her own in 2016. She started the firm Glasswing to invest in software startups enabled by AI, carving a niche ahead of the current hype cycle.

Select investments: Basetwo, Chaossearch, Crowdtwist, Inrupt, Reprise, Verusen

How to get her attention: "I am most frequently the first institutional investor in a company, so it is important that I take a holistic view of both the market opportunity and founder characteristics. I look for individuals in a founding team who meet or exceed expectations within the first and second meetings. Those who hustle, think outside the box, and demonstrate a high level of preparedness for each conversation will grab the attention of investors. Additionally, my team and I conduct a deep analysis to determine if a startup brings together the rare combination of differentiated, defensible, and business-valuable technology. Founders who can communicate domain expertise in a market and unique insight on an opportunity will consistently excite investors."

Giuseppe Stuto, 186 Ventures

Why he's on the list: In four short years, Stuto has captured the attention of rival investors in Boston who nominated him for this list. Stuto's career kicked into hyperdrive when he sold his video-chat app, Fam, to DraftKings in 2018 and joined the company in a corporate development role. By 2019, Stuto linked arms with a friend and coworker, Julian Fialkow, to make angel investments into early-stage startups. The two raised a $37 million debut fund in 2021 from institutional investors like fund of funds and endowments as well as dozens of operators, including the founders of Alchemy, Digits, and Whoop.

Select investments: Centaur Labs, Integrated Projects, Moni, Mural, Wingspan

How to get his attention: "There are two things that I strongly believe every startup must possess in order to truly break out — something I refer to as the "idiosyncratic phenomenon." First, I like to see why the founders are differentiated in some unprecedented way relative to everyone else who has previously tried to or is trying to solve such a problem— show me the absolute uniqueness whether by background or approach. Second, the founder needs to clearly convey why "now" is the right time to potentially disrupt a mass market."

Matt Thoms, Baukunst

Why he's on the list: Thoms is a cofounder and general partner at Baukunst, where he leads pre-seed investments in companies at the frontiers of technology and design. The firm made waves at launch with a $100 million fund last year, which Thoms believes is one of the largest-ever debut funds for a pre-seed firm. His portfolio includes a number of startups founded not far from home, including Brunt Workwear, a maker of premium work boots, and Fyto, a company that grows aquatic plants in tanks.

Select investments: Brunt Workwear, Fyto, HawkEye 360, Vention, Wildgrain

How to get his attention: "As much as I love new technology, I'm most drawn to founders who know technology alone isn't enough to build an enduring, high-impact business. When founders apply great tech to areas where culture is rapidly evolving, that's when we see the most interesting outcomes: business model innovation, underserved customer segments, and can't-live-without-it product experiences."

Adam Valkin, General Catalyst

Why he's on the list: Valkin is a managing director at General Catalyst, where he spends most of his time focused on investments in consumer and fintech. He joined the firm from Accel in 2013, making the leap from Accel's London office to Cambridge. At General Catalyst, Valkin seeks out founders who want to "fundamentally upgrade the consumer experience" in large markets such as food, automotive, healthcare, banking, education, and fitness.

Select investments: Classpass, Deliveroo, Rapyd, Ro Health

How to get his attention: "Founders get my attention if they demonstrate passion, resilience, thoughtfulness, and a genuine desire to have an impact on the world."

Caitlin Vorlicek, Sageview Capital

Why she's on the list: Vorlicek is a vice president at Sageview Capital, a growth-equity firm where the employees are collectively the firm's largest investor. She leads investments across supply chain, e-commerce, education, and life sciences. And while she evaluates over a hundred companies each year, she writes at most one or two checks, part of the firm's commitment to its founders.

Vorlicek began her career in investment banking at Harris Williams before diving into to venture at Summit Partners.

Key investments: A Cloud Guru, Carewell, Klaviyo, Sifted, Specright

How to get her attention: "I invest in growth-stage technology businesses that are addressing powerful upgrade cycles in big markets. As both investors and entrepreneurs, the one thing we don't control is the market, so I always ask: What market tailwinds does your business address and why will customers opt for change management over status quo? From there, it's all about innovation, execution, and customer obsession, and those are the things I look for in category-leading businesses and teams."

Russ Wilcox, Pillar VC

Why he's on the list: Before he backed startups, Wilcox played a pivotal role in the story of e-readers. His startup, E Ink, manufactured the digital-ink technology that readers like Amazon's Kindle use. In 2009, the Harvard Business School graduate sold his startup to a company in Taiwan that makes the Kindle displays for about $500 million.

Since then, Wilcox has enjoyed a successful second act as a general partner at Pillar VC, where he invests in pre-seed and seed-stage startups. Many of his investments are early-stage university spinouts, including Kula Bio and Zapata.

Key investments: Higharc, Kula Bio, Quadratic, Verve, Zapata

How to get his attention: "My main interest is helping people commercialize breakthroughs. Often that leads me to technical founders who want a collaborative-style investor with business and operating experience who can join the board at inception."

Krishna Yeshwant, GV

Why he's on the list: Yeshwant was on the fast-track to becoming a prominent tech entrepreneur after he sold two startups he founded to HP and Symantec. But he started exploring healthcare through medical research and took pre-med night classes. Yeshwant went to Harvard Medical School to see if he could fix parts of the healthcare system. Through his graduate work at a diagnostics company, he got connected with the investment team at Google. In 2008, he joined the company to help spin up its debut venture fund and never left.

Key investments: Flatiron Health, One Medical, Verve Therapeutics 

How to get his attention: "I believe there is so much we can accomplish if we bring the technology and medical world together, and I'm always looking for investments that can transform the healthcare space — from care delivery, health IT, devices, diagnostics, and payor/provider to therapeutics. As a physician, empathy is a key quality I look for in healthcare technologists and founders. It's critical to understanding the complexities of the healthcare world."
