Inside the Post-Roe Future

Thirteen conversations from the front lines

Jun 18, 2022

A tension has filled the air since a Politico leak revealed in May that the Supreme Court had voted to overturn Roe v. Wade. That reality has now set in with the publication of the Court’s June 24 opinion in Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization.

In the days and weeks that followed the leak, Insider turned to people in the thirteen “trigger law” states  where abortion will now be criminalized automatically. We wanted to find out how people there were faring, what fears were keeping them up at night, and how they thought their lives would change after the ruling.

The cast of characters that is often evoked when talking about abortion is tiny: a pregnant person and their doctor. But far more people depend on abortion. Those who spoke with Insider come from different places, generations, and faiths; they have varying lived experiences and histories. Several believe we are set to return to a time when back-alley abortions were common. Most are scared — for their patients or their children or the future. And many worry the gutting of Roe is only the beginning.

“There is no ‘little’ erosion of rights,” said Leshawn Wyatt, a pharmacist living in Texas. “Any time you start taking away someone’s fundamental right to choose how they’ll live their life, it’s only a matter of time before your whole entire life is subject to somebody else’s decision.”

Responses have been edited and condensed for clarity.

South Dakota

“Outlawing abortion doesn’t end abortion. It just ends safe abortions,” said Marvin Buehner, a provider working in Rapid City, South Dakota.
Photo by: Eunice Straight Head for Insider

Marvin Buehner, 64

provider at Black Hills OBGYN in Rapid City, South Dakota

I've terminated pregnancies for concurrent rectal cancer, for history of repeated strokes during pregnancy, for cardiac disease that makes pregnancy highly lethal, for women who have uncontrollable hypertension, and recent kidney transplants. That goes away. Those are not options anymore. It's only to save the life of the mother. It puts all women and doctors who care for pregnant women with complications in a very precarious situation.

People are going to die. I know there will be self-induced abortions. Even in my practice now, with as difficult as abortion is to get, I’ve had several patients that tried to induce their own abortion. One in particular I remember tried to stick a toothpick up through her cervix to induce a first-trimester abortion. And of course, it became infected and she was bleeding and got really sick.

That's what we have to look forward to. It’s that kind of thing only worse, because there's a lot worse things than toothpicks.

Texas

Leshawn Wyatt holds her daughter. “I would question anyone at the forefront of this drive to ban abortion, if they would feel the same way if it was their child,” she said.
Photo by: Callaghan O'Hare for Insider

Leshawn Wyatt, 44

pharmacist in Richmond, Texas

We are on the front line. Our job goes so far beyond counting pills, putting 'em in a bottle, and handing them out. People are going to be afraid, and they are going to come in and try to talk to us. There are plenty of patients that I have now that feel more comfortable talking to the pharmacist than they do the doctor. Perhaps it's accessibility. We're a captive audience. We're free.

It's definitely going to make our jobs more difficult. What you're also going to see is that collision between your personal feelings, as far as whether you feel like this law is moral or if it's constitutional.

I always thought that abortion was wrong. That's what I was raised to believe. At 44, I became a parent much later than I ever expected I would. But one of the things I can tell you is that it 100% changed my attitude about abortion. I realized that parenting, even when you desperately want to do it like I did, it's a very difficult thing.

Mississippi

The Jackson Women’s Health Organization in Mississippi is now at the center of the Supreme Court’s ruling. Michelle Colon has long been active in the state’s reproductive rights space.
Photo by: Rory Doyle for Insider

Michelle Colon, 47

abortion-rights advocate in Jackson, Mississippi

It's beyond crazy. It's beyond insane. It's beyond living in a Twilight Zone. The closest clinic for Mississippi would be Florida. I'm afraid that abortion funds and practical support funds, especially the local ones, are not going to have enough money. And they're going to have to tell people no and turn people away. My biggest fear is that we're not going to be able to help everyone.

I do the work that I do because I am a Black woman. I care about the Black community. I don't want to see another Black child being motherless or parentless because the state doesn't give a damn about them.

I just remember folks patted me on my head saying: “Oh, you're overreacting. Oh, that's just Mississippi. That's a red state.” Well, guess what? Now, 26 other states in this country are going to know what it feels like to be a woman, a pregnant person, in Mississippi.

Oklahoma

Iman Alsaden works with patients in Arkansas, Kansas, and Oklahoma.
Photo by: Diane Durant for Insider

Iman Alsaden, 34

OBGYN and medical director for Planned Parenthood Great Plains in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma

If you have a uterus, this is disgusting and cruel and shocking.

I see patients now, in this post-Roe future world that I'm practicing in, and those are the patients that I see that can make it to me. And there must be thousands of other patients that can't. I think a lot of people will just be forgotten about.

I think we're in a different situation than we were before Roe, where we now have a very safe alternative to a procedure, which is the abortion pill. And I think you'll see more and more self-managed abortion, which has been proven time and time again to be very safe.

I do worry that as more people are self-managing their abortions, people will continue to be prosecuted for ending their pregnancies. I worry about the criminalization of patients.

North Dakota

Heidi Selzler-Echola worked at the Red River Women’s Clinic, an abortion provider in North Dakota, for six years. She still refers patients there, but said the potential end of Roe would change that.
Photo by: Andrea Ellen Reed for Insider

Heidi Selzler-Echola, 38

women’s health nurse practitioner who helps run a reproductive and LGBTQ health clinic in Fargo, North Dakota

It's common knowledge among people who work in women's health that criminalizing abortion doesn't end abortion. We'll see a lot more people who are trying to self-induce abortions through various ways and ending up in emergency departments.

A lot of our transgender patients who are coming in are talking about it, and they're expressing a lot of anxiety over it. Even though it doesn't necessarily impact them, per se, about getting pregnant, they have expressed fears that when Roe v. Wade is overturned, the next focus on healthcare is going to be trans rights.

Living in a conservative state where we have seen — especially in the past few years — attacks on transgender individuals and gay marriage, when Roe v. Wade overturns, that is the natural next step that some political parties will move towards. That’s the next step of the overall grand scheme of bodily autonomy and patient's right to privacy and all of those things.

Kentucky

Mashayla Hays attended law school at a time when Roe v. Wade was still intact. Now, she’s grappling with a future for herself and her clients without it.
Photo by: Stacy Kranitz for Insider

Mashayla Hays, 29

legal counsel at The Lawyering Project in Louisville, Kentucky

I graduated law school in 2018. I learned constitutional law in a way in which we have these certain rights, and so it’s been an experience to think about what it would be like for those rights not to exist.

My biggest fear is the folks who will be forced to stay pregnant. Maternal health is a big piece of why I entered this movement. Kentucky statistics right now show that Black and brown people are three to four times more likely to die than their white counterparts when giving birth, and it’s a real reality.

There’s people every day that we walk past in the grocery store who are very much plagued with these realities, and that includes myself. So when we’re talking about what it means to force people to stay pregnant, and you couple that with the maternal mortality rates that we have here in Kentucky, and then you think about the rural counties as well and their access to healthcare just in general, it’s really scary to think about.

Wyoming

Kristen Schwartz said that domestic abusers often use sex and pregnancy to control their partners. Without abortion access, their situations will become much more dire.
Photo by: Caleb Alvarado for Insider

Kristen Schwartz, 40

executive director at Wyoming Coalition Against Domestic Violence and Sexual Assault in Laramie, Wyoming

It is just a harsh reality that sex is used to control domestic-violence survivors, that a lot of sexual assaults are never reported. So even these exceptions for sexual-assault survivors likely will not help actual survivors who are assaulted by somebody that they know, somebody that they love, somebody that they’re afraid of, somebody that has power over them.

I had heart failure when I started going into labor with my third child. The entire time the doctors were saying to me, ‘No more babies.’ My husband immediately scheduled an appointment for a vasectomy. But if I was with a man who was like, ‘No, I’m not doing anything, I’m not taking those precautions’ — I don’t know.

I worry about the domestic-violence survivor who hasn’t been able to leave because she has children in the house and her partner is using sex and pregnancy to manipulate and control.

I’ve seen so many women making these hard choices about what I do to protect my children versus protecting myself. It’s going to make it even harder.

Tennessee

Amelia Edelman previously received an abortion in Nashville in 2020 amid both the pandemic and a heartbeat bill introduced by Tennessee’s governor, Bill Lee. She gave birth to a son, pictured next to her, in April 2022.
Photo by: Kristine Potter for Insider

Amelia Edelman, 36

editorial strategist in Nashville, Tennessee

In the summer of 2020, I was in a newish relationship. I had been a single mom for a long time and I found out I was pregnant. We feel like abortions are something that just irresponsible teens need. But I'm already a mother.

I go in for my first appointment. I'm supposed to come back in 48 hours, and within those 48 hours, Gov. Bill Lee moved forward the bill with a heartbeat ban. I couldn't go back. I immediately thought, well, this is awful and I hate Tennessee, but I'm going to fly home to New York where I'm from and get an abortion. Ultimately, the ACLU got the bill struck down. I was able to get the abortion back at the same clinic.

There was a young girl at the clinic. They were counseling her through looking at bus schedules to see if she could get to Atlanta or Louisville by herself. She might have been 16. I think about her every day. I'm confident that because the bill was overturned, she did get the care she needed, but that's the type of person that this affects the most.

Louisiana

Lexi Erdheim, who has relocated to North Carolina, was until very recently a rabbi in New Orleans, Louisiana.
Photo by: Erika Ramirez for Insider

Lexi Erdheim, 31

rabbi in New Orleans, Louisiana

As a rabbi, I feel like one of my strongest callings is to ensure a world that is just and to work towards a world that enables every human being to enjoy justice. So a future without Roe, for me, is a world that is significantly more unjust.

We know there's no single faith argument on reproductive rights. There's no single faith voice that should have a monopoly on this conversation. The fact that one perspective can become the law is particularly terrifying, and especially for the Jewish population.

In Judaism, when we're talking about life, we're talking about existing life. We're talking about the life of the mother, of the woman, of the pregnant person. The preservation of human life is my most sacred task. As we move forward into the unknown that lays ahead of us, I am preparing myself.

Utah

Keaton Anderson was raised in the Mormon Church, but left when he was 24. His upbringing “hugely influences” how he thinks about dating and sex.
Photo by: Niko Krivanek for Insider

Keaton Anderson, 33

musician in Midvale, Utah

If I was a woman, I would be almost packing my bags. I could see this being my final straw.

Maybe a year ago, I hooked up with this girl and even though that didn't result in a pregnancy, because I didn't use protection, I was panicking every day. Every minute of every day for the next six weeks. I was such a wreck and I was not even at risk of carrying it inside my body. I’ve got to imagine it's worse for a woman who feels like that and then has to see news that limits her choices in that regard. That's scary. My whole life long people have been talking about Roe v. Wade, and “there's so many decades of precedent, and who's to say if it ever really is going to happen.” But now it's like, all it takes is just a majority on the Supreme Court. That's what they were waiting for, and that's what happened almost the minute that happened.

Idaho

Tess Howell and her partner frequently discussed whether they wanted to have kids. They ultimately decided they both wanted each other, rather than a child, to stay as the most important person in their lives.
Photo by: Margaret Albaugh for Insider

Tess Howell, 35

accountant in Sandpoint, Idaho

The medical providers here get a lot of hate from pro-life people for providing any sort of service like that. My doctor doesn't prescribe the abortion pill because she had protesters showing up to her house.

It’s the fear of having to be secretive about what you need, and if you do have to go to a different state, you can't tell your family or friends to talk about what you're going through, because you don't know who might know.

And all the potential for states to set those really bizarre laws where you can turn someone in who got an abortion and get a reward. In my day-to-day life, I encounter a lot of people who have very different political beliefs than me, and most of the time it doesn't matter. But it feels like that would be a huge division, and you'd have to be really careful about who knows your information.

Missouri

Megan Ellyia Green recently underwent fertility treatments, a decision that she said gave her more resolve to fight for people who may not have the means or ability to access reproductive healthcare.
Photo by: Joe Martinez for Insider

Megan Ellyia Green, 38

ward 15 alderwoman in St. Louis, Missouri

I think the first thing we have to be clear about is seven in 10 people in Missouri do not support the bans. They don’t support criminalization of abortion, and I would anticipate that in the city, particularly in the area that I represent, it’s even greater. So we really will have a state government that is acting counter to the majority.

I represent a more affluent constituency who probably is still going to find their ways to have access to abortion care. But we know that low-income folks, predominantly Black and brown individuals, often have larger barriers in order to access care, and so we’re going to see a disparity in how the implications end up impacting our community.

I think we need to investigate or put pressure on prosecutors. Even if it becomes criminalized, prosecutorial discretion still exists, and so how do we put pressure on prosecutors to agree to not prosecute people who have attempted to seek an abortion, had an abortion, or have aided somebody in accessing an abortion?

Arkansas

For Joa Morelli, a teenager, the consequences of the Supreme Court’s decision might mean a young adulthood without abortion access.
Photo by: Rana Young for Insider

Joa Morelli, 17

high-school student in Eureka Springs, Arkansas

I’m scared for myself and I’m scared for the young girls that don’t have the education that should be given to them about this problem. Teen pregnancy, STDs, STIs, that’s all going to skyrocket. I expect there to be a lot of protesting. I think there’s already a protest happening at the capitol as we speak. I think it’s going to be really bad.

A lot of our legislation is made up of old conservative men, so I don’t think there’s going to be any sympathy. I’m scared that my legislators won’t see the impact it’s having on the people they’re supposed to be looking out for. I’m scared for all of the women that are being mistreated. I’m scared for all the transgender men. I’m just scared that this will continue and become a modern-day “Handmaid’s Tale.” It’s terrifying. I really never thought that it would come to this.
