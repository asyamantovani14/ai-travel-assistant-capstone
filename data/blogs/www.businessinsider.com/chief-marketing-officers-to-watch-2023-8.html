    CMOs to Watch: Meet the top marketers recently tapped by Delta, Glossier, and others to stoke demand and capture new opportunities

    • Insider identified the chief marketing officers to watch in 2023.
    • They're first-time and newly named CMOs at top brands such as General Motors and TikTok.
    • Our list recognizes the newest marketing chiefs rethinking the status quo and reshaping marketing.

    Chief marketing officers are always under pressure. As parts of the economy slowly return to normal, marketing chiefs must capture the growth opportunity, while justifying their budgets and making sure their brands are capitalizing on new channels such as TikTok and streaming TV. 

    And many brands, including AB InBev, have recently found themselves stuck between a rock and a hard place, as their embrace of inclusivity has led to right-wing boycotts that have affected sales.

    While the chief marketer's tenure has always been among the shortest in the C-suite, it can be a path to the very top of the corporate ladder. For instance, Taco Bell's global chief brand officer, Sean Tresvant, is set to become the company's CEO next year.

    Though turnover remains high, there will always be a crop of newly minted chief marketers poised to bring their companies to new heights. With our fifth annual "CMOs to Watch" list, Insider recognizes 25 top marketers who have stepped into the role in the past year, based on dozens of nominations and our own reporting. Many of these CMOs find themselves competing in the same industries, such as Sephora and Ulta, as well as General Motors and Mercedes-Benz.

    Not all are CMOs in name, but all bring proven experience to help companies tackle some of the biggest marketing challenges, including finding ways to market efficiently on a global scale, helping smaller brands scale, and executing major brand relaunches.

    They include Kraft Heinz's Diana Frost, who is returning the food brand to growth; The Knot's Jenny Lewis, who is leading an ambitious brand relaunch; and Glossier's Kleona Mack, who's helping the company move beyond direct-to-consumer sales.

    See who else made the list, in alphabetical order by last name.

    Zena Arnold, US chief marketing officer, Sephora

    Continuing to foster growth and expansion

    Arnold, a former PepsiCo and Kimberly-Clark exec, came on board as Sephora's US CMO this summer. Her remit spans performance marketing, creative marketing, integrated marketing, external communications, and loyalty, Ad Age reported.

    Sephora was a key growth driver for its parent company, LVMH, in its most recent quarter, when it continued to pick up market share in several global regions. Sephora is on track to deliver $13 billion in worldwide revenue this year, Bloomberg reported last month, citing people familiar with the matter.

    It's been boosted by widening distribution, such as hundreds of store openings inside Kohl's locations. The Wall Street Journal said in May that those shops were Sephora's top-performing locations and expected to generate $2 billion in revenue for Kohl's by 2025.

    Arnold said she planned to continue making diversity, equity, and inclusion a cornerstone of its marketing. This month, for example, Sephora announced its first music collective, designed to support and amplify underrepresented and emerging artists via Sephora's social platforms.

    Looking ahead, LVMH is giving Sephora the spotlight as part of its recently announced sponsorship of the Paris 2024 Olympic Games. Sephora will be a partner for the Olympic torch relay. Additionally, SEPHORiA: House of Beauty returns for its fifth year in the company's first-ever hybrid format, including a live ticketed event in New York City and a free global virtual experience in select markets in September.

    "Sephora's success is defined by a variety of factors that continue to drive our business and brand," Arnold told Insider via email. "One of the most important things that I'm focusing on for long term growth in the next 12 months is to be the retailer of choice among customer growth segments like Gen Z." 

    Michelle Crossan-Matos, chief marketing officer, Ulta Beauty

    Driving beauty sales through a loyalty program

    Crossan-Matos is using social media and a loyalty program of 41 million people to better connect with beauty enthusiasts.

    She joined Ulta Beauty in January and is responsible for keeping the brand growing in the competitive beauty-retail space. For Ulta Beauty's bi-annual sales event, 21 Days of Beauty, Crossan-Matos and her team spun up email and social-media marketing to surprise customers with an extra day of sales, which resulted in Ulta Beauty's largest year of sales, according to the brand. She also helped promote Ulta Beauty's three-week-long Big Summer Beauty Sale with TikTok content and affiliate-marketing programs for influencers.

    Crossan-Matos uses data and insights to shape Ulta Beauty's marketing as well as its ad-sales division that sells ads for beauty brands to promote their products.

    She's also building out a network of influencers for Ulta Beauty that includes store employees and shoppers.

    Before Ulta, Crossan-Matos worked in marketing roles at Samsung, Vertu, and Procter & Gamble.

    Norm de Greve, chief marketing officer, General Motors

    Boosting marketing efficiency

    De Greve, a veteran CMO, started his tenure as General Motors' marketing chief at the end of July. He's stepping in after Deborah Wahl, GM's previous CMO, "elected to retire" in March, Ad Age reported.

    He has a big task ahead of him. GM CEO Mary Barra said during the company's latest earnings call, in July, that the automaker had good traction with its new vehicles, including the high-end GMC Sierra AT4 and Denali pickups. De Greve will have to keep that momentum going and support GM's vehicle launches in growing segments, such as the rebuilt Chevrolet Traverse, which goes into production later this year. GM is also chasing Tesla's lead in electric vehicles, with offerings including the GMC Hummer EV, the Cadillac Lyriq, and the revamped Chevrolet Bolt. 

    He's also meant to take "a fresh review of our spending and put us on a course to deliver world-class levels of marketing efficiency," Barra said. 

    De Greve does have a history of installing innovative marketing programs. At his gig leading marketing for the drugstore giant CVS, he helped the company pitch itself as a destination for beauty products, adapted to shifts in consumer behavior that emerged amid the pandemic, and oversaw its retail-media ad platform.

    He may have to be extremely nimble at GM. Despite the company's recent growth, it's facing some challenges that could threaten the rollout of GM vehicles. These include delays from an automation supplier that are keeping GM from ramping up production of EVs and contentious negotiations with the autoworkers' union.

    Michelle Froah, global chief marketing officer, ETS

    Transforming education amid the rise of AI and new technologies

    Froah is bringing a consumer-marketing mindset to ETS, the educational-measurement and -assessment private nonprofit. The former MetLife, Samsung Electronics, Kimberly-Clark, and Procter & Gamble marketer joined in May with big ambitions.

    "If we succeed in transforming ETS, we succeed in transforming education," Froah told Insider.

    One of the reasons Froah was attracted to the role, she said, was that its wide remit reflected how the CMO role was changing. Besides marketing, her purview encompasses e-commerce, philanthropy, customer insights, and product innovation. 

    Her global marketing and innovation group is new to the organization. Alongside driving more quality engagement with its marketing, she wants ETS to have a bigger influence on social issues that have recently come into focus, such as the teacher crisis, a gap in governments and nations being able to support their citizens, and people having less access to global mobility.

    Froah is also thinking carefully about the rise of artificial intelligence and other tech tools, along with the need for more comprehensive durable skills beyond cognitive ones and a shift in how ETS can measure and value the skills that matter — such as empathy and collaboration.

    "Our global transformation has to be focused on leveraging those technologies today delivering for consumers to produce real insightful solutions to help consumers unlock their real potential," she said.

    Diana Frost, North America chief growth officer, Kraft Heinz

    Returning 'anemic' brands to growth

    Not long after Miguel Patricio became Kraft Heinz's CEO in 2019, he set out a bold ambition as part of the consumer-packaged-goods giant's multiyear turnaround plan: Make Kraft Heinz the best marketer in the world.

    Frost, who joined Kraft Heinz in 2020 and was named chief growth officer for North America in September, quickly established the company as a marketing leader across its more than 70 brands. Kraft Heinz brands were short-listed 83 times at Cannes Lions 2023, winning 13 bronze, five silver, and three gold lions. It was also named one of Fast Company's most innovative companies this year.

    "I'm so proud of Cannes this year. Yes, the awards, but more importantly, the amount of partners and talent and people that now want to talk to us because we're showing up in a different way in culture," Frost told Insider.

    Standout recent campaigns have included Heinz's #KetchupBoatGuy. After news reports detailed a man who had survived on ketchup for 24 days after his boat was swept out to sea, Heinz started an international search to find him and buy him a new boat. 

    This year, Frost is looking forward to launching a grilled-cheese product that cooks in a microwave in 60 seconds and new products across the Mexican category.

    Importantly for a chief growth officer, Kraft Heinz has returned to growth. The company's net sales increased 2.6% in its second quarter, while net profit rose 277% versus the prior year.

    "For many years, we were in an organization that was known for cost cutting. When you're in a role like this and coming in as chief growth officer when your brands have been anemic, what's changed is an intentionality. The ambition of our company changed," Frost said.

    Frost reorganized the growth function to comprise the insights, media, brand-communications, digital-experience, and innovation teams. She describes herself as the "lead integrator" between those teams across the organization and outside partners.

    Tammy Henault, chief marketing officer, the NBA

    Increasing viewership of basketball games

    Henault is a former TV-marketing exec who took over the top marketing role at the NBA in November to drive viewership and grow the brand.

    She previously worked at Paramount leading marketing for the streaming units Paramount+ and CBS All Access. That experience will help her as the NBA looks for new direct-to-consumer distribution deals and products. One such product is the NBA app, which houses content and the NBA League Pass streaming service. 

    The first campaign under Henault's leadership was rolled out in May, "We Are All in the Finals" and features celebrities such as the South Korean rapper Suga and the TV host Jimmy Kimmel, as well as the former NFL stars Eli and Peyton Manning. The actor John Malkovich narrates the TV ad.

    Another campaign on social media followed the Larry O'Brien trophy — which is awarded to the NBA Finals champion — through famous sporting events like the Kentucky Derby and Westminster Kennel Club before arriving at the first game of the finals.

    This fall, Henault will begin marketing the NBA In-Season Tournament, a new competition that will pair up athletes across all 30 NBA teams. It's aimed at driving more interest in the sport.

    Brad Hiranaga, chief brand officer, Cotopaxi

    Bringing big brand marketing to an upstart

    After four years serving as the CMO at the CPG giant General Mills, where Hiranaga oversaw a 250-strong marketing organization for brands such as Cheerios and Nature Valley, he moved in November to a very different company: the DTC outdoor-apparel and -gear manufacturer Cotopaxi. 

    The company is known for the vibrant, multicolored aesthetic of its outerwear and bags. It also distinguishes itself for being mission-driven to get people to do good, Hiranaga told Insider.

    "We believe adventure creates compassion," he said. "Wherever you go, you meet different people and different cultures."

    He added: "General Mills had brands that had purpose at its center, but it's nowhere near where you're building something new and determined to make a change that's much bigger than the company." 

    Hiranaga is tapping his experience as a big-brand marketer to channel some of Cotopaxi's experimental creativity and scale it out. For instance, he and his team are expanding Cotopaxi's loyalty program and building an upcycling initiative. The loyalty program has been focused on driving transactions, but Hiranaga is trying to infuse it with Cotopaxi's purpose-driven messaging.

    "We're still developing this, but we want to build a badging system with bigger tiers of loyalty if you're, say, volunteering," he said. 

    The company's upcycling program is set to launch next year. Cotopaxi has a line of bags, Del Día, made from remnant material, but the new upcycling program is intended to encompass the entire business.

    Under Hiranaga, Cotopaxi also plans on making a splash at even more cultural touchstones. The company will be the outdoor sponsor at next year's Sundance Film Festival, and he's finalizing a fall campaign called "Your Bag, Your Story," which will launch through public-relations and live events, such as the All Things Go music festival in Washington, DC.

    "The idea is every bag is made from different parts and is unique, just like the person who's carrying it," Hiranaga said. 

    Kate Jhaveri, global head of marketing, TikTok

    Fostering positive brand sentiment amid surging growth

    TikTok has enjoyed the kind of meteoric growth and unaided brand awareness that would make most marketers envious. But it's also firmly in the minds of regulators and parents, who are concerned about its head company's ties to China and the influence the app may be having on children and society. (TikTok has always said that it hasn't shared data with the Chinese government and wouldn't do so.)

    Jhaveri, a former NBA CMO, joined TikTok in November in the top consumer-marketing job at the company. The role had been vacant since the departure of Nick Tran in January 2022. She also has extensive experience at tech companies, having held marketing roles at Twitch, Twitter, Meta, and Microsoft.

    She told Insider her focus for the next 12 months would be on driving the continued global growth of the app and fostering positive sentiment toward the TikTok brand. One way TikTok seeks to do this through its marketing is by celebrating positive communities that have grown on TikTok, such as #BookTok and #Gaming.

    In June, for example, TikTok partnered with Unilever to sponsor and curate cleaning content through the #CleanTok community.

    Elsewhere, TikTok is wooing brands and creators to try its in-app shopping feature, Shop, as it looks to make e-commerce a more central part of its business.

    "We're committed to highlighting the talent, trends, and joy that emerge every day on TikTok," Jhaveri said.

    Ariel Kelman, chief marketing officer, Salesforce

    Helping stabilize Salesforce after a year of chaos

    Kelman is one of several top-level execs who returned to Salesforce in June after the massive exodus that gutted the company's executive ranks last year.

    From 2005 to 2011, Kelman oversaw marketing for individual Salesforce products, including its app-development platform. His second stint brings much greater responsibility — he'll be overseeing the company's global marketing strategy.

    Kelman last served as Oracle's CMO, a role in which he oversaw a 2,000-person marketing org and brokered expensive sports sponsorships. He left abruptly in June after a dispute around budgets, sources told Insider. Shortly afterward, Oracle underwent massive staff cuts.

    Salesforce has been tested as well and was hit hard after corporate clients cut spending on enterprise software. 

    Kelman joined Salesforce just a few months after it cut many sales and marketing staffers, part of the tech giant's plan to winnow its ranks by 10%. The company is still in the early stages of transforming itself, CEO Marc Benioff said during its May earnings call.

    Kelman will be one of the many execs responsible for helping right the ship.

    He must develop strategies that continue to persuade Salesforce's corporate clients to continue buying its software — after it raised prices on some of its key cloud products for the first time in seven years, according to Reuters.

    The company is already trying to take advantage of the rise of generative AI and has talked about its work bringing the tech to its products, such as its core customer-relationship-management solution, its messaging tool Slack, and its data-visualization tool Tableau. 

    Jeff Klein, chief marketing officer, Popeyes

    Punching above its weight class to keep the brand in popular culture

    Under Klein's leadership since joining last year, Popeyes has sought to punch above its weight class in awareness by producing marketing that permeates popular culture. 

    Popeyes had the "right raw ingredients" to be a top brand, but it lacked a few things, Klein told Insider.

    "There wasn't a foundational brand positioning that was consistent across the country, and in my mind, we had a dated media plan in terms of a very traditional tonnage that was very heavy on TV," he said.

    Popeyes unveiled its new brand positioning and corresponding campaign, "We Don't Make Sense, We Make Chicken," in April. The work, its first from its new agency McKinney, was designed to emphasize the brand's New Orleans roots and unique but painstaking Southern cooking techniques — such as marinating its chicken for 12 hours and hand-tossing it 20 times to ensure the batter gets into all the nooks and crannies.

    After about five years of using user-generated content, the brand has shifted to more high-quality creative that's designed to be more memorable and have a more consistent narrative across all its media.

    Other standout work from this year included signing a deal in February with Dieunerst "Popeyes Kid" Collin, who went viral as a child in a video that showed him looking confused while standing in line at a Popeyes restaurant. Collin is now a college football player who has a name, image, and likeness deal with Popeyes after he rallied fans on social media to get the company's attention.

    After that campaign's success, Popeyes worked with Collin on a Super Bowl promotion.

    It's important for Popeyes to leverage viral activations.

    "To keep the brand in popular culture, we need to punch above our weight because we don't have the budgets of McDonald's or KFC," Klein said.

    Daniel Lee, chief marketing officer, Harman

    Driving new growth across a large portfolio

    Lee came on board as the CMO of the Samsung subsidiary Harman in December. He was tasked with building marketing programs that would raise brand awareness and revenue-driving growth of the speakers and audio products of Harman's brands, including JBL, Harman Kardon, AKG, Revel, and Mark Levinson.

    One of Lee's first jobs was consolidating the different marketing disciplines spread across the company, bringing those initiatives into the agency Havas Group. The goal was to revitalize marketing for each of those brands. For example, JBL, which sells premium speakers and headphones, needed to spark more customer buying. Lee also led a large-scale media campaign for Harman Kardon to establish the brand and celebrate its 70th anniversary.

    Lee is also leading a big push to attract Gen Z consumers by pushing the brands into sustainable initiatives. 

    Finally, Lee is fueling Harman's collaboration with the automotive industry. He helped create a multiyear marketing partnership between Harman Automotive and Ferrari by developing a media campaign that established Harman Automotive as a Scuderia Ferrari partner for the 2023 Formula 1 season. This campaign has been a boon to Harman's burgeoning automotive-tech business by establishing its presence in the auto industry.

    Marian Lee, chief marketing officer, Netflix

    Turning the streaming giant into a marketing machine

    Lee is responsible for marketing all of Netflix's series and films, consumer products, live experiences, and partnerships.

    She was promoted to CMO in early 2022 and is on Insider's "CMOs to Watch" for the second consecutive year because she's marketing the streaming giant through some significant changes, including new restrictions on password sharing, a nascent ad-supported tier, and heightened competition from streaming upstarts.

    In the past year, Lee has worked on some of Netflix's highest-profile campaigns promoting original shows. For the popular "Bridgerton" prequel, "Queen Charlotte," Lee created a global campaign with immersive experiences and pop-up orchestras, including a music video with Alicia Keys for her song "If I Ain't Got You" that has been watched more than 30 million times.

    And for the movie "Extraction 2," interactive billboards in New York and Los Angeles made it look like sweat was dripping from the actor Chris Hemsworth's face.

    Lee also works on pop-up experiences and partnerships. Netflix has created more than 35 experiences in 18 cities for fans to connect in real life for shows such as "Stranger Things." Lee's work also includes merchandise and consumer products — including working with the fashion brand Lacoste on a line of clothing inspired by Netflix hit shows such as "Sex Education" and "Lupin."

    Later this year, Lee is set to help market anticipated films including "Heart of Stone," "Rebel Moon," and "The Archies."

    Melody Lee, chief marketing officer, Mercedes-Benz USA

    Marketing the shift to electric

    Lee joined Mercedes-Benz in July as the automaker accelerated its pace toward a "zero-emissions future." It has about a 14% share of electric vehicles in its product portfolio in the US, according to the company, and is investing in charging infrastructure and marketing to help drive EV production.

    Two big factors for Mercedes-Benz, and other EV makers, to overcome are consumer hesitancy about charging availability and the length of time they can drive before the battery is drained. Part of Lee's remit will be to promote messages about the convenience of its charging network. In January, the company announced ambitious plans to launch a high-power charging network across North America, Europe, China, and other markets, beginning in the US and Canada.

    Mercedes-Benz has also been looking to enhance the in-car experience, this year striking partnerships with Google and TikTok. The company, which is a highly active sponsor of e-sports, also recently renewed its partnership with the games developer Riot Games.

    Lee joined Mercedes-Benz from the design company MillerKnoll. She also has experience in the automotive space as a former exec of Cadillac, where she developed and launched the Book by Cadillac subscription service and its "Dare Greatly" marketing campaign.

    Jenny Lewis, chief marketing officer, The Knot Worldwide

    Architecting a major brand relaunch

    Lewis is a first-time CMO, having cut her teeth heading up marketing in North America for Uber. 

    In April 2022, she got the top marketing job at The Knot Worldwide and is now helping it execute its biggest campaign ever as it seeks to build out its brand and drive awareness about all the ways it can help people plan their weddings. 

    In her first three months on the job, Lewis reorganized the marketing team so that the different departments would work together.

    "We had performance in one corner doing their thing," she told Insider. "We leaned heavily on SEO. We weren't leaning hard into brand marketing. Product marketing was undeveloped. Email channels existed but weren't working as hard for us as they could be."

    After the marketing reorg, The Knot began its next phase: a formal relaunch of its brand that went live just two weeks ago. The Knot's previous branding had gone stale and generic, the company said, and its new look has pops of color and a focus on the diverse ways that people celebrate their love. The updated visuals were designed to make the wedding-planning process feel joyful again, Lewis said, as it had started to feel heavy and stressful to couples.

    As part of easing that burden, the new messaging also informs people of all the ways The Knot can help them plan their weddings. People know The Knot for its registry and inspirational articles but not its vendor marketplace, where people can find florists, planners, and other businesses that can help them plan.

    "It was really interesting seeing our biggest business driver had the lowest awareness," Lewis said. 

    Lewis is now executing on the third stage of The Knot's marketing transformation by drastically reinventing its media mix and investing in channels meant to reach a large audience. The Knot is focusing heavily on streaming-TV and online-video ads.

    "We just launched our first truly integrated marketing campaign across nine channels," she said.

    As part of this effort, The Knot is releasing its first national video campaign, which will air targeted spots on Hulu, Peacock, and Netflix. 

    Tressie Lieberman, chief marketing officer, Yahoo

    Injecting new life into the storied internet brand

    Lieberman joined Yahoo last month, just short of two years after Apollo completed its acquisition of what was then Verizon Media from Verizon, the telecommunications giant. 

    Under new leadership and now known simply as Yahoo, the storied online portal has said it's profitable and considering a return to the public markets.

    With the main bulk of cost cutting and restructuring out of the way, it's now Lieberman's role to reposition Yahoo as an internet brand of today, rather than one of yesteryear.

    "I have always been drawn to the Yahoo brand — not only for its strong legacy, but for its immense potential," Lieberman said in a release announcing her appointment.

    Lieberman — who has marketing experience from Chipotle, Snap Kitchen, Taco Bell, and Pizza Hut — is Yahoo's first CMO since 2015. She takes on responsibility for global marketing, brand positioning, customer engagement, and user acquisition across brands including Yahoo Finance, Yahoo Sports, AOL, and TechCrunch.

    It's likely she'll draw on her track record in the world of food marketing to unite the Yahoo brand with social-media and influencer culture. At Chipotle, for example, Lieberman and her team drove influencer collaborations on TikTok, and the chain was an early mover on newer platforms such as Roblox, Discord, and BeReal. She also launched the Chipotle Rewards loyalty program, which now has more than 35 million members.

    Fernando Machado, chief marketing officer, NotCo

    Making a startup a big name

    Machado is a longtime CMO who joined the foodtech company NotCo in April.

    He's tasked with making the AI-developed, plant-based food a mainstream name. In one example, NotCo works with brands such as Kraft Heinz to launch products including mayonnaise and cheese that Machado helps cobrand.

    He's also responsible for promoting NotCo's sustainability push and getting people to try its vegan products — even nonvegans.

    "All the products that we have are vegan, which doesn't mean that vegan is the main target audience," he told Brand Innovators in June. "Most of the volume that we sell is to people like me. I'm not vegan. I'm flexitarian."

    He also helped launch a social media, print, and billboard campaign this spring using AI to show how animals would look older if they weren't killed for food.

    Machado is a former CMO of Activision Blizzard and Restaurant Brands International and is known for developing creative campaigns for Burger King and Popeyes. He was behind campaigns such as Burger King's "Moldy Whopper" in 2020, which showed how the chain didn't use preservatives. He also helped steer a buzzy campaign around the launch of the Popeyes chicken sandwich in 2019.

    Kleona Mack, chief marketing officer, Glossier

    Elevating the beauty brand beyond DTC

    Mack, a former L'Oréal marketer, was promoted to CMO of Glossier in March, having previously been the senior vice president of global marketing. Her appointment was part of a rounding out of the company's c-suite, which included a new chief commercial officer and the company's executive creative director being promoted to chief creative officer.

    The 9-year-old brand, which became an overnight phenomenon in its early years, is looking to steady itself after its meteoric rise among 20- and 30-somethings began to stall more recently. 

    Glossier underwent two rounds of layoffs last year as it shifted the company away from being a pure-play direct-to-consumer seller and began leaning more into wholesale, through a partnership with the beauty retailer Sephora. The company has also redesigned its website, has opened a string of brick-and-mortar stores, and has been expanding into product lines including deodorant.

    In an interview with the beauty title Glossy, Glossier CEO Kyle Leahy in March described 2023 as a "transformational year" for the company as it focused on bringing the brand to more people.

    Mack has had an immediate influence since her promotion in launching Glossier's third grant program for Black-owned beauty businesses and expanding it to the UK. In March, Glossier also debuted its G Suit opaque lipstick. She described the launch as an "inflection point" for Glossier makeup in an interview with Elle — a move away from the "no makeup makeup" look.

    "Our real mission — the mission all along — is to develop products that work for you, not products that make you work for them," Mack told Elle. "We're not about a singular look."

    Brett O'Brien, chief marketing officer, Frito-Lay North America

    Driving double-digit growth across PepsiCo's snack brands

    O'Brien has been at PepsiCo for over two decades, having started as a summer intern when he was at business school. After he finished his program, he managed to land a full-time job, O'Brien said during an interview with the Georgetown Entertainment and Media Alliance in September.

    He was named CMO of Frito-Lay North America in June 2022, supervising marketing strategy for brands including Lay's, Cheetos, and Doritos. 

    He already helped make a splash earlier this year with two Super Bowl spots — one for Doritos and a second to introduce PopCorners as a new type of snack. The PopCorners spot notably used characters from "Breaking Bad," which resulted in significant chatter online.

    Frito-Lay said it led to a 514% increase in followers across social channels, 27% growth in household penetration year over year, and a 50% increase in US sales, compared with 2022.

    O'Brien also led what Frito-Lay called its "largest-ever investment in women's sports" by sponsoring the FIFA Women's World Cup. 

    In just his first year, O'Brien has helped Frito-Lay North America grow revenue by 16%, thanks to double-digit growth across Lay's, Doritos, Cheetos, SunChips, and PopCorners, Frito-Lay said. It was the unit that grew the most across PepsiCo's portfolio.

    Among O'Brien's biggest challenges as a marketer is figuring out how to navigate the changing media landscape.

    "Now you've got these walled environments with Netflix, YouTube, HBO Max. How do you insert brand messages the right way where people are spending their time?" he said during the Georgetown Entertainment and Media Alliance interview. "How do you manipulate the right messaging across multiple screens because that's the reality of how people are viewing content? And how does that content break through in a sea of content-filled environments?"

    Manu Orssaud, vice president of global marketing, Duolingo

    Turning 'unhinged' marketing into serious business results

    Orssaud, who joined the language-learning-app company in 2020, has been instrumental in scaling Duolingo's social strategy — leaning into memes and jokes that link its green Duo owl mascot with popular culture. He was promoted in September to lead all of the company's global marketing.

    He described Duolingo's marketing style as "unhinged." Take its April Fools' Day stunt with NBCUniversal's Peacock in which the pair announced a show called "Love Language," where 10 singles would share a house but none spoke the same language. The "contestants" would need to take Duolingo lessons and find a match, or be kicked out. "The color of your eyes is very similar to my father's nipples," one contestant says. 

    Orssaud told Insider he wanted to continue amping up its brand-building output — "the April Fools' campaign, 'Love Language': Imagine that on steroids" — as it broadened its offering as an education company, not just a language-learning app. Duolingo launched a math-learning product last year. A big Halloween campaign is also in the works.

    The 40-person Duolingo global marketing team is small relative to most big advertisers, but Orssaud said this enabled it to be more "scrappy."

    For example, when Warner Bros. and Mattel approached the company to use the Duolingo sound in the "Barbie" movie for a character learning Spanish, the team went to Los Angeles with Duo in a suit dressed as Barbie. An ad was shot in one day and ready to be played in theaters for the release.

    "If we laugh and find it funny as a team and feel in our gut this is right, we will execute things in three days," Orssaud said. 

    Duolingo's daily active users in the second quarter grew 62% from the prior-year ro 21.4 million.

    Lisa Marie Pillette, chief marketing officer, Fossil Group

    Keeping watches and accessories cool

    Fashion brands are battling to stay ahead of tech companies and others developing watches and accessories. Marie Pillette is a veteran retail-marketing exec who joined Fossil Group last year to tackle that challenge.

    She oversees marketing for the group's brands, including Fossil, Michele, and Skagen. Fossil Group also makes watches for brands such as Armani Exchange and Diesel.

    In November, Fossil is set to launch a new image, tone, and commercial aimed at people who may be familiar with Fossil's watches from the past. The goal is to help Fossil drive culture, she said.

    "We're a global accessories brand — we're not just your first watch," she told Insider. "I don't think a lot of people understood really what the brand stood for besides something that was a meaningful moment for them, not necessarily the urgency to go out and get something today."

    One way she's getting that across is by leaning heavily into collaborations with companies including Mattel and Disney to create themed products. Fossil recently rolled out a collection of pink watches, jewelry, and leather pieces inspired by the movie "Barbie." And for "Star Wars," Fossil created a collection of themed watches.

    She's also experimented with platforms such as TikTok and Lemon8 to develop filters and influencer-marketing programs. And for Diesel, Marie Pillette helped develop a metaverse program that housed concerts and events.

    Marie Pillette is also using AI to generate product copy and send personalized text messages.

    Before Fossil, Marie Pillette was the CMO of Casper and is credited with helping relaunch the mattress brand in 2021 as it expanded into products including pillows and blankets. In addition to Casper, she has worked at Lacoste, Ralph Lauren, and Levi Strauss.

    Lisa Roath, chief marketing officer, Target

    Growing sales as consumer spending decreases

    Roath is a longtime Target exec who was tapped for the CMO role in July. She's responsible for Target's creative, social media, paid media, and marketing strategy, as well as its retail experiences.

    She most recently was the senior vice president of food and beverage merchandising, helping to grow Target's digital grocery business to compete with digital and traditional players such as Instacart and Walmart. Since 2019, Target has grown food and beverage sales to more than $5 billion — partly from selling increasingly popular items including craft cocktails and sugar-free drinks.

    Roath has also worked on merchandising projects, including speeding up how long it takes to roll out and market a new product.

    She's taking over Target's marketing at a crucial time for the retailer as sluggish consumer spending led to flat first-quarter sales. And in June, Target faced backlash for selling LGBTQ+ merchandise, which led the retailer to remove some merchandise from its stores.

    Mark Shambura, chief marketing officer, Papa Johns

    Making the pizza chain more digital

    Shambura is using his experience in marketing fast-casual restaurants to shake up Papa Johns.

    He joined the chain in May from its rival MOD Pizza and is tasked with overseeing brand marketing and advertising. In addition, Shambura is in charge of the pizza chain's menu strategy, product innovation, and digital customer experience, including a loyalty program. 

    He brings analytical and data experience to juice up the marketing and is helping the chain develop social content on platforms such as Threads and TikTok. 

    "An immediate focus is that we remain committed to meeting our customers where they are by embracing the channels and platforms our customers are using," he told Insider via email. "We shouldn't be scared to explore and experiment with new ways of engaging."

    In 2021, he helped roll out MOD Pizza's first national advertising campaign, which highlighted the company's culture — a departure from ads for fast-casual restaurants that heavily promote products or promotions. Shambura also helped launch handheld pizzas at MOD Pizza and developed promotions for the chain, including a back-to-school deal that offered parents discounts on pizzas, salads, and desserts.

    In addition to MOD Pizza, Shambura previously worked in marketing at Chipotle.

    Leslie Sims, chief marketing officer, Impossible Foods

    Making plant-based meat mainstream

    Sims is tasked with making the buzzy plant-based meat alternative a more recognizable name.

    In January, she became Impossible Foods' first CMO and creative officer. She plans to ramp up its ad spend in the coming months. Up until now, most of Impossible's growth has come from partnerships with big names such as Kroger and Disney to make and sell food products, as well as word-of-mouth marketing. 

    The brand has 15% awareness in the US, according to Impossible Foods, meaning that Sims has a large mandate in front of her to grow recognition.

    She has rolled out two national marketing campaigns. A musical-themed ad featuring the Broadway star Noah Weisberg aired during the Tony Awards in June. It encouraged meat eaters to try Impossible products. And her second campaign highlights how plant-based meat doesn't have cholesterol, through dialogue between a burger made with animal meat and one made by Impossible.

    Sims brings loads of marketing experience with her from working at agencies including Deloitte Digital, Ogilvy, and Young & Rubicam and brands such as General Mills and Burger King.

    Alicia Tillman, chief marketing officer, Delta

    Helping drive loyalty and promote diversity as travel surges

    Tillman started as Delta's marketing chief in early June, and she brings a wealth of experience, both from other Fortune 500 companies and from startups.

    She was a high-level marketing exec at American Express for 11 years, according to her LinkedIn profile, and she was the enterprise-tech giant SAP's top marketer for four years, where she oversaw a marketing organization of 2,000 people.

    Tillman also has experience in roles tied directly to sales growth. Before joining Delta, she was the chief revenue officer at the fast-growing fintech startup Capitolis, where she oversaw its marketing and brand strategy, as well as led sales.

    At Delta, Tillman has oversight of all marketing and advertising activities, taking over duties from Tim Mapes, an SVP and the chief communications officer.

    Tillman will try to keep the airline giant's momentum going as travel continues to boom and as consumers book summer vacations in droves after the industry shut down early in the pandemic.

    "Air-travel demand is strong, and the consumer is in good financial shape, particularly the premium consumer base that we target," CEO Ed Bastian said during the company's earnings call in June.

    One key aspect of Tillman's strategy is the Faces of Travel program, where the airline is trying to increase representation in travel by using more diverse people in its images.

    "All of this happening at a time when our work to drive relevant, personalized experiences wherever you might engage with Delta is kicking into high gear to drive not just membership, but loyalty," Tillman told Insider in an email.

    Suzi Watford, SVP and chief growth officer, SiriusXM

    Setting up the brand for massive growth

    Much of SiriusXM's audience growth comes from the auto industry because the broadcasting service is baked into pretty much every new vehicle sold in America. But SiriusXM is about to mount its biggest bet to expand beyond that — and grow its millennial and Gen Z audience. 

    This is a huge initiative for Watford, the former Dow Jones CMO who became SiriusXM's first-ever chief growth officer in January.

    Watford's vast purview includes marketing and branding, as well as user acquisition, retention, and engagement, across SiriusXM streaming and its music-streaming service, Pandora. 

    SiriusXM has a lot riding on its massively rebuilt app with a new interface and capabilities, which is set to launch in November. Last quarter, the company devoted $9 million toward building the "Next Generation SiriusXM experience," according to its August earnings call. 

    CEO Jennifer Witz said during the company's January earnings call that this upgrade would drive new-user growth and retention and average revenue per user "in 2024 and beyond."

    "The funnel we have of people coming into SiriusXM remains incredibly strong, and our penetration of the car market is incredibly high," Watford told Insider.

    She plans to heavy up on ad campaigns toward the latter half of the year to promote the new product.

    "The opportunity is to increase that and introduce a whole new world of customers who might not have come yet through the car funnel," she said.

    She added: "Next Gen SiriusXM will be the true launch of where we next go with the brand."

    Watford is also building new distribution partnerships to attract younger, more diverse audiences. SiriusXM is hosting an upcoming Wu-Tang Clan concert for subscribers to celebrate the 50th anniversary of hip-hop. Watford also played a big part in a partnership with Walmart+, the retail giant's membership program, in which Walmart+ subscribers got a free six-month SiriusXM trial.
