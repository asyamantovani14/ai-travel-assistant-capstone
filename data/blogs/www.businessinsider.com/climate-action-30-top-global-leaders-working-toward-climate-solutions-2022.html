Action 30

30 of the top global leaders working toward climate solutions

Insider’s inaugural Climate Action 30 highlights 30 global leaders working toward climate solutions. Our list includes activists, influencers, academics, scientists, business executives, entrepreneurs, and public-sector and nonprofit leaders working to address the climate crisis through collectivism, community, and accountability.

Insider invited each honoree to share the progress they’re making to achieve their climate goals as well as the specific actions they think our readers can take to do the same.

To wrap up Climate Week NYC, Insider hosted a virtual event ​​with Climate Action 30 honorees about the role of global organizations in accelerating climate action.

Watch full event

Ayesha Barenblat

Founder and CEO, Remake

call to action

Paying living wages will dramatically slow down production and waste creation.
visit Ayesha Barenblat's instagram visit Ayesha Barenblat's twitter
Read more about Ayesha Barenblat

She/her

California, United States

Ayesha Barenblat is the founder and CEO of Remake, a not-for-profit network disrupting the $2.5 trillion fashion industry through education programs and activism. From Karachi, Pakistan, to San Francisco, Barenblat has forged an activist pipeline of 1,500 fashion insiders, designers, merchants, models, and garment makers.

“Our theory of change is if the women of color, who are fashion's most essential workers, are paid fairly and continue to make higher wages, that is the fastest way for fashion to address its climate impacts,” she told Insider.

Through her work on the Garment Worker Protection Act, Barenblat ensured that garment workers in California would be paid at least $14 an hour; before, they were often receiving $3 to $5 an hour. Now she’s helping push the law’s protections at the federal level, in the form of a bill called The Fabric Act.

When fashion brands canceled an estimated $40 billion worth of clothing orders during the pandemic, she mobilized the Remake community on social media to hold fashion companies such as Levi’s, H&M, and Zara accountable to paying garment workers.

“The impact on the people who are on the front lines of climate change, and the wage theft that this industry contends with, were going to be catastrophic, so our community mobilized,” she said. Brands like Gap, Zara, and Levi’s reversed their decisions, and garment workers continued to receive their wages.

Barenblat’s call to action: “Our overconsumption of disposable fashion is responsible for up to 4% of global emissions. Better pay for the 70 million mostly women of color who make our clothes around the world is the best way to address fashion’s staggering human-rights violations and climate impacts. Paying living wages will dramatically slow down production and waste creation.”

Mary Black

Raleigh City Council candidate; climate-justice organizer

call to action

Critical awareness of our role as architects of the future is essential to transforming our reality and averting the climate crisis.
visit Mary Black's instagram visit Mary Black's twitter
Read more about Mary Black

She/her

North Carolina, United States

A trailblazer, activist, and aspiring politician, Mary Black is looking to make demonstrable and even radical change by centering the voices of people who are often underrepresented or silenced, namely Black and Indigenous people, youth, people with lower incomes, and women.

She’s the creator of the Radical Futurist Lab and a candidate for Raleigh City Council — as well as the first Black woman to file to run in her district. She’s young, and she hasn’t held public office, but Black told Insider she believes she has the ideas and drive to implement real change in her city.

A longtime environmentalist and community organizer, Black currently serves on Raleigh’s Environmental Advisory Board and works full time as a narrative strategy and strategic communications manager with the Chisholm Legacy Project, a resource hub for Black climate justice leadership.

In her work and activism, Black said she has experienced the racism that pervades nonprofits working in environmentalism and sustainability. Black said that stepping into politics and becoming a decision-maker would allow her to bring about changes in Raleigh — such as sustainable design and economic and housing justice — that could be a model for other communities.

Black said it’s about “having a deep critical awareness of the role that we all play in creating a new alternative future,” whether that’s “a future without prison or a future without sexual assault, or a future without the climate crisis and racism.”

“We have to be very critical in understanding our role in that and be aware in the things we are doing,” she added.

Black’s call to action: “Critical awareness of our role as architects of the future is essential to transforming our reality and averting the climate crisis. Our struggle and structures must be rooted in community-led strategy, authentic connections, localized approaches, and personal and collective consciousness. We must go together, or we risk going nowhere at all.”

Amali de Alwis

CEO, Subak

call to action

If we’re saying we care about climate action, we have to care properly. Investment in the climate space cannot just be optimized for profit.
visit Amali de Alwis's twitter visit Amali de Alwis's linkedin
Read more about Amali de Alwis

She/her

London, United Kingdom

Amali de Alwis credits her love of building things — from shoes to business strategies to startups — for landing where she is today. As the CEO of Subak, the first tech accelerator for climate not-for-profits, de Alwis is helping scale up data-driven projects that otherwise might not get off the ground.

Banks, investors, and governments are spending hundreds of billions of dollars each year to reduce greenhouse-gas emissions. A lot of that money flows to companies trying to decarbonize while also making a profit. While that’s important, de Alwis said, solving some climate problems won’t deliver returns in the near future.

“Some of these problems don’t fit the venture-capital model,” she told Insider. “We need to work in the not-for-profit space to fill the gap. Some are struggling to scale, and I think that’s where we apply lessons from the tech world. How can we use data and technology to scale quickly and drive systems change, whether that’s through policy, educating the public, or changing behavior?”

Subak, founded in 2019 by Baroness Bryony Worthington, has provided more than $1.5 million in strings-free seed money to 15 startups and 20 individuals from countries including the UK, the US, the Democratic Republic of Congo, and Australia.

Ember, which was among the first not-for-profits in Subak’s accelerator, built the first open-source dataset of global power generation, mapping how coal moves from mines to power plants to steel manufacturers. Ember also influenced the UK government’s ban on gas boilers in new homes by 2025, de Alwis said.

De Alwis’ call to action: “As funders, if we’re saying we care about climate action, we have to care properly. Investment in the climate space cannot just be optimized for profit. We need patient and follow-on capital and robust data analysis to ensure we’re deploying money to the most critical climate areas, not just fashionable ones.”

Hervé Duteil

Chief sustainability officer, Americas, BNP Paribas

call to action

Profound changes cannot be easy — but done well, they will unlock a new era for societal progress.
visit Hervé Duteil's twitter visit Hervé Duteil's linkedin
Read more about Hervé Duteil

He/him

Paris, France

Hervé Duteil had a full-time job as a derivatives trader while working in his off-hours as a volunteer at Rikers Island and at a homeless shelter.

He said that gave him purpose as he sought ways to mesh his passion for community work to his job as a derivatives trader.

He used his financial background to accelerate sustainability efforts for clients, eventually becoming BNP Paribas’ first chief sustainability officer. The goal has been to make the finance industry more sustainable without sacrificing the optimization of funds.

When Duteil started this effort a decade ago, sustainability and discussions about combating climate change weren’t new, but talk about bringing it into the financial world wasn’t widespread. Duteil told Insider he’d been able to elevate the conversation, push large corporations to work harder on sustainability measures, and connect finance and sustainability.

“And all of a sudden, it became of interest to everyone, and even regulators and public entities understand that they can leverage this competition in the private sector around sustainability to try to advance sustainability goals in terms of where we're going for the future,” he said. “It’s a universal topic today.”

Duteil said that while the financial industry can feel somewhat disconnected from the real world, it contributes to societal impact — just like any other profession or industry.

Duteil’s call to action: “We have zero time to get to net zero and enter the second industrial revolution, but we can accelerate it. We need deep transformations, but we can finance them. Profound changes cannot be easy — but done well, they will unlock a new era for societal progress.”

Tobias Engelmeier

Cofounder and CEO, Village Data Analytics

call to action

Today, with respect to the SDGs, we are still flying blind. What doesn’t get measured doesn’t get done.
visit Tobias Engelmeier's twitter visit Tobias Engelmeier's linkedin
Read more about Tobias Engelmeier

He/him

Munich, Germany

Passionate about climate justice, Tobias Engelmeier wondered why the billions of people who contribute little to the climate crisis are often the most affected by it. Then he started working on a solution.

Engelmeier, who has a background in politics and economics, began working in Germany on renewable energy. He quickly realized that these issues weren’t just German — they were global. So he sought a deeper understanding of the issue in countries like India and China where the infrastructure wasn’t as well developed and where there was a chance to make development smarter and more sustainable.

Over time, Engelmeier evolved from thinking that the fix to the climate crisis would come from the political sphere. He’s now developing scalable responses that would work no matter the political or social framework.

Engelmeier, along with Nabin Raj Gaihre and Philippe Raisin, founded Village Data Analytics, a software company that uses data to identify opportunities for investments in solar energy or mini-grids in rural or remote areas.

It was born after a project with mini-grids in Papua New Guinea illustrated the need for a faster way to identify how to direct clean-energy options into remote areas. His team spent months there trying to identify sites — a time-consuming and expensive process.

“I have a strong sense of impatience,” he told Insider. “We were looking for ways to accelerate it.”

Engelmeier began working with the European Space Agency, which had data from satellites and other technology that proved useful in determining good sites to deliver infrastructure quickly and at scale. His company created tools and software that’s now used in some two dozen countries.

Engelmeier’s call to action: “Today, with respect to the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals, we are still flying blind. No one can tell you, for example, what the real status of electrification is. We need to build a strong data ecosystem for prioritizing, monitoring, and measuring investments into SDGs. What doesn’t get measured doesn’t get done.”

Lioz Etgar

Professor of chemistry, Hebrew University of Jerusalem

call to action

As the world endeavors to reach a low-carbon, circular economy, perovskite solar technologies must become the steadfast ally for global leaders.
visit Lioz Etgar's twitter visit Lioz Etgar's linkedin
Read more about Lioz Etgar

He/him

Jerusalem, Israel

Lioz Etgar was a postdoctoral researcher at EPFL, a technical university in Switzerland, when his team discovered a breakthrough in solar energy.

The researchers found a new way to use perovskite — a family of crystalline compounds that are naturally occurring on Earth — in solar cells to convert sunlight into energy. Perovskite has the potential to make solar panels more energy efficient and less expensive than the silicon-based panels that dominate the market. Perovskite is also transparent and flexible, meaning it holds promise for solar windows and wearable technology that powers itself.

“We realized we need to work with solid-state materials on top of the perovskite to keep it stable for a much longer time, and not with liquid,” Etgar said. “Since then, the rest is history.”

Now labs around the world are studying perovskite and testing various ways to use it in solar, though its use hasn’t been commercialized. Etgar leads a lab at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and has launched three early-stage startups he hopes to scale, including one that integrates a perovskite-based solar cell with smart devices to power them with indoor lighting. Separately, his teams are developing solar applications for windows so they can power buildings, as well as greenhouses with transparent solar-panel roofs that generate electricity and allow light to pass through so plants can grow.

“I really wanted my research to make an impact on the world,” Etgar said. “That’s what we are aiming for.”

Etgar’s call to action: “As the world endeavors to reach a low-carbon, circular economy, perovskite solar technologies must become the steadfast ally for global leaders. Decision-makers must be first to invest in transparent, printable hyper-effective solar panels with unlimited applications, which will broaden the scope of environmental solutions and propel our world towards its goals.”

Lauren Flanagan

Cofounder, chair, and CEO, Sesame Solar

call to action

We need to stop relying on fossil fuels to help us recover from these events, which are causing these natural disasters.
visit Lauren Flanagan's twitter visit Lauren Flanagan's linkedin
Read more about Lauren Flanagan

She/her

Michigan, United States

A Silicon Valley pioneer who worked with Steve Jobs in the early days of software startups, Lauren Flanagan is no stranger to living at the cutting edge of technology.

The CEO and cofounder of Sesame Solar, a company developing renewable Nanogrids that use solar energy and green hydrogen batteries to deliver power in emergency situations, is supercharging a shift away from the diesel-fueled generators used in hurricanes, droughts, wildfires and other weather crises.

“The status quo is sending in diesel generators or fossil-fuel-powered emergency offices, medical centers, or communication centers,” Flanagan told Insider. “And that only contributes to more environmental damage and helps create the conditions of climate change that caused the extreme weather in the first place.”

Soon after launching, Sesame Solar set its sights on the Caribbean. “The island nations are the most under siege with rising water and in the path of hurricanes and extreme storms,” Flanagan said.

The company said that after hurricanes Maria and Ida devastated the island of Dominica, Sesame Solar set up Nanogrids in 15 minutes that were used in a makeshift hospital. The Nanogrids repurposed rainwater into 130 gallons of clean drinking water per day, powered 60-foot trailers of showers and toilets, and provided refrigeration for essential supplies like insulin.

In June, Sesame Solar launched Nanogrids that use green hydrogen for backup power. Flanagan told Insider she’s working to ensure that the future of emergency relief is fossil-fuel free.

Flanagan’s call to action: “Natural disasters are becoming more frequent and devastating as a result of climate change. If we want to reverse this trajectory, we need to stop relying on fossil fuels to help us recover from these events, which are causing these natural disasters in the first place.”

Jerome Foster II

White House climate advisor; environmental-justice activist

call to action

Act with urgency — for our planet is dying and our communities are choking on lies and greed.
visit Jerome Foster II's instagram visit Jerome Foster II's twitter
Read more about Jerome Foster II

He/him

New York, United States

From the steps of the Capitol to rallies and town halls across the US, Jerome Foster II has been vocal about cultivating a community-centered approach to climate activism with justice and young voices at its heart.

As an activist and emerging technology developer, Foster informs US policy, advising President Joe Biden’s White House Environmental Justice Advisory Council, and he was a lead organizer of the Fridays For Future youth climate strikes.

Foster began holding the weekly strikes in 2019 and continued for 80 weeks in front of the White House and Harvard University, demanding that government officials enact climate-justice legislation and that school administrators divest themselves of fossil fuels. Foster also worked alongside Jane Fonda to kick-start Fire Drill Fridays, aimed at mobilizing older generations.

Foster is on a mission to make global governments take young people seriously in the climate conversation. “I have shown that young voices bring a sense of urgency, passion, and accountability in spaces of power,” he told Insider.

Alongside fellow activists, he pushed to include Justice40 principles in the Inflation Reduction Act, which authorized about $369 billion in climate spending over a decade. Justice40 is a White House initiative to ensure at least 40% of climate and clean energy funds go to disadvantaged communities overburdened by pollution.

Today he’s working on the Waic Up platform, which aims to give climate-change-vulnerable communities the opportunity to narrate their own stories. “So much of the narrative has been about plastic straws and personal change, which is vital, but we want to make sure it’s linked with human rights,” Foster said, adding, “We have to link it back to the fact that people are dying every single day.”

Foster’s call to action: “Tell the truth and act like thy neighbor is thy friend. Act with urgency — for our planet is dying and our communities are choking on lies and greed.”

Sônia Guajajara

Leader, Articulation of Indigenous Peoples of Brazil

call to action

Human rights and the preservation of nature are inseparable. Our voices must echo all around the globe in search of change.
visit Sônia Guajajara's instagram visit Sônia Guajajara's twitter
Read more about Sônia Guajajara

She/her

São Paulo, Brazil

Sônia Guajajara, an activist and politician who in 2018 made history as the first Indigenous woman on a presidential ticket in Brazil, is now running for a federal seat in the state of São Paulo, with the hope of creating an Indigenous bench in Brazil’s congress.

“For many people the impacts of extreme weather only come when something extreme happens, but for us that impact is daily — we suffer much more,” she told Insider.

Guajajara has been involved in the Brazilian Indigenous movement since 2001 and led the Articulation of Indigenous Peoples of Brazil for nine years, helping stop bills like one that was set to authorize mining in Indigenous forests, another that would’ve legalized and encouraged land grabbing, and one that would have stripped Indigenous communities of their rights to the land.

“Indigenous people are 5% of the world’s population, but we are responsible for 80% of the preservation of the world’s biodiversity,” Guajajara told Insider. “When Indigenous territories are exploited, biodiversity suffers and then the whole of humanity is threatened.”

Guajajara coordinates the mobilization of women who share ancestral knowledge, working together to oppose governmental measures that endanger their lands. Through Brazil’s Free Land mass demonstrations, she also helps coordinate Indigenous peoples in denouncing violations of their rights.

Guajajara’s call to action: “Human rights and the preservation of nature are inseparable. Our voices must echo all around the globe in search of change. It's time to unite for the reforestation of the earth and minds, through affection, healing, collectivity, and solidarity for us and for our Mother Earth.”

Anisa Kamadoli Costa

Chief sustainability officer, Rivian

call to action

Everybody has a role to play in advancing sustainability. Identify yours.
visit Anisa Kamadoli Costa's twitter visit Anisa Kamadoli Costa's linkedin
Read more about Anisa Kamadoli Costa

She/her

New York, United States

Anisa Kamadoli Costa started working on responsible mining and ocean and land conservation long before these issues were hot topics across corporate boardrooms.

She became Rivian’s first chief sustainability officer in April, following a two-decade career at Tiffany & Co., where she carved out the jeweler’s environmental, social, and governance priorities. Costa also made sustainability the focus of Tiffany’s philanthropy, which makes grants to organizations trying to protect natural ecosystems.

Costa said milestones at Tiffany’s included setting up the first sustainability committee at the board level, building a diamond and metal supply chain that is traceable to the mine, and establishing the company’s support of the Initiative for Responsible Mining Assurance, a third-party certification that involves local mining communities and environmental and human-rights groups. Human Rights Watch in 2020 gave Tiffany’s a “strong” ranking, citing its responsible sourcing of diamonds and gold.

Tiffany also stopped selling coral in 2004, and in 2003 it stopped sourcing rubies from Myanmar. That year, the US government banned imports from the country in response to the military’s human-rights abuses. The US lifted the sanctions in 2016, but Costa said Tiffany decided against sourcing rubies again after a case study found it wouldn’t be possible to do so ethically.

Costa took her skills to Rivian, an electric-vehicle startup that has stoked the market for trucks and SUVs. Demand is booming as companies and countries try to eliminate transportation emissions — a transition that will require mining for more metals like lithium and nickel.

Costa’s call to action: “Everybody has a role to play in advancing sustainability. Identify yours. I believe we can accelerate positive change for people and the planet by engaging our own unique strengths, expertise, and influence within our organizations, no matter our role, and learn to leverage the power of partnerships and coalitions — with government, the nonprofit sector, and, yes, even competitors. That’s the only way to raise standards and meet the expectations of our diverse constituencies.”

Peter Kalmus

Climate scientist, NASA's Jet Propulsion Lab

call to action

Global leaders must immediately end new fossil-fuel expansion.
visit Peter Kalmus's instagram visit Peter Kalmus's twitter
Read more about Peter Kalmus

He/him

California, United States

Peter Kalmus didn’t think the police would show up on April 6. He’s glad he was wrong.

Kalmus was among several scientists arrested that day after chaining themselves to the entrance of a JPMorgan Chase building in Los Angeles to protest the bank’s fossil-fuel investments, which totaled nearly $317 billion from 2016, the year the Paris agreement was signed, to 2020.

“It really was an act of desperation,” Kalmus, a climate scientist at NASA’s Jet Propulsion Lab, told Insider. Until that day, he believed his activism hadn’t made a big impact. He joined a loosely knit group, Scientist Rebellion, organizing acts of civil disobedience around the world to coincide with a report from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change that described how far off the world was from keeping global warming below catastrophic levels for the planet and humanity.

A video of their arrest went viral on social media. Now Kalmus uses his Twitter account, which has more than 310,000 followers, to blame the fossil-fuel industry for the climate crisis, blast politicians for taking money from the industry, and plead with world leaders and everyday people to take it all more seriously. He also cofounded the Climate Ad Project, which Kalmus described as an ad agency for the Earth.

“The impact was beyond my wildest dreams,” Kalmus said. “Activists who’ve been doing civil disobedience and taking much bigger risks than me were inspired that climate scientists affiliated with NASA would speak out so clearly. People are suddenly listening to what I have to say. It’s a lot of responsibility.”

Kalmus wants to keep inspiring people to engage in climate civil disobedience and to stop flying, as he did in 2012. His current research is focused on how extreme heat waves will affect people in cities, in rich versus poor neighborhoods.

Kalmus’ call to action: “Eighty percent of global heating comes from burning fossil fuels. Physics demands we end the fossil-fuel industry; the faster we do so, the more we save. Global leaders must immediately end new fossil-fuel expansion and create a plan and a treaty to equitably and rapidly ramp down fossil fuels.”

Amy Keister

Global director of sustainability, Compass Group

call to action

Focus on your strengths and go big with what can make the greatest impact. Find partners you trust. Collaborate.
visit Amy Keister's linkedin
Read more about Amy Keister

She/her

Chertsey, United Kingdom

Amy Keister, Compass Group’s global director of sustainability, is on a mission to reduce the company’s climate impact by shifting to more plant-based meals and slashing food waste.

The global food-service giant serves 5.5 billion meals a year at schools and college campuses, hospitals, stadiums, and corporate cafeterias. The scale, particularly in the US and the UK, gives Compass enormous influence in the food supply chain, from farm to fork to disposal — a system that accounted for nearly a third of the world’s greenhouse-gas emissions in 2019.

Keister shaped the company’s net-zero strategy to align with Paris agreement’s goals. Compass says the food it buys accounts for 72% of its carbon footprint; the company wants to cut that share by nearly a third this decade.

Tackling food waste is a big push. Compass worked with its Bon Appétit Management Company division on a digital tool to identify how to prevent food waste in kitchens. Last year, it said, waste declined by 28% across 2,000 sites. Compass also educates consumers and policymakers about why food waste is a climate issue. Keister launched Stop Food Waste Day, which the mayors of Charlotte, North Carolina; Boston; New York; and other cities have declared days of action. The company says the social-media campaign in 2021 reached 40 million people globally.

Compass is also training chefs to make meat-free meals and incorporate more fruits, vegetables, legumes, and whole grains. Compass doesn’t have a target by which to reduce its meat purchases, but Keister said she expects that empowering chefs and influencing consumer behavior through labeling a meal’s carbon footprint will make a difference. She said Compass also has to work with its suppliers, like the meatpackers Tyson and Cargill, to promote regenerative agriculture.

Keister’s call to action: “Focus on your strengths and go big with what can make the greatest impact. Start by solving one thing, do it well, and explain why it matters. Recognize it’s a journey that you can’t travel alone. Find partners you trust. Collaborate. Celebrate your wins and recognize the difference you’re making.”

Sophia Kianni

US representative on the United Nations’ Youth Advisory Group on Climate Change; founder, Climate Cardinals

call to action

We need governments and financial institutions to end funding for all new fossil-fuel development.
visit Sophia Kianni's instagram visit Sophia Kianni's twitter
Read more about Sophia Kianni

She/her

New York, United States

At 20, Sophia Kianni, an environmentalist, student, and activist, already has an impressive résumé. She is the founder and executive director of Climate Cardinals, a nongovernmental organization translating English-language climate information into over 100 languages, and she is the youngest member of the the UN secretary-general’s Youth Advisory Group on Climate Change.

Kianni told Insider she’s proud to have been able to make a difference within climate activism “both by empowering other youth but also by having my solutions and considerations taken seriously by the UN and through the work I’ve done with my nonprofit.”

Through Climate Cardinals, which Kianni says has more than 9,000 volunteers translating documents in over 40 countries, Kianni has placed language accessibility firmly on the climate agenda. In a 2021 TED talk — which has more than 1.6 million views — she outlined how language barriers are an obstacle to progress within climate activism. Since then, she said, “both institutions within the UN and other NGOs put more effort into translation.”

Through The Inkey List’s Impact Fund and the Iris Project, Kianni also distributes up to $200,000 in micro-grants that help young people deliver change in their communities. Kianni recently participated in two White House briefings and plans to push to make conversations surrounding renewable energy accessible in every language.

Kianni’s call to action: “The latest research from the IPCC has made it very clear: In order to avoid the worst impacts of the climate crisis, we need governments and financial institutions to end funding for all new fossil-fuel development and immediately scale up massive investments in clean, renewable energy.”

Sarah Kew and Sjoukje Philip

Co-leads, World Weather Attribution

call to action

We want decision-makers to act swiftly and appropriately and the public to recognize the impact of changing climate on their lives.
visit Sarah Kew and Sjoukje Philip's twitter
Read more about Sarah Kew and Sjoukje Philip

She/her

De Bilt, Netherlands

Sarah Kew and Sjoukje Philip work as researchers at the Royal Netherlands Meteorological Institute. But since 2015 they’ve volunteered their time to the World Weather Attribution, where they now co-lead a team of researchers together with Friederike Otto, a German climate scientist at the Imperial College London.

Weeks after the deadly floods in Pakistan in August, the team at WWA found that climate change had likely intensified the monsoon rain. The proximity of homes, bridges, and farmland to flood plains along with outdated river-management systems were also factors.  

The rapid assessment linking human-caused climate change to a natural disaster wasn’t possible until recently. Previously, it would take months or years to publish a finding. This emerging field of attribution science is equipping citizens, public officials, and journalists with a powerful new understanding of how the climate crisis is wreaking havoc in real time and how communities can adapt.

WWA, founded in 2014, is the brainchild of Otto and Geert Jan van Oldenborgh, a Dutch climate scientist at KNMI who died in 2021. The WWA team developed a peer-reviewed method that combines climate models with observational data from a disaster event. The event is defined by scientists together with local experts and humanitarian groups.

Kew and Philip have since worked on more than two dozen studies at WWA, including those suggesting that climate change had made flooding in Western Europe in 2021 more likely and a prolonged Siberian heatwave in 2020 significantly more likely.

“If we do this rapidly after an event, then the decision-makers can act swiftly and appropriately for their needs, basing their decisions on scientific results,” Kew said.

Philip added, “It’s also important to disentangle climate change from from other drivers of impacts, like the vulnerability of people.”

Kew and Philip’s call to action: "We provide rapid and scientifically based answers following impactful extreme events that we analyze, so there is no need to blindly guess the role of climate change. We want decision-makers to act swiftly and appropriately on these analyses and the public to recognize the impact of changing climate on their lives."

Ed Markey

US senator from Massachusetts

call to action

To people with political power who aren’t listening to young people: Offer support, seize this moment, and build a better world together.
visit Ed Markey's instagram visit Ed Markey's twitter
Read more about Ed Markey

He/him

Washington, DC, United States

Democratic Sen. Ed Markey of Massachusetts learned at a young age how environmental pollution harms a community. His mom forbade him from swimming in the river in his hometown of Malden, Massachusetts, because it was oozing with “pre-Jimi Hendrix purple haze,” as Markey, 76, described it. A rubber factory and local coal and chemical plants were using the river as a dumping ground.

“The mentality of industry before strong safety, health, and environmental laws were put on the books was anything goes that maximizes profits,” Markey told Insider. “That’s an era I’ve been working to end since I was a boy.”

The senator has been involved in nearly every attempt at climate legislation for more than four decades, most recently the Inflation Reduction Act. It’s the most significant climate policy in US history, born of a movement Markey helped galvanize with Democratic Rep. Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez of New York — their Green New Deal became a rallying cry for climate justice among a generation of young activists.

The Inflation Reduction Act includes about $369 billion for climate action such as tax breaks for renewable energy. About $60 billion is set aside for communities disproportionately exposed to air and water pollution. The law is estimated to put the US on track to reduce emissions by 40% this decade.

It’s a victory, Markey said, but he plans to fight for more. “The power of young people just produced a historic climate bill,” he said. “We should continue to listen to them. They are right. We have a lot more work to do. Organize, don't agonize.”

Markey’s call to action: “Our young people are already our global leaders. The climate crisis isn’t a threat to future generations — it’s a threat that this intergenerational movement has to address now. To people with political power who aren’t listening to young people: Offer support, seize this moment, and build a better world together.”

Francia Márquez

Vice president, Colombia

call to action

Humanity’s greatest challenge is to either work together to preserve this planet or destroy it.

She/her

Bogotá, Colombia

Francia Márquez became Colombia’s first Black vice president in June after spending decades mobilizing women in her mountainous community of La Toma to stop illegal gold mining and protect ancestral lands. Illegal mining across the country is fueling deforestation, biodiversity loss, and water pollution while exacerbating the climate crisis. In 2014, Márquez led a 350-mile women's march to Bogotá, the capital, to demand the government stop illegal gold mining in La Toma. Weeks of protests led to a national task force and the confiscation of backhoes and other illegal machinery by security forces.

Márquez in 2010 helped organize protests against the Colombian government’s attempt to evict Afro-Colombians from La Toma to pave the way for new gold mining. The community filed a lawsuit against the government’s decision and won.

She was awarded the Goldman Environmental Prize in 2018. Despite being born into poverty in a country where Afro-Colombians are subjected to racism, as well as facing death threats for her activism, Márquez earned a law degree and ascended to Colombia’s executive branch on a pledge to reduce inequality and move the country away from oil and gas.

Márquez’s call to action: “Humanity’s greatest challenge is to either work together to preserve this planet or destroy it. It’s up to us to assume our own responsibility and defend life. In Colombia, we’re creating campaigns to incentivize reforestation, as well as recycling. We want to raise awareness about the products that can be composted and how we re-use certain items. There is so much we can do.” (from a June 2022 interview with Earthjustice)

Marcelo Mena-Carrasco

CEO, Global Methane Hub

call to action

We cannot avert climate catastrophe without drastically and rapidly reducing methane.
visit Marcelo Mena-Carrasco's twitter
Read more about Marcelo Mena-Carrasco

He/him

Santiago, Chile

Marcelo Mena-Carrasco is a professor of civil and environmental engineering at the Pontifical Catholic University of Valparaíso in Chile, the CEO of the Global Methane Hub, and a former Chilean environment minister. Backed by $220 million, the hub aims to lower global temperatures by focusing on methane mitigation in energy, food, and waste.

“We will be able to prevent warming at a rate that's three times faster than only focusing on CO2, because it’s a short-lived, very potent gas,” Mena-Carrasco told Insider.

Mena-Carrasco says that while working in government he helped reduce air pollution in Chile's cities and prioritized ocean conservation, boosting dwindling populations of Chilean sea bass and the orange roughy breed of fish. This inspired countries across South America and North America to expand their own marine-protection measures.

“As a young person, I set out to make the biggest difference I could in the time I have on environmental issues. I spend all day on it — it's my hobby; it's my day job. It's every single thing I do, and I like to lead by example,” Mena-Carrasco said.

Mena-Carrasco’s home runs on renewable energy and has green battery storage to supplement power. And when he isn’t riding his bicycle, he uses an electric car for transportation.

Mena-Carrasco’s call to action: “Global Methane Hub supports investments that lay the groundwork for transformative methane-mitigation work. Reducing methane emissions will not solve the climate crisis by itself. However, we cannot avert climate catastrophe without drastically and rapidly reducing methane. It is one of our best shots at bending the warming curve.”

Jacqueline Novogratz

Founder and CEO, Acumen; entrepreneur; author

call to action

Any climate solution requires ensuring the poor aren’t left further behind.

She/her

Africa, Pakistan, and India

Jacqueline Novogratz grew up with a desire to change the world. And over the past 30 years, she has. As the founder and CEO of Acumen, she works at the nexus of climate and poverty as a pioneer in impact investing.

A self-proclaimed “accidental banker,” Novogratz began her career working for Chase Manhattan Bank. Through that work, she saw how people with lower incomes were excluded from larger conversations and opportunities around the climate crisis and the economy. So she moved to Rwanda and began a journey that would lead her to help people become entrepreneurs through impact investing, or investing in companies and organizations to affect social and environmental issues.

“I believe in entrepreneurs,” she told Insider.

Her company, Acumen, invests in small companies that have helped move millions of people off kerosene and other forms of energy and onto cleaner alternatives such as solar.

In turn, Acumen helps empower and bring low-income people into the conversation, especially in areas where they are disproportionately affected by the climate crisis.

Novogratz’s call to action: “Any climate solution requires ensuring the poor aren’t left further behind. We have the technologies, skills, and capital to bring clean, affordable energy to the 800 million individuals still without electricity. Moral imagination and will are critical — as is investing the right kind of capital to go where it is most needed, not where it is safest.”

Martin Pei

Chief tech officer, SSAB

call to action

A greener steel industry is within reach, but a level playing field requires a CO2 price.
visit Martin Pei's twitter
Read more about Martin Pei

He/him

Stockholm, Sweden

Modern life relies on steel — it’s everywhere you look, from buildings and bridges to trains and cars to home appliances. Steel also accounts for 7% of global greenhouse-gas emissions and is one the most difficult industries to clean up because the industrial process requires a lot of coal and high temperatures that renewable alternatives can’t provide at scale yet.

Martin Pei was up for the challenge. He’s the executive vice president and chief technology officer at SSAB, a small player in the global steel market but one with an outsized impact in Sweden, where the company alone accounts for 10% the country’s carbon footprint.

Sweden’s climate law mandates net-zero emissions by 2045, and its electric grid is powered almost entirely by renewables already. Pei said the steel industry’s decades of research on how to reduce emissions helped SSAB decide that carbon-capture-and-storage technology wasn’t the right path.

Instead, Pei wanted to try to decarbonize the entire value chain. He launched a pilot project — the Hybrit initiative — in 2016 with Europe's largest iron-ore producer, LKAB, in northern Sweden, and Vattenfall, a multinational power company.

Today the majority of steel is made by feeding iron-ore pellets and coking coal into blast furnaces powered by natural gas. Hybrit is using green hydrogen made from renewable energy instead of coking coal. Meanwhile, LKAB is testing bio-oil as an alternative heat source to fossil fuels to make iron-ore pellets, which it estimates can reduce emissions by 40%.

While the project is still at pilot scale, some nearly fossil-fuel-free steel was delivered to the automaker Volvo Group in 2021. Pei said the goal is to get it on the commercial market by 2026.

Pei’s call to action: “A greener steel industry is within reach, but a level playing field requires a CO2 price and clear definitions for green steel.”

Autumn Peltier

Environmental activist; Anishinabek Nation chief water commissioner

call to action

The most important ingredient in life is water; without it there's no life.
visit Autumn Peltier's instagram
Read more about Autumn Peltier

She/her

Ontario, Canada

From an early age, Autumn Peltier has made her voice heard.

Peltier is the chief water commissioner for the Anishinabek Nation. Her documentary, “The Water Walker,” was released on HBO Canada in the spring of 2022.

An Indigenous water and rights activist, Peltier, now 17, told Insider, “We take so much from our land; what are we giving back?”

When she was 12, Peltier admonished Canadian Prime Minister Justin Trudeau at an Assembly of First Nations event about choices he made for her people and a lack of support he’d provided them. Peltier’s criticisms led Trudeau to make her a promise to protect the water. Peltier still holds Trudeau accountable for that promise.

Peltier told the Canadian Broadcasting Corporation that she was simply supposed to give Trudeau a gift at the event but felt compelled to confront him about a lack of access to clean water in hundreds of Canadian communities.

“It’s one of my proudest moments,” she told the network.

A year later, Peltier spoke at the United Nations General Assembly.

Peltier’s activism at a young age drew further attention. In 2017, she was awarded the Sovereign’s Medal for Volunteers from the governor general of Canada and the lieutenant governor of Ontario, and she was shortlisted for the International Children’s Peace Prize. In 2022, she was featured on Maclean’s Power List.

This year, Peltier received an honorary doctorate from Royal Roads University, and she received the Daniel G. Hill Human Rights Award from the Ontario Human Rights Commission as well as the Emerging Leaders Award from the Public Policy Forum.

Peltier has seen support for Indigenous communities growing — her petition demanding clean drinking water for all has more than 90,000 signatures. She also has the support of school-age kids, who send her letters.

“That’s my favorite, because you read and see how my work is impacting future generations,” she said.

Peltier’s call to action: “The most important ingredient in life is water; without it there's no life. Yet in 2022 there are still thousands of First Nations people and communities that border cities that have zero access to clean water. It's a basic human right, and my petition is looking to admonish this. Sign it here.”

Gregor Robertson

Executive vice president, Nexii Building Solutions

call to action

Every key decision we make must reduce our impacts on climate and nature. Our kids and grandkids depend on us.

He/him

Vancouver, canada

Gregor Robertson has a strong track record of bringing climate solutions to mainstream attention. He’s experienced in organic farming; was the longest-serving mayor of Vancouver, British Columbia; and cofounded Happy Planet Foods. Today he’s an executive vice president of Nexii, a building-solutions firm pushing for the building industry, a sector set to be worth $15 trillion by 2030, to become sustainable.  

As mayor, Robertson sought to transform Vancouver into the world’s greenest city; a 2012 report said it had “the smallest per capita carbon footprint of any city in North America.” “Vancouver now has a zero-emission building code and all-new buildings are being built to that eight years ahead of schedule,” he told Insider.

At Nexii, he’s continued the crusade against emissions in the building sector, which accounts for an estimated 37% of all global emissions. Robertson said concrete and steel are the worst offenders, adding, “Most people have no idea that the buildings we live and work and play in are the No. 1 driver of the climate crisis.”

Nexii says that by manufacturing building parts off-site and then rapidly assembling them on the construction site it eliminates almost all building waste and averages 31% less embodied carbon than in conventional construction.

Robertson is also involved in the City Climate Finance Gap Fund, a $55 million pot of cash to direct funding to cities in the developing world that need feasibility studies for green infrastructure and climate-action projects like electric buses or flood protection.

Robertson’s call to action: “Every key decision we make must reduce our impacts on climate and nature. Our kids and grandkids depend on us. Buildings are the top source of climate pollution. Business and government leaders must aggressively scale net-zero construction and eliminate fossil-fuel use. Dramatically decarbonize buildings and cities!”

As a yogi, mystic, and spiritual leader, Sadhguru has marshaled his online global following of millions into ecological initiatives launched by his organization, the Isha Foundation.

He spearheaded a tree-planting project in Tamil Nadu, India, with a goal of planting 114 million trees in the southernmost state and increasing the green cover by 10% in order to help bring back dwindling tree species and provide permanent vegetation to local farmers.

Sadhguru said that planting such a huge number of trees required a mindset shift for the public. “I spent time planting trees in people's heads, which is the most difficult terrain, believe me,” he told Insider.

Turning his attention to water, Sadhguru traveled across India to educate people about the effects of drought on the country’s rivers. And through his Save Soil movement, Sadhguru uses his platform to draw attention to the mass extinction of soil’s microbial life and the repercussions for the agricultural sector.

“After COP26, there was no mention of soil,” he said, referring to the 2021 United Nations Climate Change Conference. “I thought, ‘I need to do something shocking.’ So I rode from London to southern India on a motorcycle in 100 days.”

This campaign garnered the attention of people on social media and global leaders alike. Countries such as Germany, the UK, the US, and India have pledged billions of dollars to safeguard global soil health.

She/her

Massachusetts, United States

With a degree in economics and a passion for community organizing, Leslie Samuelrich found her way to responsible investing after spending years working as an environmental activist.

She’s the president of Green Century Capital Management, a mutual fund with more than $1 billion in assets. In college, she was an activist. After graduation, she took an internship with a stockbroker. But helping people with money make even more money didn’t align with her values.

“What I ended up doing was following the draw to be an environmental activist and organizer,” she told Insider.

The field of responsible investing didn’t exist when she graduated, but now that it does, Samuelrich said she’s come full circle. She’s doing the work she wanted to do 20 years ago, combining her expertise in economics and finance with her passion for environmental issues.

Green Century says it’s the only firm in the US that is owned by environmental and public-health nonprofits, using value-based streams to keep the money it invests for clients out of companies it doesn’t want to support; initially those were tobacco and nuclear-energy companies but now it includes all companies promoting fossil fuels.

She said Green Century’s clients “want to know that their money is not supporting the industries that are ruining the environment.”

The company’s base of investors, who are looking for financial products like an IRA or a health savings account, is growing — as is the field of sustainable investing.

Samuelrich’s call to action: “Stop investing in fossil-fuel companies and join the thousands of individuals and institutions that have stopped supporting the industry that has driven climate change. Align your investments with your values, and send a message to coal, oil, and gas companies that don’t support them.”

Bessie Schwarz

Cofounder and CEO, Cloud to Street

call to action

Without increased funding for climate adaptation initiatives millions of lives are at stake.

Read more about Bessie Schwarz

She/her

New York, United States

Bessie Schwarz is the cofounder and CEO of Cloud to Street, a flood-mapping platform designed to help protect climate-vulnerable communities around the globe.

Schwarz described flooding as the worst climate disaster and one of the hardest-felt impacts of climate change. Schwarz also said that for flood insurance, the protection gap — or the difference between insured losses and economic losses — is large. Cloud to Street’s platform seeks to minimize the impact of flooding by using global satellite imagery, flood gauges, precipitation data, and community reports to monitor floods worldwide in real time.

It’s designed to help governments access information to both prepare for and respond to floods.

Schwarz gave this example: The government of the Democratic Republic of the Congo used Cloud to Street’s system to identify about 7,000 refugees who had migrated to flood-prone land and help relocate them 10 months before a massive flood hit the area.

“There would have been a humanitarian crisis, but they weren’t there, so that is kind of pretty clear impact on these people’s lives,” Schwarz said.

Schwarz’s call to action: “A mere 7% of all climate-related investments are currently directed towards adaptation efforts. Without increased funding for climate adaptation initiatives — especially in communities most vulnerable to climate disaster — millions of lives are at stake. Climate resilience demands a human-centered approach for applying the technology available today.”

Leah Stokes

Associate professor of environmental politics, University of California, Santa Barbara; policy advisor to Senate Democrats

call to action

visit Leah Stokes's twitter

She/her

California, United States

As an undergraduate, Leah Stokes was organizing campaigns around using less energy when something clicked.

“Pretty quickly I realized that wasn’t a big enough lever to make change,” Stokes told Insider. “If we really want to make progress on cleaning up the energy system, we have to change policy.”

Stokes, now an associate professor of environmental politics at the University of California, Santa Barbara, has devoted her career to changing energy policy. She gained a big following in 2020 during the Democratic presidential primary, when she dissected the candidates’ climate and energy platforms in Twitter threads and pressured them to go bigger.

Soon she was advising Evergreen Action, a climate group founded by staffers of the presidential campaign of Jay Inslee, the Washington governor. That group and the Sunrise Movement of young climate activists were instrumental in getting the Biden administration to set a key goal of 100% clean electricity in the US by 2035, Stokes said. She also became a senior policy counsel for Rewiring America, a nonprofit that advocates electrifying machines that use fossil fuels.

The fingerprints of these groups are scattered throughout the Inflation Reduction Act, including some $9 billion in home-energy rebates to help Americans insulate their homes and replace stoves, furnaces, water heaters, and other appliances with electric alternatives.

Stokes became a go-to expert on the legislation for the press. Her own research on public opinion and policy taught her the effectiveness of a public-health message.

“I talk about pollution and the health costs,” Stokes said. “I wrote a paper with Chris Warshaw looking at how to frame renewable-energy policies, and we found health and jobs were effective. I think you can see that in the way the Biden administration talks about clean energy.”

Stokes’ call to action: “To cut carbon pollution and limit the impacts of climate change, we need deep decarbonization. That means widespread electrification paired with the rapid buildout of renewables like wind and solar. Individual households and policymakers alike can help speed the transition to 100% clean electricity. It’s time to electrify everything.”

Leah Thomas

Founder, Intersectional Environmentalist

call to action

I urge global leaders to take an intersectional approach to climate justice and consider that people of color face the brunt of the climate crisis.

Read more about Leah Thomas

She/her

California, United States

When Leah Thomas started her sophomore year at Chapman University, she was the only Black student in her environmental science and policy program. It was a few weeks after protests erupted in Ferguson, Missouri, where a white police officer killed Michael Brown, a Black 18-year-old. The shooting accelerated the Black Lives Matter movement.

It was especially personal for Thomas, who grew up in the suburbs of St. Louis. She wanted to do something about racial justice. Over time, Thomas realized how few people understood how social justice intersects with environmentalism — how low-income communities and people of color are more likely to be exposed to pollution. Thomas started writing about it in her blog, Green Girl Leah.

Another pivotal moment arrived in the summer of 2020, when racial-justice protests were once again making headlines.

“I was disappointed in the environmental community,” Thomas told Insider. “It was a moment of cognitive dissonance. How could so many environmentalists not understand that our work should ensure all people have clean air, clean water, and the right to breathe — like, in every sense of that?”

Thomas coined the term “intersectional environmentalism” in an Instagram post that went viral. She created another platform bearing the phrase to educate people about environmental justice. The Intersectional Environmentalism account has amassed some 430,000 followers; there is also a nonprofit and a book. Some teachers are using her work in their curriculum.

Now Thomas is on a journey to uncover and educate the public about Black women’s contributions to the environmental movement.

“It’s taught me that I’m not alone,” she said. “Even if I’m one of the only Black faces in a white space, there’s centuries of Black women who’ve had a connection with the land.”

Thomas’ call to action: “I urge global leaders to take an intersectional approach to climate justice and consider that people of color face the brunt of the climate crisis and yet are the least responsible for it. The climate crisis is as much a human-rights issue as it is an environmental issue.”

Georges Tijbosch

CEO, MiQ

call to action

We must focus on high-impact actions. Perfect cannot be the enemy of good as we transition to a renewable future.
visit Georges Tijbosch's linkedin
Read more about Georges Tijbosch

He/him

London, United Kingdom

Georges Tijbosch, the CEO of MiQ (short for Methane IQ), hopes to use market competition and economic incentives to stop methane leaks in the global oil and gas sectors.

By designing a certification system to create transparency surrounding emissions, Tijbosch aims to incentivize the tracking of methane and reduce the amount permeating our atmosphere.

Tijbosch said that despite being 84 times as potent as carbon dioxide over a 20-year time horizon, methane has been largely neglected in the search for climate solutions. “It is probably the biggest low-hanging fruit when it comes to greenhouse gases,” he told Insider.

Tracking methane can be complex, and until recently industry heavyweights in the oil and gas sectors had little reason to bother. MiQ’s certification process is designed to solve this by introducing a grading system to spur competitiveness and incentivize the adoption of new climate-solution technologies.

“We are addressing it by creating transparency, and that's going to get rid, more or less, of those methane emissions in 10 years,” he said.

Tijbosch says oil and gas companies can work with companies using cutting-edge methane-measurement and -detection technologies — such as long-range lasers or satellite imagery — to become certified with MiQ. He says his hope is to create an "ecosystem" of organizations working toward the same goal.

Tijbosch’s call to action: “Major emissions reductions this decade are crucial to achieving climate goals. We must focus on high-impact actions, like the 7 billion tonnes of CO2e of methane emissions that can be eliminated from oil and gas today, and harness the power of markets to accelerate this change. Perfect cannot be the enemy of good as we transition to a renewable future.”

As an environmental journalist, Amy Westervelt was well versed on issues of sustainability and the climate crisis.

“Critical Frequency” expands the global narrative of environmental issues, tying together threads happening all over the world and elevating the voices of people driving change.

Westervelt told Insider that those narratives are helping change the conversation about the climate crisis and other environmental and social-justice issues. Jan Wurzbacher

Founder and CEO, Climeworks

call to action

We cannot wait any longer to fight global warming, and this must start with a drastic reduction of emissions.
visit Jan Wurzbacher's twitter visit Jan Wurzbacher's linkedin
Read more about Jan Wurzbacher

He/him

Zurich, Switzerland

A project on renewable energy and a friendship led Jan Wurzbacher to found a company to remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and store it in the ground.

Wurzbacher met Christoph Gebald while at ETH Zurich. Both mechanical engineers, they shared a passion for climate advocacy. They worked together on a research project that ultimately led to Climeworks, a Swiss company that uses direct air capture to remove carbon dioxide from the environment.

Wurzbacher told Insider that Climeworks’ carbon-monoxide collectors are the size of a shipping container, with fans that suck in the air and move it to filters that capture the carbon dioxide and bind it. When the filter is full, he said, it's heated and breaks the bonds to release the CO2, which is made into a liquid. The CO2 is put in a water solution and pumped into underground rocks that then eventually turn into solid carbonate minerals.

“I myself had a dream of founding my own company,” Wurzbacher said. “And I always knew it should be something meaningful or solving one of the bigger problems on this planet.”

He’s created what he sees as a scalable solution to one of the greatest threats on this planet.

Climeworks says it has 15 units in operation and is removing thousands of tons of CO2 from the atmosphere. The company recently opened a plant in Iceland that could mean millions of tons are removed by 2030.

Eventually, Climeworks hopes to capture around 1% of the world’s CO2, which would mean it needs about 250,000 plants — a goal Wurzbacher said is achievable.

Wurzbacher’s call to action: “We cannot wait any longer to fight global warming, and this must start with a drastic reduction of emissions. Removing CO2 from the air is a complement in your net-zero journey. I made this my life’s purpose, but we need so many more like-minded folks. What’s more important than a climate-positive future? Let’s look at what the maximum one can do, not the minimum.”

Huolin Xin

Professor, Department of Physics and Astronomy, University of California, Irvine

call to action

Let’s seize the opportunity to use technological and economic forces to ‘divorce’ our society from fossil fuels.
Read more about Huolin Xin

He/him

California, United States

Electric cars are the future, but they need batteries, and most lithium-ion batteries in use contain cobalt. Cobalt is not only expensive but linked to humanitarian and environmental issues.

Cobalt is sourced primarily from the Democratic Republic of Congo, and much of it is mined by children. It’s also toxic.

Huolin Xin, a professor at the University of California, Irvine, learned this and posed a question: What if we could eliminate cobalt from batteries altogether, solve a humanitarian and environmental problem, and make better energy storage and conversion materials that last longer?

In just a few years, he and his team of researchers did just that, using research on cathode chemistry to produce cobalt-free batteries. Large automotive companies are interested in Xin’s research, and he believes cobalt-free batteries could be in production in a few years.

His research, funded by a $2.5 million US Department of Energy grant, is set to be published in Nature magazine in October.  

And he’s not stopping there. Xin told Insider his next goal is to work on electrifying aircraft and the heavy-duty trucking industry.

“Transportation is the largest energy consumption,” he said. “My aspiration is to get rid of fossil fuels from everything that we do. I create technologies that will provide economic force to make this world more sustainable.”

Xin’s call to action: “Let’s seize the opportunity to use technological and economic forces to ‘divorce’ our society from fossil fuels and give our children a sustainable future.”
