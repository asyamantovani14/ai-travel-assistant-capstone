The best 'green' vacation spot in all 50 states for people who care about planet-friendly travel

By Molly O'Brien
2023-07-27T21:52:34Z
Facebook Email X LinkedIn Copy link Impact Link
Save Saved Read in app
This story is available exclusively to Business Insider subscribers. Become an Insider and start reading now. Have an account? .
  • Eco-conscious Americans looking to take a vacation don't need to travel far.
  • Destinations in all US 50 states are making efforts to be more sustainable.
  • You can travel to Arkansas for mountain biking or visit a so-called green winery in Missouri.
  • This article is part of "Green Getaway," a series exploring how people can make more eco-conscious choices while traveling. For more climate-action news, visit Insider's One Planet hub.

As travelers across the US and beyond realize the importance of sustainability, more people are choosing to vacation closer to home.

Destinations across the US are prioritizing conservation and eco-conscious practices.

Whether you're interested in volunteering for a beach cleanup, seeing sea turtles in the wild, or staying at an environmentally friendly hotel, here's how you can experience a greener local getaway in each of the 50 states.

On Alabama's coast, the city of Gulf Shores is home to a white-sands state park and a sustainable hotel.

Gulf Shores has made citywide efforts to promote sustainable tourism. The city, on the Gulf of Mexico, has spacious, warm beaches and outdoorsy activities like kayaking, hiking, and parasailing.

The city has several ordinances designed to prevent littering and protect local wildlife. Its website, for example, says personal items like towels, chairs, coolers, and toys must be removed from the beaches after sunset or they'll be collected and disposed of by authorities. Partly in an effort to protect native animal species, there's a limit to digging on the beaches.

Visitors can stop by the nonprofit Gulf Coast Center for Ecotourism and Sustainability for information on summer camps and programs on nature education.

As for where to spend the night, the Lodge at Gulf State Park is an eco-friendly option. The hotel's website describes sustainable construction principles designed to meet certain LEED standards. (LEED, which stands for Leadership in Energy and Environmental Design, is a rating system for buildings' sustainability, energy efficiency, and environmental impact.)

The lodge sits on white sands within the 6,150-acre Gulf State Park along the coast. The hotel's website says guests have direct access to 28 miles of hiking and biking trails, in addition to a nature center, interactive outdoor classes, nature walks, and more.

Anchorage, Alaska, has a mountainous state park for camping and bird-watching.

Anchorage, Alaska's largest city, has a special focus on sustainable tourism efforts.

Its tourism organization, Visit Anchorage, says it has a sustainable-travel policy and is certified through the Adventure Green Alaska program, which "recognizes and promotes model Alaska tourism businesses who practice economic, environmental, social, and cultural sustainability."

In Chugach State Park, you can hike, camp, and bird-watch at several dedicated nature reserves. And there are more than 120 miles of paved bike and multiuse trails around the city for exploring, such as the Moose Loop, a 30-mile moose-shaped trail.

Travelers can stop by Grow North Farm, an urban farm and marketplace that says it provides land for more than 20 neighborhood farmers and independent businesses, all of refugee or immigrant backgrounds.

There are plenty of farm-to-table or ocean-to-table dining options, with same-day-caught salmon and other seafood, as well as fresh greens.

Fire Island Rustic Bakeshop is a good option for breakfast, lunch, or a snack. The artisanal bakery's website says it uses local ingredients where it makes sense, "based on what we would feed ourselves and our families." 

For dining with a twist, Bear Tooth Theatrepub is another great option. The movie theater serves food such as pizzas and burritos made with local ingredients whenever possible, according to its website. It also serves local beer from Broken Tooth Brewing on draft.

Mesa, Arizona, can satisfy sustainable foodies with a weekend at biodynamic farms, plus a bonus wind-cave tour.

Mesa is an ideal stop for Arizonans wanting a getaway centered on nature and local experiences. A self-guided Fresh Foodie Trail winds through organic, family-run olive and peach farms, plus a historic flour mill, local wineries, and restaurants.

You can also stop at Steadfast Farm, which describes itself as a 2-acre bio-intensive farming operation, to buy fruit, vegetables, and flowers grown on-site.

Another option is True Garden, which focuses on vertical aeroponic farming, a practice it says uses less land and water than most traditional farming methods.

For an immersive overnight nature experience, reserve a campground at Usery Mountain Regional Park to access miles of desert hiking trails and wind caves. The park's nature center also regularly has events like bird-watching, stargazing, and moonlit hikes.

Bentonville, Arkansas, is known as the mountain-biking capital of the world, with a network of trails to explore.

Known as the birthplace of Walmart, Bentonville is a town of fewer than 50,000 residents in the state's Ozark Mountain region.

The town is the self-proclaimed mountain-biking capital of the world — it has more than 40 miles of trails for cyclists, pedestrians, and runners. Many of those trails wind through art installations from OZ Art NWA.

Downtown, Ledger, a bikeable building that opened in the fall of 2022, has six floors of bike ramps that are free to the public.

Regardless of whether you're big on cycling, there's plenty to do outside, with several parks throughout the city, a farmers market that sells local produce and meats, and a seasonal art market where you can browse locally made pieces.

A green-focused restaurant to try is The Preacher's Son, which was nominated for a James Beard Foundation award in 2022. The restaurant, located in a refurbished church, says it prioritizes seasonal and locally sourced ingredients and has received an award for sustainable recycling practices and food-waste collection.

For organic wine tasting and eco-friendly treehouse accommodations, visit Sonoma County, California.

Famous for its wine, California's Sonoma County is also championing sustainability initiatives. The region's tourism board is part of an organization that promotes sustainable travel practices. It's also partnered with Kind Traveler, a hotel-booking site that offers perks and discounts in exchange for a donation to community charities.

The county has a sustainable-travel guide, as well as a list of volunteer tourism opportunities at local food banks and parks.

Eco-friendly overnight accommodations include Sonoma Treehouse Adventures, where guests can zip-line and sleep in cabins among the redwoods. In Healdsburg, there's the LEED-certified H2hotel and Harmon Guest House, a boutique hotel with a solar-heated pool and bicycles for guests to ride around town.

Wineries that follow sustainable farming practices include Jordan, which says it's been carbon-neutral in electricity use since 2014. There's also Quivira, a family-owned organic vineyard with a bee garden and chickens as well as a half-acre compost pile.

Spend a green weekend in Breckenridge, Colorado, by using the city's new e-bike program and volunteering for trail restoration.

Breckenridge has plenty of green initiatives, even when it's white with snow in the winter. The town banned plastic bags in 2021 and has dozens of electric-vehicle charging ports. Its "B Like Breckenridge" sustainability initiatives are designed to educate both locals and visitors on protecting the mountain town's environment.

For those interested in trail restoration, the Friends of Breckenridge Trails program welcomes volunteers to maintain local spaces by pulling weeds, picking up trash, and building trails.

In May, Breckenridge launched Breck E-Ride, a bike-share program with 75 e-bikes across 12 hubs, to encourage biking over driving. The pilot program is expected to run until October 31.

Green-conscious lodging can be found at Beaver Run, a resort with a focus on sustainable practices. The resort provides recycling bags in guest rooms, and it's partnered with Clean the World, a nonprofit that collects and recycles leftover soap bars.

For a quiet riverside getaway, you can kayak and eat locally grown produce in Ledyard, Connecticut.

Ledyard is a small town of about 15,000 residents near the state's southeastern shore, along the Thames River. There are plenty of trails and parks to traverse and a handful of scenic places to kayak, including Long Pond and Lantern Hill Pond. A farmers market, where you can stock up on artisanal goods, is held annually from June through September.

Visitors can stay at Abbey's Lantern Hill Inn, a bed-and-breakfast that's been certified by Connecticut's Green Lodging program since 2010. Guest reviews mention vegetable, herb, and flower gardens to explore.

Rehoboth Beach, Delaware, is a peaceful destination for bird-watching and beach days.

The natural beauty of Rehoboth Beach is something locals want to protect. The city government has described sustainable tourism as a "do-or-die issue," and it has policies aimed at protecting the beach, dune environments, and wildlife. Its "Tree City USA" designation credits the city's efforts to plant and preserve trees.

Bird lovers can spend time at Silver Lake, which was classified as a state bird refuge in 1933. The website Delaware Birding Trail notes it's a great place to spot redheads, canvasbacks, ruddy ducks, and mallards.

The Oceanus is an eco-friendly hotel near the ocean designed to run partially on on-site solar power and consume less water.

Learn about wildlife protection and the importance of sustainability by visiting Florida's Space Coast.

The Space Coast includes Merritt Island and more than 70 miles of coastal towns east of Orlando. Named for its role in space operations — NASA's Kennedy Space Center is near the Cape Canaveral Space Force Station — the region can emphasize for many visitors the importance of sustainability and protecting the Earth.

While the Space Coast might be known for its rocket launches — which visitors can buy tickets for on the Kennedy Space Center's website — there are plenty of environmentally conscious things to do in the area.

On the southern end of the coast, visitors can go hiking, swimming, and fishing in Sebastian Inlet State Park. And you can get around the region by bike via paved trails with scenic beach views.

The area's tourism board is considerate about protecting the vulnerable wildlife species that call the region home. It advises visitors to give these locals their space by refraining from shining lights on the beach after the sun has gone down or leaving holes dug in the sand, among other things.

In nearby Melbourne Beach, you can stay at Seashell Suites Resort, a homey property of eight suites with beach access that says it was built with eco-friendly materials.

Head to Jekyll Island, Georgia, where you can support conservation efforts while enjoying up-close encounters with wildlife.

Conservation is a pillar of Jekyll Island's mission, according to the city's website. The area has thousands of acres of maritime forest, as well as crystal-clear waters and warm weather. There are also more than 22 miles of cycling paths and trails around the island, so you don't need to drive.

Visitors can fish, kayak, and go out on a stand-up paddleboard; take educational ranger walks; bird-watch; and even stargaze.

You can also have up-close encounters with wildlife.

Visit the Georgia Sea Turtle Center to watch sea turtles being rehabilited by staff, and learn about the animals from experts. You can also go on turtle walks to see turtles nesting on the beach from May into the summer months.

Or join a Jekyll Island park ranger to learn all about the American alligator, including how to help safely conserve the species.

In Maui, Hawaii, give back to the local community and help conserve the island through its Malama program.

Visitors to Maui can give back to the local community while appreciating the beauty of the Hawaiian island through the Malama Hawaii program. (The program also operates on Oahu, Kauai, Molokai, and the Big Island.)

Through the program, you can sign up to volunteer for beach cleanups with the Pacific Whale Foundation, the Surfrider Foundation, and the Hawai'i Wildlife Fund, among other organizations. Redline Rafting provides reusable totes and trash pickers so visitors can clean up wherever they are, including on hiking trails and in parks. Volunteers can also pick up litter from a reef through Blue'Aina or help care for local animals at the Leilani Farm Sanctuary.

In terms of where to stay, the Fairmont Kea Lani says it follows several eco-friendly practices through its kuleana commitment. It says that it has cut down on single-use plastics with water-refill stations throughout the property, that it donates food waste weekly to a local pig farm, and that it recycles cooking oil into biofuel. It also recycles used soaps and toiletries through Clean the World. Guest rooms have low-flow showerheads and toilets to cut back on water consumption, the hotel's website says.

Elsewhere on the island, the Wailea Beach Resort and the Four Seasons Maui at Wailea give guests water bottles that they can refill at filtered-water stations, reducing single-use plastics.

You can volunteer for trail cleanups and go stargazing in Sun Valley, Idaho.

The Sun Valley and Ketchum area has plenty of green-friendly activities for those who love the outdoors. Visitors can go skiing in the winter and hiking and biking in the summer, all while taking in views of the surrounding Sawtooth, Boulder, and Smoky mountains.

The area has more than a dozen city parks, and the 20-plus-mile paved Wood River Trail creates a scenic connection between Wood River Valley communities. You can hike in the nearby Sawtooth National Forest or head north from Sun Valley to the mountains for swimming or kayaking at Redfish Lake. Visitors can also volunteer for trail cleanups with the Wood River Trails Coalition to give back to the local community.

Part of the Central Idaho Dark Sky Reserve, the area is known for clear skies and is a destination for stargazing.

You can find a sustainable overnight stay at the Limelight Hotel Ketchum. The mountain lodge describes itself as a LEED-certified property that uses environmentally friendly housekeeping practices and sources some of its food locally.

Rockford, Illinois, has 11,000 acres of forest reserve with lakes for kayaking and fishing.

Rockford is home to the  Forest Preserves of Winnebago County, which consists of 44 reserves totaling more than 11,000 acres with lakes, rivers, and trees. There's also the Rock River, where visitors can kayak, paddleboard, and fish.

If you're seeking volunteer opportunities in the area, Keep Northern Illinois Beautiful is often looking for people to help at the Rockford Recycle Center and to participate in other local events with the goal of reducing waste.

When you're hungry, grab a healthy snack, smoothie, or fresh-pressed juice at Ripe Life Juice Co. For dinner, head to GreenFire Restaurant Bar & Bakery, which says it doesn't use microwaves, grill tops, stoves, or deep fryers.

Stay in a renovated bottling factory and volunteer at an urban farm in Indianapolis.

Indianapolis has an abundance of green-focused activities for visitors. The city has more than 150 miles of bike and walking trails, 200 parks, and 11,000 acres of green space.

At Growing Places Indy, an urban farm that gives locals an opportunity to grow fresh food, volunteers can help weed and harvest crops. To sample locally grown foods, head to its farm stands during the summer.

For accommodations keeping sustainability in mind, stay at Bottleworks Hotel in the Bottleworks District, named for the neighborhood's former Coca-Cola plant. The boutique hotel is in the renovated bottling factory and has water-refill stations.

Des Moines, Iowa, is near a peaceful state park and an "agrihood."

About 20 minutes outside Des Moines, visitors can find an eco-friendly neighborhood called Middlebrook. It's a so-called agrihood, created with the goal of educating the community about farming and providing access to nature.

Middlebrook has a pedestrian-focused town center and numerous parks. It's just off the Great Western & Bill Riley Bike Trail, which connects the rural community to downtown Des Moines.

Visit the farm stand, open daily at Middlebrook Farm with fresh seasonal produce like tomatoes, pumpkins, and carrots, or Middlebrook Park, which has an agricultural-inspired playground. Visitors can stay a short walk from the farm at the Middlebrook Airbnb.

A few hours' drive away is Iowa's Backbone State Park. While it may be smaller than better-known state parks around the US, the park and the state forest reserve have many eco-friendly activities as well as cabins and campsites for tourists to take advantage of.

Greensburg, Kansas, rebuilt sustainably after a natural disaster.

Greensburg took a green approach to rebuilding after a tornado in 2007 damaged more than 90% of the town's structures. The community said it used disaster-recovery funds to reconstruct sustainable, LEED-certified buildings like its new city hall. Today the town is a symbol of perseverance in the face of destruction.

The 5.4.7 Arts Center (named after May 4, 2007, the day the tornado struck), a nonprofit community arts center, hosts classes, exhibits, and performances. The building is right next to another attraction, the Big Well Museum; it's advertised as the world's largest hand-dug well, at 109 feet deep and 32 feet in diameter.

Lexington, Kentucky, has great biking and even better seafood.

In 2022, Lexington completed its yearslong Town Branch Commons project, a $35.5 million investment to transform abandoned areas along the Kentucky River into paved trails for biking and walking.

A 5.5-mile loop goes through Lexington's downtown, providing convenient access to restaurants and shopping. The city also has an urban service boundary, meaning some available land around Lexington, including farmland and environmentally sensitive land, is protected.

Visitors can learn about sustainable farming practices at FoodChain, a nonprofit indoor aquaponics farm that combines fish and plant farming to raise tilapia and grow produce. The farm has volunteer sessions, community meals, and cooking demonstrations to educate visitors on sustainability in the kitchen. Next door, Smithtown Seafood serves FoodChain's daily catch.

Folsom, Louisiana, combines nature and the arts.

Little River Bluffs — a nature retreat in Folsom, about an hour outside of New Orleans — is an eco-friendly getaway surrounded by 30 acres of woods and a river.

Beyond the resort, guests can explore the village of Folsom. At a local coffee shop called Giddy Up Folsom, visitors can listen to live music or buy produce at the weekly farmers market.

Art fans can stop by Far Horizons Gallery, which hosts the Folsom Scenic Rivers Art Festival, where artists share submissions depicting Louisiana's scenic rivers to increase awareness of the fragile natural environment.

Outdoor enthusiasts can hike in Bogue Chitto State Park or take a tour of the Honey Island Swamp. About a 50-minute drive from Folsom, the swamp has almost 70,000 acres of protected wildlife including alligators, snakes, and eagles.

As the home of a gorgeous state park, Bar Harbor, Maine, takes its tourism seriously.

Bar Harbor, on Mount Desert Island, is near Acadia National Park. Visitors can volunteer with Friends of Acadia, an independent organization with a mission to preserve, protect, and educate outdoors enthusiasts.

The Island Explorer provides free transportation via propane-powered buses to trails, shops, beaches, hotels, inns, campgrounds, and more. If you traveled to Maine by car, the Acadia Gateway Center and Village Connector Trails are the best places to park to access the Island Explorer buses.

Eco-conscious tourists can stay at Terramor Outdoor Resort, a glamping retreat that has said it's dedicated to keeping a small environmental footprint. It says that it sources food locally when possible and that many employees live on-site.

Columbia, Maryland, has been living green since the '60s.

Columbia was built in the 1960s as a suburban area of Howard County prioritizing community, nature preservation, and farming.

The county has a green map showing green living spaces, farms, parks, recycling stations, and more. It uses "aqua mowers," machines designed to sweep rivers and lakes for invasive weeds and debris without harsh chemicals.

Columbia's community events and volunteering opportunities include weeding, planting trees, and picking up litter. And farm­ers mar­kets, pick-your-own farms, and roadside farm stands offer plenty of places to buy local food.

Hingham, Massachusetts, is a paradise for the outdoorsy.

Hingham has miles and miles of nature to enjoy. Wompatuck State Park is a must-visit, with more than 3,500 acres of forest, streams, and ponds; more than 250 campsites; and 40 miles of trails.

Another stop for nature exploration is World's End, a 274-acre park overlooking the coast offering hills, tree-lined trails, and footpaths with views of Hingham Harbor and the Weir River.

Visitors can experience farm life and learn about local food systems at Weir River Farm, a family-friendly property home to horses, goats, sheep, llamas, and pigs.

For farm-to-table dining, head to Scarlet Oak Tavern, a restaurant that says it sources produce from its own farm and other local growers.

Grand Rapids, Michigan, has the first LEED-certified microbrewery in the US.

In addition to providing miles of bike lanes and nature trails, Grand Rapids was one of 12 areas in the US that participated in the Zero Cities Project, which aimed to help cities improve their buildings' energy efficiency and reduce their carbon emissions.

Visitors should check out Brewery Vivant, the first LEED-certified microbrewery in the US. The brewery aims to use less water and energy while also reducing greenhouse-gas emissions. Its property is also historic: It was a lumberyard at the turn of the 20th century. The pub is in what used to be a funeral-home chapel, and the production facility was built in the 1920s as a car dealership.

After a beer, check out downtown restaurants like Grove and Terra that take advantage of their proximity to Midwestern farms to cook with fresh, sustainably sourced ingredients. Visitors can also get fresh produce from the 100-year-old Fulton Street Market and family-run farms like Bob Alt Farm and Steffens Orchard Market.

Duluth, Minnesota — described as a "climate-proof" destination — takes sustainability seriously.

The Tulane University professor Jesse Keenan has called Duluth, on the shore of Lake Superior, a "climate-proof" destination. A recent New York Times article described it as having an "ample supply of freshwater" and mild temperatures in the summer.

Duluth's government has a sustainability office that aims to develop the city sustainably.

Visitors can access over 9,000 acres of parkland, over 300 miles of trails, and Lake Superior for outdoor activities. Leave the car parked and stroll the Duluth Lakewalk, stretching about 8 miles from Bayfront Festival Park to Brighton Beach along the lake. If you'd prefer not to walk, there are electric scooters at charging stations throughout town.

For eco-friendly lodging, the Inn on Lake Superior has initiatives to reduce waste and energy use. Tourists can eat at restaurants like the Duluth Grill and At Sara's Table Chester Creek Café, which say they use locally produced ingredients and compostable takeout containers.

Coastal Mississippi's swamps, marshes, and beaches are teeming with things for nature lovers to do and see.

Coastal Mississippi is a little-known region for a green getaway.

Nature enthusiasts can visit the Pascagoula River Audubon Center, which has more than 70,000 protected acres of land demonstrating sustainable, bird-friendly landscaping.

A Save Our Shells program recycles used oyster shells from nearby restaurants and returns them to the coast to be reused by oyster larvae. Plastic Free Gulf Coast is an organization in the region dedicated to eliminating plastic waste.

In Gautier, visitors can go on a kayak eco-tour of the cypress swamps and salt marshes of the Pascagoula River to see wildlife like alligators and pelicans.

For dinner, try Vestige in Ocean Springs — it says it serves fresh, in-season ingredients from local farmers.

Platte County, Missouri, takes preservation seriously and features the state's first green winery.

Platte County's parks-and-recreation department has a rain-garden initiative to help filter runoff water to protect the quality of lakes and streams. The county's also working to preserve land along streams, rivers, lakes, and other sensitive natural areas. It says it has restored or cultivated more than 100 acres of native prairie plants in locations including Platte Ridge Park, Prairie Creek Greenway, and Green Hills of Platte Wildlife Preserve.

Visitors can stop by Jowler Creek Vineyard & Winery, billed as Missouri's first green winery. It says it uses sustainable practices to minimize soil loss and improve biodiversity. It also has a creative approach to pest control: It keeps sheep as natural lawnmowers, bee colonies to pollinate the vines, and chickens and bats to eat harmful bugs.

Big Sky, Montana, has a range of eco-friendly activities, no matter the season.

Big Sky, a small town along the Gallatin River, is part of the Greater Yellowstone Ecosystem. It's about a 45-minute drive from the west entrance to Yellowstone.

In the summer visitors can go hiking, mountain biking, horseback riding, and fly fishing, while in the winter they can ski at one of the largest resorts in the country, Big Sky Resort. The resort says that its goal is to achieve net-zero carbon emissions by 2030 and that its lift operations have been running on carbon-free energy since 2020.

Outside of the resort, the town center has a weekly farmers market and an outdoor concert series in the summer and an ice rink in the winter.

Stay at an award-winning lodge known for its sustainable practices in Nebraska City, Nebraska.

Nebraska City is the birthplace of Arbor Day, a tree-planting holiday that dates back to 1872. It's celebrated annually in April, but you can visit Arbor Day Farm — a 260-acre property with parks, a historic mansion, and the 140-room Lied Lodge — year-round.

The lodge's website says it has won an award for its eco-friendly practices, such as encouraging guests to reuse linens and towels and installing windbreaks to slow the wind and protect the farm's soil.

While you're there, book a table at Timbers. The restaurant — open for breakfast, lunch, and dinner on weekdays and brunch and dinner on weekends — serves classic American fare made with locally sourced ingredients and produce grown on the farm.

The farm also offers wine tastings and tree-climbing adventures, where you can explore 11 treehouses connected by bridges and spread out over 3 acres. Its Treetop Village also features a 50-foot slide. It says proceeds from the tickets go toward the Arbor Day Foundation's mission to plant trees around the world.

You can drive 45 minutes north to Fontenelle Forest, a preserve that spans more than 1,500 acres with 19 miles of hiking trails and a birds-of-prey rescue habitat.

Or head south to Indian Cave State Park, a 3,052-acre park, and go hiking or cycling on 22 miles of trails. The park is known for its beautiful foliage along the Missouri River in the fall.

In Nevada, stay at a sustainable resort on Lake Tahoe's stunning south shore.

For a destination that's both beautiful and sustainable, visit Lake Tahoe, the region straddling Nevada and California that's home to the largest alpine lake in North America with 72 miles of shoreline.

Stay on the south shore of the lake at Edgewood Tahoe in Stateline, Nevada. An article from Visit Lake Tahoe indicates that if you book through Kind Traveler you can add a donation to a charity to get perks like a dining credit at the hotel or a spa gift card. Edgewood recommends visitors donate to Take Care Tahoe, a local initiative supporting efforts to preserve the area's environment.

The resort says it has made efforts to be more sustainable by ditching plastic bags and disposable plastic water bottles, planting over 130 trees on the property, and offering electric-vehicle charging stations for guests. (If you don't have an electric car or don't want to pay to rent one, catch an eco-friendly ride on the TART Connect, a free bus system you can request via an app.)

Edgewood is also a host of Clean Up the Lake, which hosts litter cleanups for volunteers.

When you're not out on the lake, hike the Tahoe Meadows Interpretive Loop, an easy 1.3-mile trail known for its displays of wildflowers in the spring and summer. In the winter, it's a popular place for snowshoeing.

Go mountain biking on a scenic trail and picnic in a pollinator garden in Nashua, New Hampshire.

The city of Nashua in southern New Hampshire has a large trail system and scenic parks where you can cycle or hike.

You can spend time outdoors in places like Mine Falls Park, where a network of trails pass by the Nashua River, ponds, and wildlife such as chipmunks, beavers, and muskrats. Open year-round, the park is a good spot for snowshoeing in the winter and mountain biking in the warmer months.

Two trails at Yudicky Farm Conservation Area are ideal for mountain bikers, hikers, and runners.

If you're looking to give back while you're in the area, volunteer with the Nashua River Watershed Association to pull invasive water chestnuts from the river via canoe.

You can also visit the Nashua National Fish Hatchery to support its efforts to conserve fish, wildlife, and plants. Bring lunch and sit on a picnic bench surrounded by flowers in one of the hatchery's pollinator gardens, designed to help sustain bumblebees, hummingbirds, and butterflies.

Vacation in Long Beach Island, New Jersey, where you can volunteer for a beach cleanup and eat ice cream made with locally sourced ingredients.

Long Beach Island is home to several beaches and small towns.

The Alliance for a Living Ocean, a conservation nonprofit founded in 1987 regularly hosts volunteer beach cleanups on the 18-mile-long New Jersey barrier island.

In the north part of the island, you can visit Barnegat Light to picnic in the state park or climb 217 steps to the top of a historic lighthouse to see sweeping views of the bay and the island. Or you can head to the nearby Interpretive Center to see live images from the top.

Visitors can avoid a drive to the beach by staying at the oceanfront Drifting Sands Hotel, which is also pet-friendly.

For sustainable dining, visit Black Eyed Susans, which serves pizza, pasta, seafood, salads, and more. The restaurant says it sources ingredients from local farmers, fishers, and distributors.

After dinner or a long day at the beach, stop by The Woo Hoo, which makes fresh ice cream daily using locally sourced ingredients like blueberries and mint.

Stay in an Earthship and go skiing at a carbon-neutral ski resort in Taos, New Mexico.

Whichever time of year you visit, you'll find plenty of eco-conscious activities in Taos.

In the winter, Taos Ski Valley is a go-to spot for skiing and snowboarding. The resort says it became certified carbon neutral in 2022 and has a goal of achieving net-zero carbon emissions by 2030. It says it has also invested in more-energy-efficient chairlifts, low-flow water fixtures and waterless urinals, water-bottle refill stations, and electric-vehicle chargers.

If you're visiting in the warmer months, go hiking or mountain biking on one of the scenic trails at the Rio Grande del Norte National Monument, about a 25-minute drive from Taos.

And visit Earthship Biotecture, a property consisting of off-the-grid homes about a 20-minute drive from the city. The organization's website says the solar-powered, self-sustaining homes were built with natural and recycled materials and use rainwater-collection systems and toilets that use gray water (wastewater that hasn't made contact with toilet waste) for flushing. You can stay in one of the homes or stop by for a tour.

Visit the Adirondacks region of New York to hike the largest state park in the lower 48 states.

New York's Adirondacks region is ideal for people looking for an outdoor adventure. Here you'll find 10 destinations with lakes including Lake Placid, Saranac Lake, and Tupper Lake.

Over 2 million acres of land in the Adirondack Park are classified as forest preserve and protected from development, including remote backcountry areas and campgrounds operated by the Department of Environmental Conservation.

The Adirondack Park is also the largest state park in the contiguous US — at 6.1 million acres, it's bigger than two of the largest national parks, Death Valley and Yellowstone, combined. Visitors can explore seemingly endless trails and outdoor activities. To learn more about the region's wildlife, stop by the Wild Center, where you'll find a museum with trails, wildlife exhibits, and animals like otters and owls.

Sample specialty goods and learn how certain foods are made in Greensboro, North Carolina.

Greensboro earned a LEED silver certification from the US Green Building Council in 2020 for its sustainability efforts, including water-conservation and trash-cleanup efforts and a long-term clean-energy plan to reduce emissions.

Visitors can stay at Proximity Hotel, a green hotel with eco-driven energy and lighting systems and guest programs. The property has bikes that guests can borrow to explore Greensboro's system of more than 60 miles of hiking and biking paths.

Sample fresh produce and specialty goods at the Farmers Curb Market, which was founded in 1874 and is considered one of the oldest producers' markets in North Carolina. You can also learn more about local agritourism efforts by planning trips to farms in the area such as Homeland Creamery in nearby Julian to learn about ice-cream making and dairy farming or Bernie's Berries for a berry-picking tour.

Hike the miles of trails in Fargo, North Dakota.

Fargo may not have mountains, but the city makes up for its lack of altitude with outdoor experiences in the flat prairies and beneath open skies.

During the summer, you can explore the more than 150 parks and 105 miles of trails in the Park District and go fishing or kayaking on the Red River, which separates the city from Minnesota. In the winter, there are several trails for cross-country skiing.

Eco-conscious travelers can stay at the Jasper Hotel, which Booking.com has named a Travel Sustainable property. The hotel features modern rooms and a farm-to-table restaurant.

If you want to try Midwestern favorites at a local restaurant, have brunch or dinner at Camp Lonetree, a casual spot with an airy rooftop patio and a seasonal menu.

Ditch your car and explore Cleveland, Ohio, by foot or bike.

As a Rust Belt city, Cleveland is often seen as a manufacturing hub, but it has aimed to create a more sustainable place. It has transformed abandoned buildings into apartments and community spaces, facilitated urban farming, installed bike infrastructure, and more.

Visitors downtown can explore local restaurants and barhop on foot. Cyclists can head to the Lakefront Bikeway to access 17 miles of trails on the Lake Erie shoreline. For a full-day excursion, visit Cuyahoga Valley National Park to hike, take a scenic train ride, or volunteer to remove invasive plants or collect native seeds.

You can learn more about Cleveland's urban farming by touring the 6-acre Ohio City Farm and sampling the harvest. Or attend a tree-planting event or a water-chemistry training run by Cleveland's Watershed Volunteer Program.

Walk around the revitalized neighborhoods of Oklahoma City.

Oklahoma City's efforts to turn abandoned sites into vibrant spaces make it an ideal spot for the eco-conscious.

The Environmental Protection Agency's website describes the city's focus as "creating a new city from the inside out" by turning underused commercial and industrial properties into revitalized community spaces and tourist attractions. For example, the Bricktown neighborhood once had dilapidated warehouses but now is a hot spot for restaurants, bars, and live entertainment.

Travelers looking for outdoor activities can find a slice of nature in the downtown area at Scissortail Park, a 70-acre urban green space with gardens, a lake with pedal boats and canoes, and a lawn for picnicking.

Spend the night at The National, a bank built in 1931 that's now an upscale hotel within walking distance of plenty of food and shops downtown.

Sip on some wine at sustainable vineyards in Oregon's Willamette Valley.

Oregon has some of the most protective land-use policies in the nation. In 1971 it became the first state to introduce a bottle bill in response to a litter problem.

The Willamette Valley — which spans four regions in Oregon and includes cities such as Salem, Eugene, and Portland — was an early adopter of sustainable tourism practices and conservation efforts. The area, known as Oregon's wine country, has over 700 wineries, many of which are certified sustainable, organic, or biodynamic.

Just 15 minutes from Salem is Left Coast Estate, which has indoor and outdoor tasting rooms and a 1-mile hiking trail open to pets. The winery also hosts an annual run to benefit local white-oak restoration efforts. It says it has helped preserve more than 100 acres of land.

In Eugene, you'll find King Estate, billed as the largest biodynamic vineyard in the US. It says it has set aside 150 acres of protected land, with up to 200 species of native wildlife and oak woodlands. It also says it has installed over 4,100 solar panels at the vineyard.

Several wineries in the South Willamette Valley have partnered with the Cascades Raptor Center to set up nest boxes for rehabilitated birds. Barn owls and kestrels can find a safe place to live while helping the wineries control pest populations.

The Willamette Valley isn't just for wine drinkers, though — eco-friendly travelers will find plenty of things to do, including hiking and kayaking.

Help keep the Pocono Mountains in Pennsylvania clean by volunteering to pick up litter in between hikes through the region's state parks.

From state parks to walkable towns, the Pocono Mountains region has plenty of eco-friendly activities.

Towns like Honesdale, Hawley, Jim Thorpe, and Stroudsburg are convenient places to stay to access trails, lakes, and parks. Several communities, including Hawley, have regular farmers markets where you can buy Pennsylvania-grown vegetables and other regional goods. You can kayak or swim at Mauch Chunk Lake Park, or visit the Cherry Valley National Wildlife Refuge for ranger-led wildlife walks and the chance to spot animals like bald eagles and wild turkeys.

Travelers looking to get more involved in the region's sustainability efforts can volunteer to help clean up litter on roadways and in other public spaces as part of Pick Up the Poconos, a program created by the Pocono Mountains Visitors Bureau in 2018.

Several hotels in the Poconos are part of the Delaware Highlands Conservancy's Green Lodging program. The visitors bureau says guests who book a stay at one of the hotels contribute to the protection of clean waters, wildlife habitats, and outdoor recreation opportunities.

Stay at a sustainable inn that hosts beach cleanups in Westerly, Rhode Island.

Westerly, a quiet beach town on the border of Connecticut, is a good place to visit for hiking. Many parks are preserved by the Westerly Land Trust, a local nonprofit focused on conservation. You can use the organization's app to find hiking trails and keep a record of your progress.

Go to Watch Hill Beach to see a historic lighthouse, and explore Napatree Point, a 1.3-mile sand spit and conservation area that the National Audubon Society has designated a globally important bird area to see osprey, American oystercatchers, and other species. Sunbathe or swim at East Beach, one of Rhode Island's least developed beaches; it even has reef-safe sunscreen available for visitors.

On the banks of Quonochontaug Pond, a saltwater pond near the coast, stay at the Weekapaug Inn, a resort with green initiatives including a zero-waste kitchen. The inn says it's phasing out single-use plastics, composts food scraps in partnership with a local farm, and hosts monthly beach cleanups during the summer.

See local wildlife from a hiking trail or kayak in North Myrtle Beach, South Carolina.

North Myrtle Beach has made efforts in recent years to become more environmentally friendly. In 2010 it became the first city in the state to install an off-shore wind turbine, and its Keep North Myrtle Beach Beautiful Committee organizes litter-removal and -prevention programs.

Visitors are drawn to the area for its quiet beaches, marshland, and golf courses. You can glimpse wildlife on the Heritage Shores Nature Preserve, a peaceful 7-acre park that extends into the Cherry Grove Marsh, accessible via elevated boardwalks and observation docks. Walk along the 1-mile trail to see wildlife like whitetail deer, red foxes, and birds including blue herons, pelicans, great egrets, and more. You can also take in views of the park from a kayak.

Ingram Dunes Natural Area, a 7-acre park and maritime forest with miles of trails, is worth a visit as well. The park says it has the highest relic dune in the state, at 50 feet above sea level. It also says that in 2019 residents pushed back on plans to build over 30 houses on the site.

In South Dakota, hike through a ghost town in the Black Hills region and feed alpacas.

South Dakota may be known for its most famous monument, Mount Rushmore, but that's not all there is to see in the state's Black Hills region.

The Black Hills, covering a large area in western South Dakota and part of Wyoming, is home to the Black Hills National Forest, where you can take a scenic drive and stop at lookouts to take in the view. You can also hike through Spokane, a ghost town with crumbling structures where miners once lived.

About an hour's drive east of the forest you can take a private tour of the family-run Dry Creek Farm and Ranch, which describes itself as "holistically managed" and says it focuses on "soil health, direct market meat production and community education." Then drive five minutes to Caputa Alpacas and Guest Ranch, a family-friendly alpaca farm where visitors can get a tour and feed the sociable animals.

If you're heading east out of the forest, visit the Black Hills Farmers Market in Rapid City. Open year-round, the market has plenty of fresh produce, live music, and a pop-up library.

In Chattanooga, Tennessee, stay at a treehouse hotel and hike to an underground waterfall.

Eco-conscious visitors to Chattanooga, between the mountains of Southeast Tennessee and the Tennessee River, can stay at Treetop Hideaways at Ruby Falls, a treehouse hotel perched on Lookout Mountain. The hotel says its three treehouses were partially built with reclaimed materials and come with amenities like a smokeless firepit, s'mores supplies, and a record player.

While you're there, visit Ruby Falls, billed as the deepest and tallest underground waterfall open to the public in the US. You can book a zip-line experience and guided cave tours online.

In the city, get around by using Chattanooga's bike-share program. You can also hike Chattanooga's Riverwalk, which runs along the Tennessee River.

In Chattanooga's Southside neighborhood, dine at the Feed Co. Table & Tavern, a farm-to-table restaurant that says it serves "down home Southern favorites with an elevated twist" for lunch, dinner, and, on the weekends, brunch.

Volunteer for a beach cleanup and see wildlife up close in the Gulf Coast city of Port Aransas, Texas.

At Port Aransas, a beach city on Mustang Island that has 18 miles of shoreline along the Gulf Coast, visitors can spend plenty of time at the beach.

To protect its shoreline, the city runs a program called the Port A Way encouraging tourists to dispose of trash on the beach. If you want to help, volunteer with Flynn's Beach Squad, which hosts beach cleanups twice a week and provides reusable trash bags and grabbers.

Outside the beach, you can connect with local wildlife by visiting the Port Aransas Nature Preserve, which has several thousand acres of wetlands, dunes, and ponds. The preserve hosts outdoor activities like yoga classes, bird-watching, and guided nature walks.

You can also visit the University of Texas Marine Science Institute to see animals like seahorses and otters up close, and tour the connected Amos Rehabilitation Keep, a refuge for turtles and sea birds.

Stay at an environmentally conscious resort founded by Robert Redford in Sundance, Utah.

About an hour's drive from Salt Lake City is Sundance, in Utah's Wasatch Mountains.

Founded by the actor Robert Redford in 1969, the Sundance Mountain Resort says that it has followed sustainable practices protecting the local environment since its inception and that it has continued its water-conservation, recycling, and mountain-restoration efforts.

It says that its rooms are cleaned using nontoxic supplies and that the water at the resort is from local mountain-fed springs. The property also has a glasswork kiln used to turn old glass bottles into new dishware or art.

For a creative afternoon, book a soap-making, pottery, or jewelry-making class at the resort's art gallery and studio, or take a zip-line ride for an adrenaline rush and views of the mountains. The resort's website says that its zip-line tours have a vertical drop of over 2,100 feet and that riders can go as fast as 65 mph.

In Park City, a little under an hour's drive from Sundance, visit the Swaner Preserve and EcoCenter, a nature center on a 1,200-acre preserve that's run by Utah State University. Visitors can take guided tours of the wetlands, go geocaching, or snowshoe.

Pick up some maple syrup and support local farms in Burlington, Vermont.

Burlington is on the eastern shore of Lake Champlain and near the Green Mountain national forest, where visitors can find campgrounds, hiking and skiing trails, and lakes for fishing.

The city says it's working toward a goal of becoming a net-zero-energy city by 2030. A green mindset is reflected in the offerings at restaurants like the Farmhouse Tap and Grill and the on-site dining at the Hotel Vermont, which both say they source ingredients from nearby farms.

The region has agritourism opportunities, with farmers welcoming visitors to see their land and harvests. If you're visiting during apple-picking season, from late August to October, you can drive about 20 minutes south of Burlington to Shelburne Orchards, a family-run apple farm with a farm stand, a brandy distillery, and a tasting room.

Maple sugaring, or the process of extracting sap from sugar maple trees to be boiled down into maple syrup, typically takes place from late February to early April. In Stowe, about a 45-minute drive from Burlington, you can visit the family-owned Nebraska Knoll Sugar Farm to see its extraction and syrup-making facilities and buy syrup directly from the makers.

Help clean local beaches and learn about sea life in Virginia Beach, Virginia.

Virginia Beach, a coastal city in southeastern Virginia on the edge of the Atlantic and Chesapeake Bay, encourages locals and visitors to protect the environment. Tourists can participate in public cleanup events such as the Keep It Beachy Clean litter-prevention program, which aims to keep trash off the beaches and out of the waterways.

If you're visiting in early June, you can take part in the annual Clean the Bay Day to clear litter and debris from local beaches. The event has taken place in Virginia Beach and other cities around the Chesapeake Bay since 1989.

Outside the beach, visitors can enjoy the outdoors at Woodstock Park, a 30-acre reserve with a dog park and a skate plaza, and Mount Trashmore Park, a former landfill that's been turned into a 165-acre park with picnic areas and a lake.

There's a family-friendly adventure park with tree climbing and zip-lining at the Virginia Aquarium and Marine Science Center. While there, you can take a tour to learn about maritime wildlife and ocean conservation and see sea turtles, sharks, harbor seals, and more. The aquarium offers boat tours to spot dolphins along the coast as well.

Spend a night at a resort in Leavenworth, Washington, built with the environment in mind.

About a two-hour drive from Seattle, Leavenworth, is an ideal nature getaway thanks to its location in the Cascade Mountains. The region was inhabited by the Wenatchi, Yakama, and Chinook tribes, who hunted for deer and elk and fished for salmon. Today it's a popular destination because of its Bavarian-inspired architecture, created during a renovation in the 1960s to drive tourism to the struggling town.

Visitors can find plenty of hiking and biking trails or try their hand at fly-fishing or rock climbing. Leavenworth's Give a Schnitzel initiative encourages tourists to respect trail etiquette and follow outdoor fire and water safety guidelines.

The Sleeping Lady Mountain Resort is owned by the Icicle Fund, a local nonprofit that uses proceeds from guest stays to support environmental and community programs in the region. The resort says it was built with natural materials, features water-conserving fixtures in the bathrooms, and has EV charging stations.

Spend your nights gazing at the stars in Pocahontas County, West Virginia.

Pocahontas County describes itself as "nature's mountain playground." Nestled in the Allegheny Mountains, it has three Dark Sky Parks — Watoga State Park, Calvin Price State Forest, and Droop Mountain Battlefield State Park — where you'll find ideal conditions for stargazing. It's also home to the Green Bank Observatory, the Snowshoe Mountain Resort, the Cass Scenic Railroad, and more.

Visitors can explore an abundance of outdoor recreational opportunities. Trek into the Monongahela National Forest, which has more than 800 miles of trails across nearly 1 million acres. Or visit the Thorny Mountain Fire Tower, which overlooks the Seneca State Forest, for 360-degree nature views. You can even book an overnight stay in the tower between May and October.

Explore the Cranberry Glades Botanical Area, one of the largest areas of bogs in West Virginia, from a half-mile boardwalk so you can see the area without interfering with the environment. Once you leave the glades, drive along the Highland Scenic Highway for breathtaking views and access to plenty of hiking trails.

In the Fox Cities region of Wisconsin, take scenic bike rides and stop at farmers markets to taste local food.

The Fox Cities, a region in central Wisconsin made up of 19 communities along the Fox River, offers a mix of urban sites and outdoor activities.

In Appleton, the Bubolz Nature Preserve has over 700 acres of protected land and trails for hiking, skiing, and snowshoeing. The Fox River State Trail can make for a scenic bike ride, as it stretches for 25 miles along the water.

Many local farmers markets across the area are open during the summer and fall, including in Kaukauna and downtown Appleton, where you can sample apples, rhubarb, and corn.

In Menasha, you can visit the Heckrodt Wetland Reserve, a 91-acre preserve with trails, a pond, and a nature center featuring exhibits on local wildlife like turtles, frogs, and fish, many of which you can also spot in the park. The reserve also hosts community events like gardening and seed-cultivation workshops and runs a recycling drive.

Hike designated trails and view wildlife from a distance in Jackson Hole, Wyoming.

Jackson Hole is a valley region between the Gros Ventre and Teton mountain ranges. Though visitors are lured in by the skiing and proximity to national parks such as Yellowstone, the area is committed to environmental sustainability, making it an ideal location for the eco-conscious.

Teton County, which includes the town of Jackson, uses hydroelectricity and has extensive recycling and composting services as part of a zero-waste program.

Jackson Hole Mountain Resort says it uses wind energy sourced from a neighboring farm to power its buildings and operations like chairlifts. Visitors to the resort can ski and snowboard during the winter and bike, fish, and paraglide in the summer.

Travelers can visit the Jackson Hole Farmers Market for local and sustainable food. In Jackson, you can take a tour of the National Museum of Wildlife Art to see animal sculptures or visit the National Elk Refuge between December and April to spot herds of migrating elk.

Stay overnight at Hotel Terra, Wyoming's first LEED Silver-certified resort. The property says that it uses solar, hydro, and wind energy and that it gives guests reusable water bottles to refill.
