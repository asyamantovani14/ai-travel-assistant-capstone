Litquidity Unmasked

Inside the rise, ambitions, and secret identity of the ex-banker dominating Wall Street's rumor mill.
By Reed Alexander and Hayley Cuccinello
2024-02-07T12:36:09Z
Facebook Email X LinkedIn Copy link Impact Link

No one expected an anonymous Instagram account to send the global banking powerhouse Goldman Sachs into panic mode.

But on March 17, 2021, that's exactly what happened.

The Instagram meme account Litquidity published a slide deck produced by 13 Goldman Sachs investment-banking analysts. In the presentation, the entry-level bankers described the isolation of working during the COVID-19 pandemic, when they were busier than ever thanks to a mergers-and-acquisitions boom. "The sleep deprivation, the treatment by senior bankers, the mental and physical stress…" one wrote. "I've been through foster care, and this is arguably worse."

"My body physically hurts all the time, and mentally I'm in a really dark place," another noted.

The analysts had presented the deck to a senior Goldman Sachs executive the month prior, according to New York magazine, and a copy made its way to CEO David Solomon. The deck was never meant to be seen outside the firm, and it's still unclear how Litquidity got ahold of it. When the presentation hit the internet, The New York Times, CNBC, and Business Insider were among the outlets that leaped on it. "Goldman's junior bankers complain of crushing workload," CNBC wrote; "Goldman analysts work too hard," Bloomberg reported.

The deck kicked off a game of one-upmanship among Wall Street banks trying to keep their employees happy. Solomon promised to hire reinforcements to help with the M&A and IPO deal frenzy and to enforce a rule prohibiting junior bankers from working Saturdays. Other banks went further, offering $20,000 bonuses, free Pelotons, and all-expenses-paid weeklong vacations. In a matter of months, Goldman had bumped first-year analyst pay from $85,000 to $110,000 before bonuses, while firms like Evercore went as high as $120,000.

Watching all this unfold was "Lit," as he calls himself — the man behind Litquidity, which now has more than 800,000 followers. The Goldman deck scoop "put him on the map," said a first-year investment banker who requested anonymity so as not to violate company policy, but whose name is known to BI. "It was a seminal moment when people realized that Litquidity had become a place where you can safely, anonymously vent and expose abuse in the finance industry."

When the Litquidity Instagram account launched in 2017, it was just supposed to be a funny meme page — the go-to for GIFs about women wanting to marry "a 6'2" private equity principal with a Tribeca loft" or memes about a "starter pack" for "old money" analysts that included clothing from the Italian luxury brand Brunello Cucinelli and a membership to the Metropolitan Club of New York. Lit capitalized on poking fun at Wall Street culture, selling $35 dad hats that read, "Do You Know Who My Father Is?"

Over time, hundreds of likes snowballed into thousands, and Litquidity began to act as the financial world's answer to the celebrity-obsessed account @deuxmoi. He broke news about a pay bump at Warburg Pincus and layoffs at Nomura, and he chronicled the gripes of Citigroup employees. The account has become equally iconic among Wall Street's rank and file and the big-bank PR teams dreading a mention. "I think more people know Litquidity than Jamie Dimon by name," an investment-banking analyst said.

Except people do know Jamie Dimon's name — and very few people know Lit's. He disguised his voice on the podcast he cohosted in 2022. He covers his face with emojis on Instagram. In Zoom calls for previous BI stories, he used a Litquidity logo and asked reporters to sign nondisclosure agreements. (The reporters declined; Lit gave the interviews anyway.)

At first, he said this was because he worked at one of the banks he sometimes reported on or parodied. Running his account, Lit claimed, could get him fired. "I'd have to be discreet, hunching over making sure from the cubicle that no one could actually see what I was doing," he told the Financial Times. After he quit his banking job in 2021 to focus on his brand, anonymity was simply convenient. "If you're on Twitter, you see how things can get ugly," he told BI in a 2023 interview unrelated to this story. "People like to dox, find addresses, and it's just something I'd rather avoid." He added that he liked the "general mystery" of anonymity — like Daft Punk or Banksy or Deadmau5, it's "part of the brand itself." With the exception of Banksy, the other artists' names are public. (About 36 hours after BI sent Lit a list of facts and allegations appearing in this story, the Financial Times published an interview with Lit in which he revealed his name. Upon BI's publication, Lit emailed to note "inaccuracies" in the story but did not elaborate.)

As Lit's audience has grown, he's expanded beyond Instagram, leveraging his social-media fame into a podcast, an investment portfolio, and an email newsletter called Exec Sum. In October 2021, Bloomberg reported that Litquidity was on track to pull in about $1 million in revenue that year. One of his latest ventures is the financial-services recruiting firm Litney Partners, a collaboration with Whitney Partners and their managing director, the former "Bachelorette" contestant Bennett Jordan.

Lit has big ambitions, among them to expand his business into an empire. In a 2021 pitch deck viewed by BI, Lit described how Barstool Sports had superseded "buttoned-up boomer brands" like ESPN. Litquidity, the deck said, would do the same for Bloomberg and CNBC.

I think more people know Litquidity than Jamie Dimon by name. an investment-banking analyst

But some people who've worked with Lit say his refusal to publicly reveal his identity, which for so many years helped him succeed, is now holding him back. (BI spoke with numerous current and former associates of Lit's who asked to remain anonymous, either to maintain their professional relationships or for fear of backlash from Lit and his followers.) Lit himself has seemed to grapple with this dilemma. In an issue of Exec Sum at the end of 2022, he described how hiring another high-profile employee — Mark Moran, a 32-year-old associate at the investment bank Centerview Partners who had been a contestant on the reality show "FBoy Island" — led to a case of "forgotten founder." People saw Lit as secondary at his own company. Lit didn't seem to like it, writing that the experience gave him an "identity crisis."

"At some point, if he really wants to scale the business," said a senior investment-firm executive who met with Lit in 2022, "I don't understand how you can do that completely anonymously."

There have, of course, been endless rumors about Lit's identity, especially among Wall Street underlings. One junior investment banker said it was "well known within the industry" that Lit most recently worked as a private-equity associate at the investment firm Permira. Someone on the anonymous online forum Wall Street Oasis suggested Lit might have previously worked at the investment bank Jefferies given his purportedly close relationship with CEO Richard Handler, who regularly comments on Litquidity posts and has hosted events with Lit. Another post suggested Lit was laid off from a sales role at a bank.

Lit has made himself known in certain circles. He told New York magazine in 2021 that his family and many of his former colleagues knew about his secret identity. He described having a few drinks and revealing it to friends, watching them turn "starstruck." And he admitted to using the name "Lit" to get into Montauk's Surf Lodge.

Isaac Laifer, a 24-year-old entrepreneur and Litquidity fan, saw Lit speak at a closed-door event hosted by the private-equity firm Bain Capital in San Francisco last June. To attend, guests had to agree not to use their cellphones or take photos. Laifer recalled Lit positioning himself as "the voice for the people" — someone who said "what everyone else was feeling, but through the protection of being anonymous."

Business Insider has been in touch with Lit on and off since 2020, when he hosted a virtual lunch with Jefferies' Handler and agreed to be interviewed about the event. He kept his camera off for video calls and declined to give his name or any identifying information. But, when reporting for this story began, it didn't take long to figure out who he was. Several of Lit's former associates volunteered a name, which half a dozen people who know or directly worked with him confirmed. The name could also be cross-checked with documents and searched on FINRA, the regulatory agency that keeps records of brokers' employment histories. The available information corresponded to the details Lit had revealed about his career. The name also appears on a public form that Litquidity Venture Partners LLC filed with the SEC in early November of last year.

We'd found our man.

Litquidity is Henry "Hank" Medina, a former investment banker.

So why out someone who might prefer to remain anonymous? For starters, Medina is a public figure. He's also no longer at risk of being fired for running Litquidity — a name he's used to build a business with multiple revenue streams. Then there's the matter of his Wall Street bona fides, which he's used to bolster that business, but which most people can't confirm, even if they're paying him $1,000 for a 60-minute phone call about how to break into the financial industry. The same goes for his potential conflicts of interest: his friends, his former employers, those he might want to protect or prop up. All of this is hard or impossible to determine without a name.

Medina has, in fact, worked on Wall Street. A nondescript 32-year-old with dark hair and dark eyes, he graduated from Cornell University in 2013 and joined Jefferies as an analyst the following year. He left for the investment firm Wexford Capital — where he appears to have started his Instagram account — before landing at Deutsche Bank in 2019, according to FINRA records.

Medina worked at Deutsche as an associate for about a year before leaving to run Litquidity full time. Along with his Instagram and X accounts, Medina also runs Exec Sum and other finance meme accounts including Bart P. Fuchs IV; LinkedIn Flex; and Karl Smith, CFA. He splits his time between New York City and Miami.

Litquidity's online persona is loud, rude, and crass — the kind of guy you might see at Phebe's Tavern in NoHo or Mr. Purple on the Lower East Side. It would be easy, he has implied, to make certain assumptions about the man behind the account. "Over the years it has been fun for a lot of people to assume I'm another, like, Chad, who grew up in fucking Connecticut and dad's a hedge-fund manager, which is not the case," he said in an episode of his podcast last year, adding that he "can speak from the perspective of minority groups." (Medina is Hispanic.)

People who have met Medina say he doesn't necessarily match what he projects online — in calls with BI reporters, Lit came off as soft-spoken and unpretentious. "I was almost shocked by his demeanor," said one media company founder, describing Medina as "humble" and "quiet."

"His online personality is polarizing and not one I'm always aligned with," the founder said, "but I think it's a bit more of a persona."

Growing a business is never easy, but being an anonymous founder can add a whole new set of obstacles. In late 2021, Medina decided Litquidity needed a public face. Mark Moran seemed like the ideal front man: tall, strawberry blond, with a chiseled jawline and high-octane personality. Moran joined the brand that October.

Medina had high hopes for the partnership. While Moran told Bloomberg he'd taken a pay cut to join Litquidity, an internal spreadsheet viewed by BI predicted he could earn nearly $7.6 million at the brand over five years.

Moran and Medina wanted to turn Litquidity into a media behemoth, complete with its own TV show and podcast. At the time, Medina was thinking of joining forces with Bullish Studio, an ad agency and content studio run by Brian Hanly, a media executive in New York who previously worked at Vice News.

Medina and Hanly discussed bringing in Bloomberg's star Wall Street correspondent, Sonali Basak, marrying her vast Rolodex with Litquidity's reach and Bullish's content-development capabilities. In the fall of 2021, a group including Basak, Medina, and Hanly met twice at Manhattan's Rue 57 restaurant to discuss the venture. Basak, one attendee said, wanted to take a "wrecking ball through it all" and hold Wall Street heavy hitters accountable. But Medina worried her approach would alienate his industry friends.

At one of the dinners, talk turned to who would run the venture, and Hanly volunteered to step in as CEO. Two attendees said Medina seemed upset at this and withdrew from the conversation. She ultimately decided not to go forward with the partnership. (Basak declined a request for comment.)

Several more of Medina and Moran's proposals died on the vine. There was the concept for a TV show, "Litquidity x Bloomberg," which was meant to tap Litquidity's audience of young Wall Street insiders, according to a pitch deck viewed by BI. The deck also offered Bloomberg access to Litquidity's pipeline of industry leaks: "Litquidity is willing, under the right terms, to exclusively break news (including M&A deals) to Bloomberg, cutting out CNBC, Fox Business, etc." The show ultimately went nowhere.

Then there was Litquidity's podcast, "Big Swinging Decks," where Moran and Medina (with his voice disguised) dished about everything from dildos and mental health to affirmative action in finance. To fund it, they reached a deal in February 2022 with the cryptocurrency exchange CoinFLEX for $1.5 million: one-third in cash, one-third in CoinFLEX equity, and one-third in CoinFLEX's proprietary digital token.

For the most part, the show was a flop; new episodes averaged about 5,000 listeners, said a former employee at the company that produced the podcast. The employee asked to remain anonymous because they weren't authorized to speak on company matters. "I think the people listening were the people who wanted the same crass humor and to feel validated for being a straight man on Wall Street — a super arduous thing to do," the person joked.

When cryptocurrency crashed a few months later, the CoinFLEX funding dried up, and Medina and Moran's relationship with the production company collapsed, the former employee said. "We said, when you figure out how to finance this, we'll be ready to get going again. And they never got back to us."

As the disappointments piled up, Medina and Moran's relationship started to sour. Moran told people that Medina was unprepared, "boring," and "holding him back," the former production-company employee said. "You could see that tension rising — and you could see that they were both using the other for the thing that they didn't have," the person added. "Mark wanted an audience and he was the personality. Hank wanted to be something greater than who he is."

Even as they attempted to grow their digital footprint, Medina and Moran were looking for people to invest in Litquidity. According to the same internal spreadsheet that projected Moran could make about $7.6 million, Medina estimated that Litquidity was worth $20 million in the fall of 2021 — and as much as $200 million at an unspecified future date. (It's not clear how Medina arrived at these numbers.)

A colleague of Medina's tried to tell him his expectations were unrealistic for a media company but said Medina wouldn't listen. He insisted Litquidity was as valuable as any tech startup. "It's all about an ego thing," the colleague said.

Potential investors weren't convinced. One, then a senior executive at an investment firm, said he took a meeting with Moran and Medina in early 2022, despite being worried about the tone of Litquidity's content and the reputational risk it might pose to the firm. He was also skeptical that Litquidity could launch new products, noting that the seven-figure podcast deal with CoinFLEX made up the majority of the company's income.

Perhaps most important, the senior executive was concerned about working with the founder of a newer, smaller company who insisted on remaining anonymous to his audience. Medina and Moran's presentation repeatedly compared Litquidity to Barstool, but as the senior executive noted, Litquidity doesn't have the same prominence as Barstool's controversial founder, Dave Portnoy.

Medina introduced himself with a first name, which the senior executive assumed was fake, but it was Moran who "controlled the room."

"Ultimately, if I'm investing at that stage of the company, I'm buying into the founder," the senior executive said. "And the founder wasn't present."

At the same time, Medina wanted to prove he was a Wall Street savant by becoming an investor in his own right. He founded Litquidity Ventures, the investment arm of Litquidity Capital, in 2021. Since then he's made more than 30 investments, according to PitchBook, including in Linktree, a link-in-bio startup also backed by Coatue Management, and Amberjack, a men's dress-shoe brand Medina has promoted in his newsletters.

One founder, who received funding from Litquidity Ventures in 2021 without knowing Medina's identity, said Medina promoted their company on his platforms and helped brainstorm about how to get his followers interested.

I'm buying into the founder. And the founder wasn't present. a senior investment-firm executive who met with Medina

Ayden Syal, the CEO and founder of the sports-focused influencer-marketing startup Mogl, said he received a "six-figure" investment from Litquidity Ventures last year and hoped to get the company involved in Mogl's latest fundraising round. He first met Medina, who called himself "Lit," in a Zoom meeting where Medina kept his camera off. They communicate almost exclusively via email and have never met in person.

Others have had more contentious experiences with Medina. One startup founder, Kyle Zappitell, said Medina did four sponsored Instagram posts for his NFT marketplace app, Neon, in November 2021. Medina then expressed interest in investing $10,000 in the company. Neon's minimum investment was $25,000, but they made an exception in the hopes that having Litquidity involved would help with publicity, Zappitell said.

A month later, Medina emailed that he'd received the SAFE agreement (a contract giving an investor the right to future equity) and would wire the $10,000 the next day. But the money never came, and in late January Medina backed out, saying he'd "exhausted" his funds. Zappitell said he'd never experienced an investor changing their mind after signing a SAFE.

Last March, Zappitell aired his grievances, tweeting that Litquidity Capital was "still a meme" rather than a serious enterprise. He posted a screenshot of Medina's email promising to wire money that never materialized. "Dude this is pretty fucking pathetic from you, ngl," Medina shot back. "I wish you the best."

In August 2022, after less than a year at Litquidity, Moran announced in Bloomberg that he was leaving to launch his own investor-relations consultancy. "Mark and I ultimately had differences in interest and vision, and decided to amicably pursue different paths," Medina wrote in Exec Sum.

But things were more contentious than Medina had let on. Later that month, he accused Moran in an email of stealing his connections and breaking his trust. In another email, Medina wrote: "If I continue to be left out of this and continue to lose out on business because these relationships were siphoned, I'll have to look at all options." Moran wrote back that day to "fully deny" Medina's allegations.

In a 2022 year-end letter to his newsletter subscribers that was also posted to his website, Medina looked back on the collapse of his business relationship with Moran. "In hindsight, a lot should have been done differently," he wrote, adding that navigating the friction with Moran while running his company was "a logistical nightmare" that "took a big toll on my mental health."

Moran's supposed betrayal hasn't stopped Medina from moving on to the next thing — and the next, and the next. He introduced a smattering of new ventures in 2023, including paid consulting calls ($1,000 for 60 minutes) and a prospective sports club for the finance community "where the social element of Soho House meets the fitness of an Equinox," he told the New York Post. "For me (personally), 2023 was a year filled with big life changes, profound self-growth, and the planting of seeds for the future," Medina wrote in Exec Sum.

It was also the year he launched Litney Partners with Bennett Jordan — a partner who, like Moran, embodies Litquidity's alpha-male brand with his workout videos and rugged 5 o'clock shadow.

Litney Partners' goal is to become the go-to junior recruiting organization for major banks and asset managers. While the company hasn't shared much information about its clients, it has posted about job searches on their behalf. In January, Litney Partners' Instagram page put out a call for mid-level professionals to work for a long-only public equity fund focused on the software, internet, and tech sector. But a senior Wall Street executive said she wouldn't trust the firm's judgment when it came to selecting job candidates, both because of Lit's anonymity and because she's inherently skeptical of the kinds of people who are Litquidity fans. "For that reason I would question why someone would send their résumé through a platform like Litney Partners — which is run by an Instagrammer — versus using one of the many respected recruitment agencies that exist today," she said.

Whether or not Medina becomes the major player he seems to want to be, he's already transformed Wall Street culture, where loyalty is sacrosanct and employees are forbidden to speak publicly about what goes on at their firms. Litquidity has encouraged the younger generation to be bolder. That shift would have been unimaginable just a decade ago, when "you'd be beaten down and squashed like the bug you are" for leaking information or speaking out, said Paul Argenti, a professor of corporate communications at Dartmouth College and a former Goldman Sachs advisor.

That's the contradiction inherent in Medina: He's set himself up to be an advocate for Wall Street's low-level employees, even as he tries to replicate the success of their bosses. If he plays his cards right, one day he may have his own army of disgruntled underlings. At least now they'll know his real name.

Correction: February 8, 2024 — An earlier version of this story misstated the name of the university where Paul Argenti is a professor. It's Dartmouth College, not Dartmouth University.
